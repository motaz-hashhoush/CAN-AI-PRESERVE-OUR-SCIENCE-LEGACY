The moraines span the late Wisconsin\n(11-25 ka)\nto the Illinoian\n(130-190\nka) glaciations.\nWe are using the Mono Basin area as a\n"calibration\nsite" to establish remote dating techniques for eventual transfer to the more\ninaccessible\nbut geomorphically\nand climatically\nsimilar moraines of the South American\nAndes Mountains.\nPlanned polarimetric\nSAR imagery acquired by JPL AIRSAR (South\nAmerican\nCampaign)\nand SIR-C (Andes super-site)\nwill be analyzed\nto establish\nchronologies\nof previously\nundated moraine sequences in a study of Pleistocene\nclimatic\nchange in the Southern Hemisphere.\nThe dry climate and sparse vegetation is also favorable for correlation of ground\nsurface roughness with radar polarization signature.\nThe slow weathering processes acting\nover thousands\nof years reduce\nthe size, frequency\nand angularity\nof surface boulders\nwhile increasing\nsoil development\non the moraines.\nField observations\nbased on this\nhypothesis\n(Sharp and Birman 1963) result in relative ages consistent with those inferred\nfrom nested position within the valley. Younger moraines, therefore, will appear rougher\nthan the older smoother\nmoraines\nat scales measurable\nat AIRSAR\nwavelengths.\nPreviously\ndocumented\neffects of ground surface roughness\non polarization\nsignatures\n(Evans et al. 1988, Ray et al. 1991) suggest\nthat analysis\nof moraine polarization\nsignatures\ncan be useful for relative dating.\nThe technique may be extended\nto predict\nnumerical ages.\nThe data set reported here were acquired on September\n8, 1989 with the JPL\nAIRSAR collecting polarimetric\nimagery at C- (5.6 cm), L-(24 cm) and P-band(68\ncm)\nwith a flight-line parallel to the strike of the mountains.\nPhase calibration was performed\non the analyzed scene by setting the co-phase of a smooth lake to zero as described in\nZebker and Lou (1990). that the polarization\nsignatures from the glacial\nmoraines of the Walker Valley may be used as a relative dating tool. Specifically,\nthe\nparameters\nof pedestal\nheight and the ratio of circular expected\nto circular\ndiffuse\npolarization\nhave shown correlation\nwith moraine age at P-band and to a lesser degree at\nL-band.\nThe established\ncorrelation\nof relative moraine age with crest ground surface\nroughness\nprovides\nthe physical\nlink from scattering\nmechanism\nto relative age. The\nviolation of the trends at L-band for the Mono Basin moraine may be the result of diffuse\nscattering\nfrom vegetation\ncreating the "appearance"\nof a rougher surface.\nThe Mono\nBasin moraines have the most densely vegetated crests. The ground shrubs may have less\nof a response at the longer P-band wavelength.\nThis hypothesis may be further supported\nif the Mono Basin moraine appears even rougher at C-band.\nAdditional signatures need to be acquired at more locations representing\na diverse\nset of incidence angles, local incidence angles, pixel numbers used for signature, and crest\norientations\nto confirm the observed trends. The shape of the polarization\nsignatures will\nbe further analyzed for implications\nof the nature of the scattering mechanism.\nA more\ndetailed and comprehensive\ncharacterization\nof the moraine surface roughness is planned\nthrough a high resolution DEM to be generated from low altitude stereo photogrammetry.\nThe potential exists for relative dating of moraines in inaccessible\nareas from remote\npolarimertic\nSAR platforms.\nREFERENCES\nBurke, R.
