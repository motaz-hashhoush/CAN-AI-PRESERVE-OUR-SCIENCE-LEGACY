b'Extended Abstract for 32nd AIAA Applied Aerodynamics Conference, 16-20 June 2014\n\nAeroelastic Modeling of a Nozzle Startup Transient\nTen-See Wang*\nNASA Marshall Space Flight Center, Huntsville, Alabama, 35812\nXiang Zhao\xe2\x80\xa0,\nAlabama A&M University, Huntsville, Alabama, 35762\nSijun Zhang\xe2\x80\xa1\nESI CFD, Inc., Huntsville, Alabama, 35806\nand\nYen-Sen Chen\xc2\xa7\nApplied Research Laboratory, Hsinchu 30078, Taiwan\n\nLateral nozzle forces are known to cause severe structural damage to any new rocket\nengine in development during test. While three-dimensional, transient, turbulent, chemically\nreacting computational fluid dynamics methodology has been demonstrated to capture\nmajor side load physics with rigid nozzles, hot-fire tests often show nozzle structure\ndeformation during major side load events, leading to structural damages if structural\nstrengthening measures were not taken. The modeling picture is incomplete without the\ncapability to address the two-way responses between the structure and fluid. The objective\nof this study is to develop a tightly coupled aeroelastic modeling algorithm by implementing\nthe necessary structural dynamics component into an anchored computational fluid\ndynamics methodology. The computational fluid dynamics component is based on an\nunstructured-grid, pressure-based computational fluid dynamics formulation, while the\ncomputational structural dynamics component is developed under the framework of modal\nanalysis. Transient aeroelastic nozzle startup analyses at sea level were performed, and the\ncomputed transient nozzle fluid-structure interaction physics presented.\n\n*\n\nAerospace Engineer, ER42, Fluid Dynamics Branch, Propulsion Structure, Thermal, and Fluids Analysis Division,\nSenior Member AIAA.\n\xe2\x80\xa0\nAssociate Professor, Member AIAA.\n\xe2\x80\xa1\nTechnical Fellow, Associate Fellow AIAA.\n\xc2\xa7\nSenior Research Fellow, Associate Fellow AIAA.\n1\n\nNomenclature\nC1,C2,C3,C\xef\x81\xad= turbulence modeling constants, 1.15, 1.9, 0.25, and 0.09.\nC\n\n= damping\n\nCp\n\n= heat capacity\n\nD\n\n= diffusivity\n\nFyz, Fy, Fz = integrated force, and component forces in the lateral direction\nf\n\n= frequency\n\nH\n\n= total enthalpy\n\nK\n\n= thermal conductivity or stiffness\n\nk\n\n= turbulent kinetic energy\n\nM\n\n= mass\n\nQ\n\n= heat flux\n\nr\n\n= Eigen function\n\nT\n\n= temperature\n\nt\n\n= time, s\n\nu\n\n= mean velocities\n\nV2\n\n= \xef\x83\xa5 u2\n\nx\n\n= Cartesian coordinates or nondimensional distance\n\nY\n\n= physical displacement\n\nZ\n\n= generalized displacement\n\n\xef\x81\xa1\n\n= species mass fraction\n\n\xef\x81\xa5\n\n= turbulent kinetic energy dissipation rate\n\n\xce\xbc\n\n= viscosity\n\n\xce\xbct\n\n= turbulent eddy viscosity (=\xef\x81\xb2C\xef\x81\xadk2/\xef\x81\xa5)\n\n\xce\xbe\n\n= damping parameter\n\n\xce\xa0\n\n= turbulent kinetic energy production\n\n\xcf\x81\n\n= density\n\n\xef\x81\xb3\n\n= turbulence modeling constants, 0.9, 0.9, 0.89, and 1.15 for Eqs. Transient nozzle side load is therefore considered a high risk item and a critical design issue. For that reason,\nmany research efforts [5-26] have been devoted to understanding the side load physics and their impact on the\nmagnitude of side loads. For regeneratively-cooled engines such as the Space Shuttle Main Engine (SSME), the\npeak side load generating physics have been identified as the \xce\xbb shock oscillations across the nozzle lip [7]. For filmcooled engines such as the Japanese LE-7A engine and the U.S. J-2X engine under development, the major side load\ngenerating physics have been associated with the jump of the separation line [3, 8]. Other side load physics such as\nthe Free-Shock Separation (FSS)-to-Restricted-Shock Separation (RSS) transition have been mentioned as the\ncritical physics for the European Vulcain engine [10].\nIn the aforementioned research efforts, computational fluid dynamics (CFD) have been demonstrated as a\npowerful analysis and design tool in computing and understanding the underlying transient side load physics. And\nmost of the CFD efforts, have been focused on computing the side load physics with rigid nozzles and not flexible,\nor aeroelastic nozzles. However, during actual hot-firing of a rocket engine, the nozzle wall or the structure of the\nnozzle, often flexes or deforms in response to the lateral aerodynamic forces. The deformation of the nozzle wall\nsimultaneously modifies the aerodynamic flowfield and the lateral forces, which in turn affects the nozzle wall\ndeformation. This aeroelastic movement of the nozzle wall, which was not considered in the rigid nozzle modeling,\nis indeed one of the important side load physics and needs to be considered.\n3\n\nIn early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, Pekkari [21] proposed a simplified model to study the aeroelastic stability of the first bending\nmode of the Vulcain engine. The model consists of an equation of motion describing the displacement of the nozzle\nwall. For calculation of the aerodynamic load on the wall, a simplified pressure distribution, based on a linearized\nsupersonic flow theory, was used before the separation point. After the separation point, ambient pressure was used.\nThe separation point was established by assuming ratios of separation pressure to ambient pressure as parametric\nstudies. The aerodynamic load calculation of the model was later improved by \xc3\x96stlund [10], where the pressure\nforce applied to the nozzle wall, before the separation point, was extracted from three-dimensional Euler\nsimulations, while the separation point was estimated through an empirical criterion. These two pioneering\nresearches introduced the concept of aeroelastic modeling into nozzle side load studies. In addition, \xc3\x96stlund [10]\nshowed schematically the two asymmetric modes and six buckling modes, which were first visualized in nozzle tests\nof Tuovila and Land [22].\nTo follow up on the aeroelastic analysis of Pekkari and \xc3\x96stlund, it appears that two improvements are in order.\nThat is, to refine the simplified wall pressure distribution or the Euler solution on the fluid side by the more accurate\nfull CFD solutions, and to go from addressing the first bending mode on the structure side with more detailed\ncomputational structural dynamics (CSD) formulations that could cover more major deformation modes. These\nimprovements were not realized until 2008, when Zhang, et al [25] embarked on probably the first coupled\nCFD/CSD methodology for aeroelastic nozzle study, by coupling among a full Navier-Stokes solver CFDFASTRAN, a CSD code FEM-STRESS, and an interface code MDICE, on a quasi-steady, two-dimensional nozzle.\nIn 2013, the same methodology was further demonstrated to show aeroelastic deformation of nozzle wall by Zhao et\nal. [20], on a quasi-steady, three-dimensional analysis of a J-2S nozzle for 0.1818 s of elapsed time. A short while\nearlier in 2012, Blades, et al. [26] developed a similar methodology to that of Zhao, et al. [20] and Zhang, et al. [25],\nby coupling a full Navier-Stokes solver CHEM, a CSD code Abaquas, and an interface code CSE, to demonstrate\nthe aeroelastic deformation of a SSME Block II nozzle transient startup for 0.021 s, started at 0.79 s into the startup\ntransient. These developments represent advances over earlier simple aeroelastic nozzle analyses, however, it is\nnoted that these analyses ignored chemically reacting flows which plays an important role in all transient nozzle\nphysics for engine hot-firing at sea level. In addition, none of these analyses computed at times or chamber pressures\nat or close to the occurrence of any major side load events. For example, transient times in the analysis of Blades, et\n\n4\n\nal. ranged from 0.79 s to 0.811 s, which is far from the occurrence of the first major transient nozzle physics for\nSSME startup transient \xe2\x80\x93 the FSS-to-RSS transition.\nEngineers at Marshall Space Flight Center and its contractors have been developing a pressure-based, turbulent,\nchemically reacting flow CFD methodology for computing the rigid-body transient nozzle flows. For example, a\ntransient SSME nozzle flow separation computation invoking system modeling for inlet flow history was first\nreported in 1992 [24]. The analyses that captured and anchored major side load physics and its peak magnitudes for\nregenerative cooled SSME nozzle [7] and film cooled J-2X nozzle [8] were reported in 2009. That anchored CFD\nmethodology has been used to support the design and analysis of the current J-2X engine development and selected\nefforts were published in literatures [8, 9, 19, 23]. With the recent advances in aeroelastic modeling of nozzle side\nloads [20, 25, 26], and the recognition of the importance of fluid-structure interaction on transient nozzle physics,\nthere is a need to improve our design and analysis methodology by considering the aeroelastic modeling. While\nexamining the recent aeroelastic analyses [20, 25, 26], it occurred to us that the CFD and CSD computations were\ncarried out in different codes and those codes were only connected through an interface code, thus the analyses can\nonly be categorized as loosely coupled aeroelastic solutions [20]. In 2013, Wang, et al. [38] implemented the\nnecessary CSD formulations directly into the anchored CFD code, and demonstrated the tightly coupled fluidstructure interaction algorithms on the transient startup of the SSME Block I engine at sea level, from 2.8 s to 2.876\ns \xe2\x80\x93 inside the region of \xce\xbb shock oscillations across the nozzle lip. In this effort, the computations are carried out from\nthe command start to the time of full flow in the nozzle, in order to capture all the major physics during the transient\nnozzle startup.\n\nII.\n\nComputational Methodology\n\nA. predictor and corrector solution algorithm was employed to provide coupling of the governing equations. A\nsecond-order central-difference scheme was employed to discretize the diffusion fluxes and source terms. For the\nconvective terms, a second-order upwind total variation diminishing difference scheme was used. To enhance the\ntemporal accuracy, a second-order backward difference scheme was employed to discretize the temporal terms.\nPoint-implicit method was used to solve the chemical species source terms. Sub-iterations within a time step were\nused for driving the system of second-order time-accurate equations to convergence. Details of the numerical\nalgorithm can be found in Ref\xe2\x80\x99s [27-30].\nAn extended k-\xef\x81\xa5 turbulence model [31] was used to describe the turbulence. A modified wall function approach\nwas employed to provide wall boundary layer solutions that are less sensitive to the near-wall grid spacing.\nConsequently, the model has combined the advantages of both the integrated-to-the-wall approach and the\nconventional law-of-the-wall approach by incorporating a complete velocity profile and a universal temperature\nprofile [32]. A seven-species, nine-reaction detailed mechanism [32] was used to describe the finite-rate,\nhydrogen/oxygen afterburning combustion kinetics. The seven species are H2, O2, H2O, O, H, OH, and N2. The\nthermodynamic properties of the individual species are functions of temperature. Computational Mesh Dynamics\n\nThe geometric and inertial effects of the motions of deforming structures are fed into the flowfield\nthrough the varying fluid-dynamic boundary conditions at the surface of structures, which account for the\nlocations and the velocities of these surfaces. In addition, in order to maintain boundary and grid\nconformity at the fluid-structure interface, the flowfield grid must be deformed to reflect the motion of the\nfluid-structure interfaces. The new coordinates of the fluid mesh nodes are computed by a computational\nmesh dynamics procedure. In the present work, the flowfield grid is deformed at every fluid-structure data\nexchange, which is carried out once at the end of every global time-step, continuously accommodating the\ndeformed shape of the structures in the aeroelastic model. The deformation of the fluid-dynamic grid in\nthis work is accomplished by using a spring analog approach, which is applicable for any type of meshes.\nThe assumption with the spring analog approach is that the mesh nodes are connected like a network\nof springs.
