SATELLITE SCHEDULED FOR LAUNCH\n\nIntelsat V-F, the sixth of a new series of nine international telecommunications satellites owned and operated by the\n105-nation International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat), is scheduled to be launched by the NASA Kennedy\nSpace Center on board an Atlas Centaur launch vehicle no earlier\nthan May 19, 1983, from Cape Canaveral, Fla.\nThe five earlier Intelsat Vs were successfully launched by\nNASA in December 1980, May 1981, December 1981, March 1982 and\nSeptember 1982.\nIntelsat V-F weighs 1,996 kilograms (4,400 pounds) at launch\nand has almost double the communications capability of early\nsatellites in the Intelsat series \xe2\x80\x94\n\n12,000 voice circuits and\n\ntwo color television channels.\nMay 13, 1983\n\\\n\n-more-\n\n-2-\n\nThis will also be the first Intelsat to incorporate a maritime communication system for ship to shore communications.\n\nIt\n\nwill be positioned in geosynchronous orbit over the Atlantic\nOcean as a major path Intelsat satellite to provide communications services between Europe and North America.\nIntelsat V satellites are built by the Ford Aerospace and\nCommunications Corp., Palo Alto, Calif., using system components\ndeveloped by firms in the United Kingdom, France, the Federal\nRepublic of Germany, Italy and Japan.\nThe International Telecommunications Satellite Organization\nis headquartered in Washington, D.C.\n\nNASA is reimbursed for all\n\ncosts of the Atlas Centaur and launch services under the provisions of a launch services agreement.\nThe Atlas Centaur (AC-61) will place the Intelsat V-F into a\nhighly elliptical transfer orbit 166.8 kilometers perigee altitude by 35,807.4 km apogee altitude (103.6 by 22,253.5 miles).\nIt is from this orbit at apogee that a solid propellant rocket\nmotor attached to the satellite will be fired to circularize the\norbit at geosynchronous altitude over the equator.\n\nAt that\n\naltitude, because the speed of the satellite in orbit matches the\nrotational speed of the earth, the satellite remains in position\nover one spot.\n\n-more-\n\n-3-\n\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center, Cleveland, Ohio, has management responsibility for Atlas Centaur development and operation.\nIntelsat V-F marks the 100th launch by engineers of Lewis Research Center. NASA\'s Kennedy Space Center, Fla., is assigned\nvehicle checkout and launch responsibility once the Atlas Centaur\nreaches Cape Canaveral.\nOverall direction of the NASA expendable launch vehicle program is vested in the Office of Space Flight in Washington, D.C.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE; BACKGROUND INFORMATION FOLLOWS.)\n\n-more-\n\n-4-\n\nATLAS CENTAOR LAUNCH VEHICLE STATISTICS\nIntelsat V-F will be launched by the Atlas Centaur, NASA\'s\nstandard launch vehicle for intermediate weight payloads. The\nlaunch vehicle has the following general characteristics:\nHeight:\n\n40.8 meters (134 feet) including nose fairing\n\nDiameter:\n\n3.05 m (10 ft.)\n\nTotal Liftoff Weight:\nLiftoff Thrust:\n\n148,285 kg (326,907 Ib.)\nincluding spacecraft\n\n1,936,196.6 newtons (435,296 Ib.) sea level\n\nAtlas Stage\nThe Atlas stage consists of the booster section (one-half\nstage) and the sustainer/vernier section (first stage). The Atlas\nis manufactured by General Dynamics/Convair, San Diego, Calif.,\nusing the MA-5 engine system supplied by Rocketdyne Division of\nRockwell international, Canoga Park, Calif. The MA-5 system consists of two booster engines, one sustainer engine and two vernier engines. The Atlas stage has the following characteristics:\nHeight:\n\n22 m (69.5 ft.)\n\nDiameter:\n\n3.05 m (10 ft.)\n\nPropellants:\nThrust:\n\nRP-2 kerosene for fuel and liquid oxygen\n(LOX) as the oxidizer\n\nTotal Booster: 1,679,120 N (377,500 Ib.) sea level\nSustainer: 266,880 N (60,000 Ib.)\nTotal Vernier: 4,074.4 N (916 Ib.)\n\nTotal Liftoff Thrust:\n\n1,950,074.3 N (433,416 Ib.)\n\nCentaur Stage\nThe Centaur (second stage) is manufactured by General\nDynamics/Convair, using the RL-10 engines built by Pratt and\nWhitney Aircraft Group, West Palm Beach, Fla. This stage has\nthe following characteristics:\nHeight:\nDiameter:\n\n9.1 m (30 ft.)\n3.05 m (10 ft.)\n\nPropellants:\nThrust:\n\nLiquid hydrogen for fuel and liquid oxygen\nfor the oxidizer.\n\n146,784 N (33,000 Ib.) vacuum\n\n-more-\n\n-5-\n\nIADNCH OPERATIONS\nIntelsat V is scheduled to be launched aboard Atlas Centaur\n61 from Pad A of NASA\'s Launch Complex 36, Cape Canaveral Air\nForce Station, Fla.\nThe Atlas and Centaur stages of the launch vehicle arrived\nat the Cape on Nov. 10, 1982. The Atlas stage was erected on the\npad on Nov. 12, the interstage adapter on Nov. 13, and the\nCentaur stage was mated to the vehicle on Nov. 16. The flight\nevents demonstration test, a comprehensive electrical test of the\nlaunch vehicle, was undertaken on March 14.\nIntelsat arrived at Hangar AO at the Cape on Dec. 8 where\nthe spacecraft\'s electrical systems were tested and the vehicle\'s\nthree earth sensors and two solar wings were installed. The\nsatellite was moved to Satellite Assembly and Encapsulation\nBuilding No. 2 in the KSC Industrial Area on April 28. Intelsat\nunderwent additional checkout in SAEF-2 and the apogee kick motor\nwas installed on May 2.
