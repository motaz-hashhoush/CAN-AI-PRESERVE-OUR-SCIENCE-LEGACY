A global, long-term data set of these variables is needed to verify whether satellite\nretrievals of normalized water-leaving radiance are within acceptable error limits and, eventually, to adjust\natmospheric correction schemes.\nTo achieve this objective, volunteer officers, technicians, and scientists onboard the selected ships\ncollect data from portable SIMBAD and Advanced SIMBAD (SIMBADA) radiometers. These instruments\nare specifically designed for evaluation of satellite-derived ocean color. They measure radiance in spectral\nbands typical of ocean-color sensors. The SIMBAD version measures in 5 spectral bands centered at 443,\n490, 560, 670, and 870 nm, and the Advanced SIMBAD version in 11 spectral bands centered at 350, 380,\n412, 443, 490, 510, 565, 620, 670, 750, and 870 nm. Aerosol optical thickness is obtained by viewing the\nsun disk like a classic sun photometer. Normalized water-leaving radiance, or marine reflectance, is\nobtained by viewing the ocean surface through a vertical polarizer in a specific geometry (nadir angle of\n45o and relative azimuth angle of 135o) to minimize direct sun glint and reflected sky radiation. The\nSIMBAD and SIMBADA data, after proper quality control and processing, are delivered to the SIMBIOS\nproject office for inclusion in the SeaBASS archive. They complement data collected in a similar way by\nthe Laboratoire d\xe2\x80\x99Optique Atmosph\xc3\xa9rique of the University of Lille, France.\nThe SIMBAD and SIMBADA data are used to check the radiometric calibration of satellite oceancolor sensors after launch and to evaluate derived ocean-color variables (i.e., normalized water-leaving\nradiance, aerosol optical thickness, and aerosol type). Analysis of the SIMBAD and SIMBADA data\nprovides information on the accuracy of satellite retrievals of normalized water-leaving radiance, an\nunderstanding of the discrepancies between satellite and in situ data, and algorithms that reduce the\ndiscrepancies, contributing to more accurate and consistent global ocean color data sets.\n\n5.2 RESEARCH ACTIVITIES\nSince November 2002, SIMBAD measurements were made during 7 research cruises of opportunity,\nbringing to 69 the number of campaigns with SIMBAD measurements realized during the period October\n1996-Otober 2003. RESEARCH RESULTS\nAerosol mixtures for ocean color remote sensing\nA Probabilistic Self-Organizing Map (PRSOM) has been applied to AERONET retrievals of aerosol\nsize distribution and refractive index at island and coastal sites (Figures 5.2, 5.3, and 5.4). The PRSOM\nsuggests that there are two strong (likely) situations: weakly absorbing mixtures on the one hand, and\nabsorbing dust or urban soot aerosols on the other hand. Intermediate situations are possible, but not\nprobable. The weakly absorbing mixtures (Figure 5.5) are very different from the classical (Shettle and\nFenn, 1979) aerosol models (Figures 5.6 and 5.7). If we assume that AERONET provides representative\nconditions for ocean color remote sensing, two important results have to be emphasized. First, the classical\nmodels allow one to process low values of the Angstr\xc3\xb6m exponent \xce\xb1 (670), but these conditions may not be\nthe most encountered, even in the open ocean. Second, the PRSOM mixtures indicate a different\nassociation between Angstr\xc3\xb6m exponent and single scatttering albedo. As expected, it is not possible to\nseparate absorbing from weakly absorbing mixtures from a given (observed) \xce\xb5 (\xcf\x87, 670, 865) (Figures 5.8\nand 5.9). However, within one of the two types of situations, the PRSOM neurons can be clearly identified\nusing a directional sampling of the \xce\xb5 parameter (case of POLDER & MISR). Figure 5.9 summarizes the\ndirectional behavior of \xce\xb5 using a principal component analysis.
