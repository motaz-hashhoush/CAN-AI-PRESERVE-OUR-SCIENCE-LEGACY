Descriptions of ball lightning observations such as those\nquoted by Stekol\'nikov (ref. 6) include a great variety of appearances and behaviors. Numerousphysical models have been proposed to account for the formation and continuing existence of a lightning ball.\nThese include models based\non the assumedplasma character of the sphere (refs. i, 2, 4, 5, 7 to 12) as\nwell as one based on inhomogeneousspace charges (ref. 15) and another using\nchemical processes (refs. 14 and 15). One of the barriers to a satisfactory\nmodel has been the assumption that the mechanismmust account for stored energies on the order of 106 joules.\nThese energies are deduced from reports of\none or two instances.\nOne difficulty\nin dealing with this subject is the lack of agreement as to\na definition of ball lightning.\nThe term has been applied to almost any type\nof aerial luminosity.\nSomeof the occurrences can undoubtedly be explained in\nterms of corona discharges or Saint Elmo\'s fire.\nOthers might be incandescent\nor burning material thrown from the point of impact of a lightning bolt.\nA\nwide variety of less probable mechanismscan be invoked to account for isolated\nobservations. Finally there is always the possibility\nthat any particular\nreport may be inaccurate.\nSomeinvestigators, using the etymological approach,\napparently feel that the ball lightning label should be restricted to phenomena\nthat are literally\nlightning in the shape of a ball.\nSuch an approach may be\nthe source of occasional disputes as to whether ball lightning exists.\nTo avoid such difficulties,\nthis report will adopt the lexieographical\napproach and accept as ball lightning any phenomenon\nthat an observer has so\nlabeled. As a consequence, fundamentally different phenomena\nmay be included.\nAlthough not in complete agreement, the summariesof ball lightning reports\ngenerally indicate that very wide ranges of size_ duration, color, brightness,\nand motion have been attributed to ball lightning.\nSizes range from a few\ncentimeters up to manymeters; durations from a fractional second up to tens of\nminutes. Nearly all colors have been cited, and motions have been reported in\ndiverse directions and velocities.\nThe very range of characteristics\nseems\nimmediately to imply either observational inaccuracies of great extent or a\ndiversity in the types of phenomena\nincluded.\nCertain accounts imply that ball lightning my involve substantial amounts\nof energy (on the order of 106 J). Any process capable of storing such energy\nor of confining an energetic plasma for appreciable periods is of obvious interest.\nNevertheless it must be stressed that the occurrences from which such\nenergy estimates can be madeare but a tiny fraction of the total reported,\nactually only a few total instances. On the other hand, manyreports are available implying negligible energy, glowing sphere:3 that disappear quietly.\nTheoretical models that have been proposed have often been criticized\nfor\ntheir inability\nto account for the extreme, high-energy manifestations of ball\nlightning.\nIt has been repeatedly pointed out, for example, that the 106 joules\nsometimesassociated with a lightning ball exceeds the energy involved in the\nionization of an equivalent volume of air.\nFurthermore, the recombination of\nsuch a volume of ionized gas would take place with great speed. Such consider-\n\n_tions led Kapitza (ref. 4) to propose a mechanismthat depends on the postulated existence of an intense electromagnetic radiation to supply a continuous\nenergy input. A plasma spheroid so sustained would exhibit a characteristic\ndimension related to the radiation wavelength_ it would also tend to move in a\nmanner unrelated to air movement.\nSuch a process would explain someof the reported ball lightning features:\nconstancy of size_ erratic motion_ even a possible small bang as the sphere\ncollapses.\nUnfortunately there appears to be no other evidence for the existence of such intense and prolonged naturally occurring radiation.\nIt might\nwell be possible to use such a system to produce a spherical plasma in atmo-sphere_ but this would not prove that any of the ball lightning phenomena\narise\nin like manner.\nThe ball lightning phenomena\nare not easily studied. The occurrences are\nunpredictable and sufficiently\ninfrequent to minimize the chances of bringing\nanalytical\ninstruments to bear. Consequently it is advisable to attempt to\nextract the maximum\namount of information from those occurrences that have been\nobserved even though the circumstances of the observations may preclude a high\ndegree of accuracy. In principle_ given a large body of observations of these\nphenomenait should be possible to use correlation techniques to extract significant relations amongthe characteristics\nand circumstances of the observations.\nOne might say that the signal-to-noise ratio in the raw data is low, but\nthat the signal can still\nbe extracted by the processing of a sufficient\nTaantity of data.\nThis report describes a pair of surveys conducted amongthe employeesat\nNASA\nLewis Research Center.
