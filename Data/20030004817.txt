At upper left, the unit is being tested on a\nderelict ship in Mobile Bay, Alabama. At left it is\nbeing used by the Miami (Florida) Fire Department\naboard a surplus amphibious vehicle capable of\nreaching a fire location by either land or water routes.\nNow commercially\navailable, the module--called\nFirefly--is\nmanufactured\nby Aviation Power Supply,\nInc., Burbank, California.\nBeginning this year, Firefly will undergo a multiyear evaluation in demonstration\ntests and regular\noperational\nuse at St. Louis, Missouri in a program\njointly sponsored by NASA, the Maritime\nAdministration\nand the U.S. Coast Guard. The aim\nof the program is to show the feasibility of reducing\nmarine fire protection\ncosts through emergency use\nof Firefly-equipped\ncommercial\ntugs operating as\nauxiliary fireboats.\nOriginally developed\nby Marshall Space Flight\nCenter for marine use by the Coast Guard, the\nmodule has other applications.\nIt can be mounted\n\non a light truck (above) for use by local fire\ndepartments,\nor it can be transported\nby helicopter\n(above right) to hard-to-reach\nfire scenes, for example,\nin forests, on high-rise buildings or on offshore oil\nrigs. It also has utility in nonfirefighting\napplications\nsuch as flood control or emergency pumping for\nmunicipal water supplies.\nCompletely\nself-contained\nin a compact package\nweighing less than 3,000 pounds, the Firefly can\ndraw water from the sea, a river or other sources\nand pump up to 2,500 gallons a minute through two\nwater "cannons."\nThe two-stage pump, a derivative\nof liquid rocket engine pumps, was developed\nfor\nNASA by Northern Research and Engineering\nCorporation\n(NREC), a division of Ingersoll-Rand;\nNREC also produces\nthe pumps for the commercial\nversion. Power for the pump is generated\nby an\naerospace-type\ngas turbine built by Detroit Diesel\nAllison Division of General Motors Corporation.\nAviation Power Supply, Inc., has teamed with\nNREC and Detroit Diesel to develop an advanced\nFirefly II capable of pumping 3,000 gallons a minute;\nthe new unit is expected to go into production\nsoon.\n\n83\n\n'
