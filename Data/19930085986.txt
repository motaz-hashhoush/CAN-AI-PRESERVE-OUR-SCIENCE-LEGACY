Variations in ram-pressure ratio (flight\nspeed) change the performance characteristics by effectively changing the compression ratio of the engine. In general, the increased\noperating pressure with increasing ram-pressure ratio raises the\ntotal expansion pressure ratio of the engine (from turbine inlet to\njet-nozzle throat) until critical flow is established in the jet\nnozzle. At lower engine speeds, the corrected tail-pipe indicated gas temperature decreased with increasing\nram-pressure ratio.\nEffect of Jet-Nozzle Area on Performance\nA rational examination of the effect of jet-nozzle area on\nengine performance, neglecting secondary effects, indicates that a\nreduction in jet-nozzle area increases the resistance to flow\nthrough the nozzl~thus raising the tail-pipe pressure. Because,\nfrom considerations of typical centrifugal-compressor performance,\nthe compressor pressure ratio remains nearly constant for small\nchanges in air flow, the higher tail-pipe pressure caused by a\nreduction in jet-nozzle area results in a decreased expansion pressure ratio across the turbine. In order to maintain the required\nturbine power output, it is then necessary to raise the turbineinlet temperature, which results in an increased tail-pipe temperature as well. The engine air consumption is essentially proportional to the turbine-inlet pressure and inversely proportional\nto the square root of the turbine-inlet temperature. Because the\nturbine-inlet pressure, as previously explained, remains nearly\nconstant and the turbine-inlet temperature rises, a small decrease\nin air consumption would be expected. This decrease would cause\nthe turbine-inlet temperature to rise slightly at the same fuel flow\nused with the larger jet nozzle, but an increase in fuel flow to\nthe engine is probably necessary to attain the turbine-inl et temperature required by the smaller jet nozzle. The higher tail-pipe\npressure caused by a reduction in jet-nozzle area results in an\nincrease in jet-nozzle-throat velocity in the subcritical flow\nrange, or in a greater excess of nozzle-throat static pressure over\nambient static pressure in the critical flow range. In either case,\nthe jet thrust for a given gas flow would increase. Because the\njet-nozzle-throat velocity varies directly as the square root of the\ntail-pipe temperature and the engine air consumption varies inversely\nas the square root of the turbine-inlet temperature, these effects\ntend to cancel each other and should not have much effect on the jet\nthrust. The net result should therefore be an increase in jet\nthrust due to the higher tail-pipe pressure.\n\nNACA RM E9I27\n\n(J)\n\nCD\n\n(J)\n\n11\n\nPerformance parameters at a simulated altitude o~ 30,000 feet\nand a ram-pressure ratio of 1.30 using the 18.41-inch-diameter jet\nnozzle are compared with the engine performance using the standard\nnozzle (18.75-in. diameter) data from reference 1 (figs. 28 to 33).\nThe decrease in area of 3.5 percent showed the expected trends,\ncausing a small increase in jet thrust, net thrust, and tail-pipe\nindicated gas temperature over the entire speed range.
