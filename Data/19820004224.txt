V-C the third in a series of nine advanced international telecommunications satellites owned and operated by the\n106-nation International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat), is scheduled to be launched by the NASA Kennedy\nSpace Center on board an Atlas Centaur launch vehicle no earlier\nthan Dec. 9, 1981, from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Fla.\nThe first two Intelsat Vs were successfully\non Dec. 6, 1980 and May 23,\n\nlaunched by NASA\n\n1981.\n\nIntelsat V-C weighs 1,870\n\nkilograms (4,110 pounds) at launch\n\nand has almost double the communications capability of satellites\nin the earlier Intelsat IV series -- 12,000 simultaneous two-way\nvoice circuits and two color television channels.\n\nNovember 27,\nAeronautics and Space Administration). . 8\nCSCL 22B\n02687\n\n1981\n\n-2-\n\nPhysically it is 15.9 meters (52 feet) long and 6.4 m (21\nft.) wide in orbit.\n\nIt will be positioned initially in geosyn-\n\nchronous orbit at 15 degrees east longitude while it undergoes\nextensive in-orbit checkout.\nThe Intelsat V series of communications satellites are built\nby the Ford Aerospace and Communications Corp., Palo Alto,\nCalif., using system components developed by firms in the United\nKingdom, France, the Federal Republic of Germany, Italy and\nJapan.\nThe International Telecommunications Satellite Organization\nis headquartered in Washington, D.C.\n\nNASA is reimbursed for all\n\ncosts of the Atlas Centaur and launch services under the provisions of a launch services agreement signed in May last year.\nThe Atlas Centaur (AC-55) will place the Intelsat V-C into\na planned elliptical transfer orbit ranging from 166.6\nkilometers (103 to 22,347 miles).\n\nto 35,964\n\nIt is from this orbit at apo-\n\ngee that a solid propellant rocket motor attached to the satellite will be fired on command from the Intelsat launch control\ncenter in Washington, D.C.,\n\nto circularize the orbit at geosyn-\n\nchronous altitude over the equator.\n\nAt that altitude, because\n\nthe speed of the satellite in orbit matches the rotational speed\nof the Earth, the satellite remains in position over one spot.\n\n-more-\n\n-3-\n\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center, Cleveland, has management\nresponsibility for Atlas Centaur development and operation.\nNASA\'s Kennedy Space Center, Fla., is responsible for launch\nvehicle checkout and launch.\nOverall direction of the NASA expendable launch vehicle program is vested in the Office of Space Transportation Operations\nin Washington,\n\nD.C.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE; BACKGROUND Kennedy Space Center is responsible for the\npreparation and launch of Atlas Centaur AC-55, which will carry\nIntelsat V-C into orbit.\nThe Atlas and Centaur stages of Atlas Centaur 55 arrived at\nthe Cape Canaveral Air Force Station on May 27, 1981.\nThe Atlas\nwas erected on Pad B of Launch Complex 36 on July 16, 1981, and\nthe Centaur stage on July 18, 1981.\nA Terminal Countdown Demonstration Test was carried out on Sept. 30, 1981.\nThe Intelsat V-C satellite arrived on Oct. 24, 1981, and was\nchecked out in Hangar AO at Cape Canaveral. It was moved on Nov.\n16, 1981 to the Explosive Safe Area for final servicing and capsulation. There, the satellite\'s attitude control system was\nfueled with hydrazine, the apogee kick motor was installed and\nthe protective shroud was put in place.\nThe satellite and the launch vehicle were scheduled for\nmating on Pad B on Dec. 1,
