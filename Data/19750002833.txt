These primary clocks which provide the accurate time are maintained on\na long term basis and are traceable to the origin of an epoch. The time signal\'\nare disseminated through radio frequency emissions or other techniques to users,\nincluding other national time keeping agencies. The international comparison\nof time signals among the national time keeping agencies formulate the data\nbase for the adoption of uniform time scales such as the Coordinated Universal\nTime (UTC) and the International Atomic Time (TAI). It is the time as a scale\nrather than the time as an epoch that is in increasing demand by modern users.\nWith the advent of space science and applications in the last decade the requirements for precise and accurate time and time interval have approached the\ncapabilities of national time keeping and maintenance agencies. To satisfy the\nsophisticated users, the national time keeping agencies issue time corrections\nperiodically in the form of bulletins or announcements. These corrections are\ngiven relative to a primary time standard or master clock. For example, the\nBureau International de l\'Huere (BIH) issues a monthly circular, circular D,\nwhich gives the time comparison between the various national time standards\nrelative to UTC and TAI. The use of these bulletins, announcements, and\ncirculars and the long term stability of the primary time standards relative to\nthe international adopted time scales is presented in this report. Users who\nneed precise and accurate time and time interval on the order of microseconds\nor better undoubtedly recognize the need to use these corrections. The proper\ninterpretation of the user\'s requirements in terms of time or time interval, the\ndifference between accuracy and precision of measurement, and the accuracy\nof maintaining a clock relative to a time scale is generally the responsibility\nof th4 users who must communicate effectively to those experts who generate\nand maintain the time standards and/or who provide the techniques for clock\nsynchronization.\nCLOCK COMPARISON TECHNIQUE AND DATA\naccuracy and precision.\nClock comparison techniques are numerous and vary\nF bands have\nIn general, radio frequency transmissions in VLF, LF, a\nbeen used as the work-horse and provided continuous, reliable an real-time\n1\n\ntime transfer references for clock comparisons. Portable clocks, satellites\n(both natural and artificial), and coherent radiations from quasars are in increasing use to meet specific needs. As the accuracy in timing requirements\nis increased, not only must the accuracy of transmission and the precision of\nmeasurement be increased, but also the stability of the oscillators (fly wheel)\nwhich generate the time must be increased.\nThe most often used time transfer reference signal for clock comparison among\nprimary clocks in national laboratories is the 100 kHz transmissions of the\nLORAN-C navigational system. At present, LORAN-C consists of eight chains\nwhich provide adequate coverage for the northern hemisphere. The resolution\nof time comparison of an identified cycle of the received signal is about 1/100th\nof a cycle or 0. 1 microseconds. The long term stability of the propagation\ndelay, even for groundwave, is probably not much better than \xc2\xb1 0. 5 microsecond. The long term stability of the time transmission of a LORAN-C chain\nat present is about one order of magnitude lower.\nThe data collected by each national laboratory or observatory is published and\nis available to the users. For example, in the United States the Naval Observatory issues to general users a series of time bulletins and announcements on\na weekly basis, and sends corrections by telegrams on a daily basis to special\nusers. The National Bureau of Standards issues a special publication, NBS\nSpecial Publication 236, on a monthly basis. In other countries, for example\nin the United Kingdom, the Royal Greenwich Observatory publishes a monthly\nTime Service Circular. Also, the National Research Council of Canada publishes their LORAN-C measurements in letter form once every ten days and\nTime and Frequency Bulletins monthly. The Institute Electrotecnico Nazionale\nof Italy publishes a monthly circular and the Bureau International de l\'Heure\npublishes monthly circulars and annual reports. The contents, as well as the\nfrequency of publications vary as do the needs. In general, the information\nis readily available and the publications can be had upon request. Typical\ntime service notices, bulletins, announcements, and circulars are given in\nAppendix A.\nHISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF ATOMIC TIME\nPresent primary time standards, as maintained by national laboratories, are\nbased on cesium atomic oscillators which are made either in the laboratories\nor by a commercial firm. The time maintained by these oscillators is referred\nto as atomic time. Historically, it was the weighted average of nine cesium\natomic standards which were located in nine laboratories in four countries. 2\nThis average was maintained by the U. S. Naval Observatory (USNO) and\nformulated the atomic time scale referred to as A. 1. A. 1 is presently the\n\n2\n\naverage of an ensemble of 15 to 30 commercial cesium atomic standards\nmaintained at the USNO. 3 Each individual national laboratory also maintained\nan atomic time scale identified with a laboratory such as the U. S. Atomic Time\n(USAT) of the National Bureau of Standards which is now known as AT(NBS).\nOther examples are the Greenwich atomic time (GA) and now the GA2 of the\nRoyal Greenwich Observatory, and the A3 of the BIH.\nAs the various atomic time scales went through the process of evolution it\nbecame obvious to many that an international standard must be adopted. 4,5\nThus, the XIIIth General Conference of Weights and Measures (CGPM) adopted\nin 1967, the definition of the second as "the duration of 9,192,631,770 periods\nof the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine\nlevels of the ground state of the cesium atom 133. " The CGPM also defined\nInternational Atomic Time (TAI) as "the time coordinate established by the\nBIH on the basis of the readings of atomic clocks operating in various establishments conforming to the definition of the seconds, the unit of time of the\nInternational System (SI) of Units."\nThe XIIth Plenary Assembly of the International Radio Consultative Committee\nof the International Telecommunication Union, adopted in 1970 the improved\nUTC system. This system eliminated the changing frequency offset between\nUTC and TAI and increased the step-time adjustments from 0. 1 to 1 second\nwhich is now called a leap second. 6 Thus the UTC and TAI have the same rate.\nCLOCK COMPARISONS\nBased on the published clock correction data of BIH, the atomic time scales,\nas maintained by several national laboratories and observatories, are plotted\nfor 900 days as shown in Figures 1 and 2.
