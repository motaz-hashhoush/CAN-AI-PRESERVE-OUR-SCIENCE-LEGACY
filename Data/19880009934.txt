Introduction\nConvection and segregation in directional solidification and crystal growth by the\nBridgman-Stockbarger technique have been traditionally treated by assuming axisymmetric\n1\nthermal conditions on the ampoule wall. For most recent work and earlier references see [ 1 .\nIt is, however, difficult to achieve such a condition in an experimental setup. Conventional\nheating devices (furnaces) typically have several degrees asymmetry in the temperature\ndistribution of a plane normal to the geometrical symmetry axis. This can be significantly\nimproved upon using heat pipes. But the Bridgman technique requires axial temperature\ngradients, i.e. transition zones between two quasi-isothermal zones (heat pipes) at different\ntemperatures. Furthermore the temperatures required for the melt growth of most\ntechnologically important materials are high enough that radiative heat transfer to the ampoule is\nsignificant, if not dominant. Then, any axial misalignment of the ampoule, even in a perfectly\naxisymmetric gradient, wl lead to a non-axisymmetric temperature distribution on the ampoule\nil\nwall. This is due to the different view factors of the ampoule surface elements in a plane\nnorhal to the ampoule\'s axis.\nAny deviation from an axisymmetric temperature field on the wall of a vertical ampoule\nrepresents a horizontal temperature gradient. The horizontal density gradient that results\nfrom thermal expansion in the melt under this condition must lead on Earth to some\nbouyancy-driven convection, no matter what the axial (vertical) temperature distribution that\nis imposed on the melt. It is the purpose of this study to estimate the magnitude of such\nconvective flows for conditions representative of the MSFC mercury-cadmium-telluride (MCT)\nBridgman setup.\n\n2. One would expect that Hart\'s model, due to less\nflow impedance by no-slip surfaces, results in a somewhat larger convection velocity than our\ntotally confined geometry. This expectation was, indeed, confirmed by the value of 0.83\nc d s e c that we obtained for the maximum velocity, as compared to 0.27 c d s e c from our\ncylindrical model for otherwise similar conditions.\nIt should be emphasized that such high convection velocities that result from "small"\ndeviations from axisymmetric thermal boundary conditions, exceed diffusion velocities\ncharacteristic of semiconductor melts by some three orders of magnitude. These convection\nvelocities also exceed those obtained in earlier Bridgman modelling work (Grenoble furnace)\n[l], assuming axisymmetry, by one to two orders of magnitude. In addition, the convective\nflow resulting from the asymmetry condition extends throughout the melt. This is in contrast\nto the highly localized, interfacial flows obtained in the earlier modelling. These localized\nflows arise from the mismatch of the thermal conductivitiesof melt, crystal and container.\nOur preliminaxy study needs to be expanded considerably. For instance, the stabilizing\neffect of larger vertical temperature gradients needs to be explored. Furthermore, the effect of\nreducing the seventy of the asymmetric thermal boundary condition needs to be investigated by\nimposing flux conditions rather than a fixed temperature profile. But is it probably safe to say,\ne;en at this early point, that our model shows that symmetric transport conditions in the\nBndgman technique will be very difficult to realize in the laboratory practice. Future modelling\nmust recognize this.\n\n5.\n\n[I]\n\nReferences\n\nP.M. Adornato and R.A. Brown, J. Crystal Growth, 80 (1987) 155.\n\n9\n\n[2]\n\nM.S.
