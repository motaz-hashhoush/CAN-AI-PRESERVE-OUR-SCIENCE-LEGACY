Lipscomb, M. D.\nBaylor University College of Medicine\nRhythmicity of function appears to be an inherited characteristic\nof all biological material on this earth. The earliest observations of\nsuch rhythms of which we have complete recorded information dates to the\nmiddle of the eighteenth century when the French astronomer De Marian\ndescribed rhythmic daily movementsof plant leaves, as recorded by a\ncrude kymograph. During the next 250 years, the literature\nhas become\nreplete with descriptions of biological rhythmicity in every known genera\nand species, from plant and animal unicellular\norganisms up to whole\norgan systems including man. Additionally,\nthe types of rhythms have\nencompassed\nevery conceivable area of biochemistry, motor-function, and\nmorphologic growth.\nFrom rhythmic leaf movementsof plants to inter-tidal\nmigration and\ncolor change in Crustacea to daily fluctuations\nin electrolytes in man,\nrhythms have been described in virtually\nevery measurable biological process. Additionally,\nit is important to note that the character of these\nrhythms has invariably possessed a striking correlation with external\ngeophysical events, most notably solar or lunar cycles possessing generally a period of twelve or twenty-four hours. Indeed, so precisely synchronized do these rhythmic biological events seemto be to external\nevents (primarily, day-night cycles) that they have been termed diurnal,\nnychthermal, or endodiurnal rhythms.\nClassic descriptions of rhythmic phenomenain plants and animals\nover these years have generally been restricted to short-term series of\nobservations with little\nor no attempt to interpret the underlying mechanisms generating such rhythmic behavior. The earliest and apparently\nmost logical explanation for such events was that from a teliologic\nstandpoint rhythmic events existing in biological life were triggered and\nentrained and synchronized with the day-night cycle. Two points, however, mitigate against this as a totally valid hypothesis, the one being that such rhythms are seen to persist even after day-night cycles\nare abolished or exist in blind individuals,\nand the more recent observation that rhythms under certain circumstances do not possess the precise 24-hour period, but rather periods somewhatshorter or somewhat\nlonger than precisely 24 hours. In fact, this latter observation\nprompted the coining of the term circadian which means"about a day,"\n\n116\n\nto suggest that the rhythms were not precisely 24 hours in length.\nThe\nobservation of the persistence of rhythms under constant conditions and\nthe presence of persistence of rhythms with periods longer or shorter\nthan our customary geophysical day-night cycle has led several observers\nto believe that someform of endogenousclock-mechanisms must exist which\nmay modulate or at least integrate external signals coming from our terrestrial environment.\nThe time has now come, therefore, to go from simple observation of\nthe phenomena an investigation\nto\nin depth of the mechanismsunderlying\nbiological rhythmicity.\nThis is particularly\nimportant in terms of the\nmannedspace effort for the following reason. Recent observations on\nhumansdisplaced by jet travel rapidly across time zones east-west or\nwest-east suggest that such displacement abolishes these rhythms for a\nperiod of several days, and during this period of abolition of rhythms,\nhumanperformance of discrete sensory-motor tasks is seriously impaired.\nThe questions which we might pose here, therefore, are whether or not\nsuch rhythmic behavior in biological and metabolic functions are tantamount to efficient humanperformance, and whether or not, under conditions of extra-terrestrial\nflight,\nwe might lose useful external time\ngivers, thereby suffering impairment of performance. If such proves to\nbe the case, it may well be worthwhile that we consider providing rhythmic exteroceptive cues in order to maintain rhythmic biological functions, and thereby performance. Furthermore, we would like to know, if\nthese rhythms prove to be exogenous in nature, whether or not an heirarchy of external clues exists, such as, whether light, then temperature,\nthen humidity, then barometric pressure act as sequentially important\ntime givers to humanbiological rhythms and whether an heirarchy exists\nin the rhythms themselves. For instance, whether or not rhythmic secretion of Dituitary adrenal hormonesmight be critical\nwhereas body temperature might be rather unimportant to adequate performance. These\nthen are the major questions which we would like to answer in our own\nstudy of rhythms, and to which we are currently addressing our efforts.\nTowardsthis end, we are currently undertaking for the National\nAeronautics and SpaceAdministration a series of experiments conducted\nwithin the Texas Medical Center at the Baylor University College of\nMedicine designed to study rhythmic biological,\nbiochemical, and physiologic events in normal humans, alter these rhythms by subtle manipulation\nof the external environment (light, duration) and assess the ability of\nthe subjects discrete sensory-motor tasks of vigilance, time-perception,\nand other coordinated hand-eye movements. In such an environment, we\nintend to place subjects for protracted periods of time and subject them\nto the environment of altered lighting situations,\nsimultaneously measuring several parameters of physiologic function (blood pressure, pulse,\nrespiration, temperature, EKG, EOG,EEG), parameters of biochemical function in urine (electrolytes,\nsteroids, and catechol amines), and discrete\n\n117\nparameters of sensory-motor performance (time-perception, judgment,\nproblem-solving, radar-tracking, vigilance).\nSubjects confined to this\nchamberwill be given no external clues as to time of day or night; and\nin such a chamber, time disorientation poses as the only major sensory\ndeprivation imposed upon the experimental subjects.\nIn the establishment of a laboratory capability for the performance\nof these biochemical determinations, we are presently prepared to offer\nto other NASAcontractees laboratory facilities\nfor the performance of\na number of biochemical, biological assay, and pharmacologic determinations on body fluids, which are enumerated in Appendix 1.
