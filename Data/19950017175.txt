Phase-ResolvedSpectroscopy\nWe have made a preliminary attempt at calculating spectra for particular rotation\nphases. A detailed application of this analysis is still in progress. The folded data were\nfirst divided into five phasebins, as shown in Figure 8 (the total data set for the September\n1993observation). Then the five individual pulse height spectra were normalized by their\neffective exposure times, and divided by the average spectrum. The resulting "ratio"\nspectra are displayed in Figure 9. Any change in the soft blackbody temperature with\nrotation phase would make itself evident as a change in slope of the points between 0.1\nand 0.5 keV. It can be seen that the temperature is roughly constant with phase, with\nperhaps a slight decreasein phasebins 1 and 5 where the intensity is smallest, and a slight\nincreasein the other phasebins. This is consistent with a model in which the modulation\nof the soft blackbody component is due to small differencesin temperature over the surface\nof the star. This will be tested further with detailed spectral fitting. The statistics are poor\non the harder component becauseit contains few photons, even in the summed spectrum.\nNevertheless,the temperature at harder energiesappears to be greatest in phase bin 2,\nwhich contains the peak of the hard component (since it leads the soft pulse by about\n90\xc2\xb0 . Overall, the spectra and pulse profiles appear consistent with our two-temperature\nmodel, in which rotation is responsiblefor carrying the emissionregionsin and out of view\nwithout greatly changing their apparent temperatures.\n\n-4-\n\n2. DIFFUSE EMISSION AND PATHOLOGICAL SEYFERT SPECTRA\nThis program combinesPSPC and HRI observationsof selectedSeyfert galaxieswhich\nhave unusual and vraiable spectra. The purpose is to disentangle diffuse X-ray emission\nfrom the nuclear source, in order to properly interpret the soft X-ray spectral shapesin\nterms of partial covering and/or warm-absorber models. The targets in the program are\nNGC 3516,NGC 3227,and NGC 7314. So far, we haveperformed a detailed analysis only\non NGC 3516, and we summarize the results here.\nNGC 3516 is historically the Seyfert galaxy with the highest degree of soft X-ray\nvariability. In addition to intrinsic variability of at least a factor of 30, the apparent column\ndensity has ranged from > 1024to essentially zero. NGC 3516 also displays variable UV\nabsorption lines of the type that are sometimesthough of as low-velocity analoguesof the\nsamephenomenonin BAL QSOs. Figure 24 is our PSPC light curve, which indicates that\nthere was variabilty of about 25% in one day. The power-law spectral fit displayed in\nFigure 25 shows the classic signature of deviations due to a warm absorber. Indeed, a fit\nto a warm-absorber model (Figure 26) is a good fit, with an edge of optical depth 0.9 at\n0.75 keV that can be attributed to O VII with a column density of 4 \xc3\x97 1018,equivalent to\na hydrogen column of 5.7 \xc3\x97 1021 Otherwise, there is no significant cold column above the\n.\nGalactic value of 3 \xc3\x97 102\xc2\xb0. Dividing the data into high and low states (Figures 27 and 28)\nreveal no significant differencesin the parametersof the absorption edge,as the overlapping\nconfidencecontours in Figures 29 and 30 demonstrate. Given that the intensity changedby\nonly 25_, it is not surprising that the properties of the warm absorber remained roughly\nthe same.\nWe plan to combine these data with historical X-ray spectra and ASCA spectrum\nthat we have also obtained, in order to specify the state of the warm absorber, and relate\nit to the UV absorption lines and large-amplitude historical X-ray variability.\n\n-6-\n\n4. In this Letter we present the results of our investigation\nof these allegedly normal spiral galaxies with extreme X-ray\nluminosities.\n\nThe cosmic X-ray background\n(XRB) is thought to originate\nfrom the integrated\nemission of discrete sources. The main\nweakness of this explanation\nis, however, that the characteristic spectra of known classes of extragalactic\nX-ray emitters\nare all considerably\nsofter than the spectrum of the XRB itself.\nSince active galactic nuclei (AGNs) are the dominant class of\nextragalactic\nX-ray sources at high fluxes, it is commonly\nbelieved that they are responsible for the vast majority of the\nXRB. However, the space density and correlation\nlength scale\nof AGNs, in addition\nto their typical broad-band\nX-ray\nspectra, are incompatible\nwith an XRB origin in which AGNs\ncontribute\nmore than ~ 50% of the 2-10 keV flux (Fabian &\nBarcons 1992, and references therein). Furthermore,\nthe best\nestimate to date of the soft X-ray luminosity function of AGNs\n(Boyle et al. 1993) rules out AGNs producing all of the XRB\nbelow 2 keV.\nThe contribution\nof star-forming\ngalaxies to the XRB has\nbeen frequently considered, at both soft and hard X-ray energies (Persic et al. 1989; Griffiths & Padovani 1990; Lonsdale &\nHarmon 1991; Fruscione\n& Griffiths 1991; Rephaeli et al.\n1991; Green, Anderson,\n& Ward\n1992; David, Jones, &\nForman 1992). The most plausible scenario for such a contribution, suggested\nby Griffiths & Padovani\n(1990), proposes\nthat galaxies undergoing bursts of star formation in regions of\nlow metallicity could spawn enough luminous high-mass X-ray\nbinaries to have both high-integrated\nX-ray luminosities\n(> 1042" ergs s 1) and spectra well matched\nto the spectrum of\nthe XRB. Possible examples of such starburst\ngalaxies have\nsurfaced in a recently published study of infrared- and X-ray-selected galaxies, which has claimed to find a new class of\nX-ray-luminous,\n"normal"\n(non-AGN)\ngalaxies (Boiler et al.\n\n2.
