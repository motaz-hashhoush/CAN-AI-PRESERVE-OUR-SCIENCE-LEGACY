a parametric study of the rollout distance required by the Orbiter. The\nstudy considers the maximum expected dispersions in the landing speed and the\ntouchdown point. These dispersions are assumed to be correlated so that a fast\nlanding occurs before the nominal touchdown point. The maximum rollout distance\nis required by the maximum landing speed with a 10-knot tailwind and the center\nof mass at the forward -l imit of its longitudinal travel. The maximum weight\nthat can be stopped within 15 000 feet on a hot day at Kennedy Space Center\n(KSC) is 248 800 pou.-as. The energy absorbed by the brakes would exceed the\nlimit for reuse of the brake.\n\n79FM44\n2.0 INTRODUCTION\nThe first part of this section will describe the basic sequence of events during\nthe rollout of the Orbiter. The pitch at touchdown is maintained for several\nseconds in an attempt to prevent the Orbiter from bouncing. The elevons must be\ndeflected in a positive direction (downward) in order to decrease the pitch. The\nimmediate effect of this deflection is to increase the lift of the Orbiter in addition to causing a nose-down moment. The lift of the Orbiter will decrease as\nthe pitch decreases, but the initial increase in lift may be enough to cause the\nOrbiter to rise from the runway. This tendency may be decreased by maintaining\nthe initial pitch for a short time.\nAfter the initial delay, the pitch is decreased to some ar,)atrary value and then\nheld constant until the equivalent airspeed (EAS) decrease6 to a specified\nvalue. The present values of these parameters are 6 degrees and 165 knots, respectively. The delay in completing the pitchdown serves to reduce the maximum\nload on the main landing gear. The maximum load occurs as the nose gear touches\nthe runway, when the Orbiter is at a negative angle of attack, and the elevons\nare still deflected upward. A delay in the pitchdown will reduce the dynamic\npressure and consequently reduce the load on the main landing gear, but the\ndelay in the pitchdown is limited because it will increase the rollout distance.\nThe deceleration of the Orbiter is greater with all the tires on the runway than\nat some positive pitch angle, and the start of braking cannot begin before some\ninterval after nose-gear touchdown. The delay in pitchdown is also limited by\nthe ability of the elevons to control the pitch. If the pitchdown is delayed\ntoo long, the pitch rate may become uncontrollable and cause excesssively high\nloads on the nose gear.\nThe start of braking will be determined by either the maximum energy that the\nbrakes can absorb or by the time that the nose gear touches the runway. \'there\nare two pertinent values of the energy that can be absorbed by the brakes. The\nfirst of these is the energy that the brakes can absorb and be useable on another flight. The second energy limit is the amount of energy that the brakes\ncan absorb and still provide braking on that landing. The start of the braking\ncannot begin until the nose gear has been on the runway for approximately 3 sec ord- =d until the speed has become less than the speed corresponding to the\nhigher energy level. It would be desirable not to begin braking until the speed\nhas decreased enough to allow the brakes to be reused.\nA complete simulation of the rollout would require the use of a comprehensive\nsix-degree-of-freedom solution. The amount of computer time required to perform\na parametric study with such a program is prohibitive. An approximate solution\nto the motion of the Orbiter during the rollout was documented in reference 1.\nThis approximate solution was used to calculate the total rollout distance for\na range of Orbiter weights, longitudinal position of the center of mass, wind\ncondition, and density altitudes. Me results of the study are presented in\nthis report.\n\n2\n\n79FM44\n3.0 METHOD\nThe derivation of the approximate solution to the motion of the Orbiter during\nrollout is discussed in detail in reference 1. The assumptions that were made\nduring the development of the equation of motion are constant mass, constant\nwindspeed, no crosswind, no thrust, constant grade of the runway, and no brakes\non the nose-gear wheels. The absence of nose-gear brakes causes the aerodynamic\nforces line of action, the aerodynamic pitching moment, and the center-of-mass\npositions to be introduced into the equation of motion with all the wheels on\nthe runway. The coefficient of rolling friction is constant above some speed,\nand the braking coefficient is a quadratic Function of the groundspeed but is\naveraged over the integration interval. The effectiveness of the brakes\nis represented as a constant ratio to the perfect value of unity. The coefficients of lift, drag, and moment are linear functions of the elevon position and\nthe effects of variations in pitch are averaged over the integration interval.\nThe acceleration of the center of mass normal to runway is ignored. The equation of motion has the same form for all phases of the rollout and can be\nintegrated exactly over the chosen interval, which is limited only by the necessity to average the effects of changes in the pitch and the braking coefficient.\nIt is possible to develop an equation for the amount of energy absorbed by the\nbrakes. This equation is used to determine the maximum speed at which the\nbrakes could be applied without absorbing more than a specified amount of\nenergy. The actual braking speed is either this value or , the speed 3 seconds\nafter the nose gear touches the runway.\nThe approximate solution is used to determine the maximum length of runway that\nwould be required for a range of weights and atmospheric conditions at either\nKSC or Edwards Air Force Base (EAFB). The nominal landing speed is the minimum\nlanding speed plus the decrease in speed during the 5 seconds prior to touchdown\nand an additional allowance of 12 knots. The minimum landing speed is limited\nby the angle of attack at which some portion of the Orbiter would scrape the\nrunway. The decrease in speed during the 5-second interval is based on the deceleration at the minimum landing speed. The nominal touchdown point is 3000\nfeet from the start of the runway. The maximum expected dispersions in the landing speed and the touchdown point are +24 knots and +1650 feet, respectively.\nThe dispersions are assumed to be correlated such that the: highest landing\nspeed occurs at the shortest touchdown point. These nominal conditions and\ndispersions are applicable only for the 240 000-pound Orbiter, but they are\napplied over the entire weight range as a convenience. This should cause\nthe results of the lower weights to be conservative.\nAdditional conditions that are used in the calculations are listed in table I.\nThe longitudinal position of the center of mass is limited to lie between 65.0\nand 67.5 percent. The final position of the speedbrake is limited by its effect\non the rate of rudder movement. The rollout distance is not changed\nsignificantly by changes of 5 to 10 percent in the final position of the\nspeedbrake so that the final value is not critical.\n\n3\n\n79FM44\n4.0 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION\nThe relationship between the ratio of the local atmospheric: density to the sea\nlevel value of the 1962 Standard Atmosphere (ref. 2) and the density altitude is\nshown in figure 1.
