The fabrication of the ferrite\nplanes by pressure-sintering yields storage planes with protruding conductors\non regular center spacings. The pressure-sintering was developed sufficiently\nto produce planes with acceptable mechanical and magnetic properties if the\nconductors are platinum (maximum processing temperature of 1230%). Further\ndevelopment is required to lower the processing temperature to accommodate\ngold conductors (melting temperatures of 1063%). The combination of p-channel\nMOS devices and n-p-n bipolar transistors in the integrated address selection,\ncurrent drive, and sense circuitry results in optimum performance consistent\nw th low power drain. .A 13-million-bit module, 65,536 words x 200 bits, consuming 20 to 25 watts is estimated to occupy 4 to 6 cubic feet. The electronics\ncomplement of the module includes 65,536 integrated bipolar drivers, a 1024output-binary-tree address decoder, 200 sense amplifiers, 200 dual uni,polar\ndigit drivers, and a 200 x 16 digit-sense multiplex circuit. These circuits\nwere designed and evaluated in breadboard form. Further developments in the\nareas of Large-scale integration of the electronic circuitry and mass interconnection techniques are required to fabricate this type of mass memory.\n\n1\n\ny\t\n\n(f4Y\t\n\n\t\n^; d 7,vi a\n\ni\n\nI\n\n0t.\xe2\x80\xa2 "\n\t\n\nr-4\n\n1. INTRODUCTION\n\nLaminated ferrite and integrated semiconductor are appealing technologies\nto combine for the realization of a medium-speed, mass random-access memory.\nThe high bit-packing density and low-power operating capability of laminated\nferrite arrays are a natural match for the high-density and relatively low-power\nhandling characteristic of large-scale integrated .,emiconductors. The system\ngoals are a capacity of 10 8 bits at a cycle time on the order of 2 \xc2\xb5sec with\nthe constraints of minimum power, volume, and weight to make such a memory\npractical for application to on-board data processing in spacecraft.\nThe work described here is a culmination of previous RCA activity with the\ngeneral goal of a mass random-access memory (Refs. 1-5). The experience of\nthose programs and the background of rapidly changing technology led to a\nmodification in approach in two important areas; the laminated ferrites were\nto be fabricated utilizing pressure-sintering (Ref. 6), and the building blocks\nof the integrated electronics were to be p-channel MOS transistors combined with\nn-p-n bipolar transistors. Pressure-sintering was found to be a means of\nforming a laminate with suitable magnetic properties while holding a tolerance\nin lateral dimensions sufficient to permit automated interconnection. The MOS\ntransistor itself is not as capable a device for high-current or high-gain-bandwidth applications as the bipolar transistor. The combination of MOS and bipolar\ndevices, called BIMOS, is extremely attractive for laminated ferrite electronics.\nThe BIMOS integrated arrays are circuits with low power drain because of their\ncomplementary nature but with the high performance necessary for signal amplification and current driving.\nThe work described in this report was pursued at RCA Laboratories, Princeton,\nNew Jersey and at RCA Electronic Components, Somerville, New Jersey. LAMINATE FABRICATION BY PRESSURE-SINTERING\n\nIntrod uction\n\nA.\t\n\nThe hiring of a laminated ferrite plane causes it to shrink approximately\n17% in all dimensions; the shrinkage, however, is not precisely reproducible and\n\nmay, in .fact, be nonuniform throughout n laminate. In order that interconnections be made rapidly and accurately the laminate conductors must be precisely spaced. A possible solution to this problem is to employ a procedure\ncalled pressure-sintering. Here the unfired laminate is placed between two flat\npunches, or plates, and pressed during firing. The application of pressure in\nthis way can reduce the lateral firing shrinkage to less than 1%, thus providing\nthe desired dimedsional uniformity. The required volume shrinkage is obtained\nby a decrease in thickness.\nThe electrical resistance of the conductors, particularly in large arrays,\nshould be very low. Gold, silver, and copper, then, would be the most obvious\ncandidates for conductor material. Goad is the most suitable material because\nsilver and copper entor the spinel structure, adversely altering the magnetic\nproperties. The use of pressure-sintering offers the best possibility of\nobtaining satisfactory laminates using gold conductors because processing\ntemperatures below the melting point of gold can be used with this method.\n\nB. WORD SELECTION DRIVE CIRCUITRY\n\nA. Introduction\nThe basic requirements of the integrated word selection-drive circuitry\nare to select and to drive one of 65,536 laminated ferrite memory word lines,\n64 planes of 1024 words, with bi-directional read and write current pulses.\nThe drive circuitry must be capable of generating a 150- to 200-mA peak read\ncurrent, followed by a 75- to 100-mA write current of the opposite polarity,\nwith both rise-times and pulse widths of approximately 200 nsec. The words are\n400 crossovers long. The selection of a particular word within a given memory\nmodule is accomplished through two levels of decoding. One of 64 individual\nplanes and one of 1024 lines are simultaneously selected by their associated\naddress decoders. To meet the system requirements for a 2- to 3-4sec cycle\ntime, word decoding should not exceed 0.5 \xc2\xb5sec. The main emphasis in the design\nof the integrated-circuit selection-drive system was to minimize power consumption. The system has been designed, breadboarded, partially integrated, and\nevaluated.\nIn commercial practice, circuits incorporating n-p-n bipolar transistors\nand transformers have been used to generate bi-directional current pulses. The\nquiescent power dissipation exhibited by such circuits is generally negligible.\nSince no transformer-coupled drive scheme lends itself to integrated-circuit\n(I-C) fabrication, other techniques were investigated. Circuits incorporating\nonly n-p-n bipolar transistors as active components consume an excessive amount\nof power. Strips of MOS switches for driving laminated ferrites have been\npreviously fabricated (Refs. 4, 5). However, the factory yield for these\nstructures that operate at relatively high current levels was expected to be\npoor. Although complementary bipolar circuits have been designed to provide\nbi-directional word drive current pulses, there is still some difficulty in\nfabricating both high-quality n-p-n and p-n-p devices on a common substrate.\nThe approach pursued for meeting the requirements of this program incorporates\nn-p-n bipolar transistors and p-channel MOS enhancement devices (BIMOS). This\ntechnique is compatible with present I-C technology.\n\nB. Introduction\nA sense-digit regeneration loop was designed, and portions of the system\nwere breadboarded and tested. The functions of 010 regeneration loop are to\ndetect and write, or regenerate, information into each of the 200 bits of a\nselected word. Information is detected by an ac-coupled, differential-input,\nsense amplifier. During the write tame, the digit driver feeds a current pulse\nto one of two digit lines, coinciding with the write pulse at one of the two\ncrossovers of a memory bit. The digit driver consists of two identical current\ndrivers under the control of the taming generator and a flip-,flop in the memory\ndata register.\nIdeally in a system, there would be a single sense amplifier, digit driver,\nand flip-flop for each bit of the word line. This would require the digit\ndriver to feed 65,536 bits; the line delay time would be excessive at that\nlength, as described in Section II. A digit/sense segment of 4096 bits, or\nfour of the 64 planes, is a reasonable compromise between circuit complexity\nand cycle time. In addition, MOS devices are\nincorporated to multiplex the low level signals into a single sense amplifier.\nTo minimize digit line delay time, the multiplex transistors are connected to\nthe midpoints of the digit lines.
