This fact in itself was a driving reason to\nstart follow-on studies to assure that an expanded supersonic transport (SST) technology base would be available to proceed with the development of an advanced supersonic transport at a future date. In addition, the follow-on programs capitalized on\nthe government funding already invested in SST technology and the Industrial Research\nand Development (IRAD) funds expended by Boeing, kept the design teams active, and\nperformed research on major unsolved technology problems.\nThe defunct U.S. SST program had its beginnings in research conducted in the\nlaboratories of NACA/NASA. NASA configurations were part of the mix of options\navailable to Boeing, and a NASA configuration (SCAT-15F) was the closest contender to\nthe finally selected prototype design (Boeing 2707-300). However, technological\nproblems existed which directly related to factors which terminated the SST program\nin 1971; for example, concern for engine noise levels; upper atmospheric pollution;\nflutter characteristics; requirement for stability augmentation systems not within\nthe state of the art; sonic boom characteristics; and low range/payload characteristics due to excessive structural weight fraction, high specific fuel consumption, a\nmarginal configuration concept with insufficient lift-drag ratio, and economics.\nThe Supersonic Cruise Research (SCR) Program was initiated in fiscal year 1973\nby the National Aeronautics and Space Administration at the direct request of the\nExecutive Office of the President and Congress following termination of the U.S. SST\nprogram. Originally, the program was entitled Advanced Supersonic Technology (AST);\nthis was later changed to Supersonic Cruise Aircraft Research (SCAR) and, finally, to\nSCR.\nThe SCAR Program was a focused Research and Technology (R&T) effort.\nall objectives may be summarized as follows:\n\nThe over-\n\nI. To provide an expanded technology base for future civil and military\nsupersonic aircraft\n2. To provide the data needed to assess environmental and economic impacts on\nthe United States of present and future supersonic transport aircraft\n3. To define the potential benefits and trade-offs of advancements in\naerodynamic efficiency, materials and structures, propulsion systems, and\nstability and control methods applied to promising advanced supersonic\ncruise aircraft concepts\nThis program included System Studies (SS) and the following disciplines:\nPropulsion (P)\nStratospheric emissions impact (SEI)\nMaterials and structures (M&S)\nAerodynamic performance (A)\nStability and control (S&C)\n\nThe SCR Program was managed by the Office of Aeronautics and Space Technology\n(OAST), Aeronautical Systems Division, with Langley Research Center designated as the\nlead center. The Advanced Supersonic Technology (AST) Office was established at\nLangley for technical management and coordination of the program. Ames Research\nCenter, Lewis Research Center, Dryden Flight Research Center, and Jet Propulsion\nLaboratory, in addition to Langley Research Center, implemented the program through\ncontracts with the aircraft industry, research grants to universities, and in-house\nexperimental and analytical work.\nThe NASA/Lewis Variable-Cycle Engine (VCE) Component Program was initiated in\nfiscal year 1976 to augment the overall SCR technology effort in the area of propulsion. Phase I provided for the initial development and evaluation of certain critical component technologies unique to VCE concepts for a supersonic cruise aircraft.\nVCE Phase II Technology Program was intended to build upon the results of the VCE\nComponent Test Program and allow the evaluation of component/system performance and\nenvironmental characteristics of the General Electric Double-Bypass Engine (DBE), the\nPratt & Whitney Variable-Stream Control Engine (VSCE), and the Turbine Bypass Engine\n(TBE). Phase II was restructured, beginning in mid fiscal year 1981, as the result\nof the elimination of funding for fiscal year 1982 and beyond. Large-scale test-bed\nengine activities were phased out and model nozzle testing and anlytical flow-field\nprediction code development were emphasized in the revised program. Work was completed with remaining fiscal year 1981 funds.\nThe YF-12 aircraft were utilized in the SCAR Program for demonstration of technological solutions to problems in high-temperature structures, control of engine\nperformance, control for cruise Mach number, and control for altitude.\nA block diagram showing the initial organization of SCR in fiscal year 1973 is\nshown in figure 1(a). As progress was made during the first 7 years, the program\nstructure gradually changed to that shown in figure 1(b). The major disciplines\n(namely, propulsion, materials and structures, aerodynamic performance, and stability\nand control) plus system studies remained essentially the same. Stratospheric emissions impact was absorbed into propulsion, and a new discipline which addressed\npropulsion-system/airframe interaction was initiated in fiscal year 1978. The\nVariable-Cycle Engine Component Program and the flight research experiments shown in\nfigure 1(b) are other OAST programs which provided technology data for the SCR development base. The relative level of effort expended for each discipline from fiscal\nyear 1973 to fiscal year 1981 is presented in figure 2.\nIn order to keep the technical community informed of progress, two conferences\nwere held and the proceedings published as NASA CP-001 (ref. I), and NASA CP-2108\n(ref. 2). The conference presentations only highlight the progress and one must turn\nto the more technical documents for details. All the NASA formal reports, articles,\npresentations, and contractor reports on record at the AST Office at mid-1980 are\nlisted in two bibliographies, NASA RP-1003 (ref. 3) and NASA RP-1063 (ref. 4).\nThe purpose of this report is to document the contracts and grants of both programs in order to provide a subject and cost record which would be valuable in planning follow-on programs. This information may be used with the SCR Program bibliographies (refs. 3 and 4) to delineate the areas studied and the level of effort\nexpended, to avoid duplicaton of effort, and to identify new technological areas for\nstudy. The large scope of this Supersonic\nCruise Aircraft Research Program is evident, and it indicates the Herculean effort in\nplanning, management, and research that was required in order to produce approximately 1000 technical publications consisting of NASA formal reports, NASA contractor\nreports, company reports, and articles.\n\nCONCLUDING REMARKS\nThe Supersonic Cruise Research Program and Variable-Cycle Engine Technology\nProgram were very successful in conducting advanced technology studies for future\nsupersonic commercial and military jet transports. At the close of the program in\n1982, the studies identified technologies for producing an efficient supersonic commercial jet transport which could transport 300 passengers nonstop from Los Angeles\nto Tokyo in about 4 hours. One major problem which has not been solved to date is\n"sonic boom." Future studies, however, should be conducted in order to optimize such\naircraft and to develop transport airplanes for cruising at higher Mach numbers.\nFunds expended by SCR between fiscal year 1973 and fiscal year 1981 on contracts\nand grants were $63,174,000 and $3,015,000, respectively. The SCR net R&D was\n$73,227,000 and total program authority (506 W) was $86,982,000. The corresponding\nlevel of effort for VCE was $27,103,000 for contracts and $750,000 for grants, out of\na net R&D of $31,177,000 (506 W was $31,730,000).\nThe total number of contractors and grantees on record at the AST Office in 1982\nwas 101 for SCR and 4 for VCE.\n\nLangley Research Center\nNational Aeronautics and Space Administration\nHampton, VA 23665\nJune 15, 1983\n\nREFERENCES\nI. Abstract\nThe NASA-SCAR (AST) program was initiated in 1972 at the direct request of the Executive Office of the White House and Congress following termination of the U.S. SST\nprogram.\nThe purpose of SCR was to conduct a focused research and technology program\non those technology problems which contributed to the SST termination and, also, to\nprovide an expanded data base for future civil and military supersonic transport\naircraft. Funding for the Supersonic Cruise Research (SCR) Program was initiated in\nfiscal year 1973 and terminated in fiscal year 1981. The program was implemented\nthrough contracts and grants with industry, universities, and by in-house investigations at the NASA/OAST centers. The studies included system studies and five disciplines: propulsion, stratospheric emissions impact, materials and structures, aerodynamic performance, and stability and control.\nThe NASA/Lewis Variable-Cycle Engine\n(VCE) Component Program was initiated in 1976 to augment the SCR program in the area\nof propulsion.\nAfter about 2 years, the title was changed to VCE Technology Program.\nThe total number of contractors and grantees on record at the AST office in 1982 was\n101 for SCR and 4 for VCE. This paper presents a compilation of all the contracts\nand grants as well as the funding summaries for both programs.\n\nf\n\n17. Key Words(Sugg_ted\n\nby Author(s))\n\n18.
