Many land surface types lend themselves to classification in terms of\n"uniform" surfaces. The linear nature of the data within a class of this\ntype Makes the class well suited to discrimination by statistical techniques\nbased on the covariance matrix.The classification can be left at this\nprimary level, or the linearity of the data can be used to classify to greater i\nresolution within the class.\nHowever those surfaces, noteably most types of vegetative cover, which\ncould not be considered to be "uniform" surfaces will need to be classified\nby methods taking into account the subtle and dynamic\xe2\x80\x94like changes that\noccur across these surfaces. To understand, and model the relationship\nbetween factors influencing spectral reflectance, and the reflectance, will\nrequire accurate, reliable and sufficient field data, that can be related to\nthe imagery. Given this Information,then as great a resolution in \t\nclassification of these surfaces can be achieved as with those classified\nusing the "uniform" surface approach.\n\ni\n\nF\n\nf,\n1,\t\n\nF.SRO Contractor Report, "Hultispectral Scanning Systems and\npotential application to Earth-Resource Surveys"\n\nbys\t\nZ.\t\n\ntheir\n\nA.D.
