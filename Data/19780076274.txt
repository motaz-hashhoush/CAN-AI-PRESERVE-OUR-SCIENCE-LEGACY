LAUNCH GEOS-2 SATELLITE FOR ESA\n\nThe second Geostationary Scientific Satellite (GEOS-2)\nis scheduled for launching by NASA for the European Space\nAgency (ESA) from Cape Canaveral, Fla., no earlier than\nJuly 14.\n\nThe planned launching time is between 6:43 and\n\n7:49 a.m. EOT.\n\nUnder an agreement between the United States and ESA,\nthe satellite will be launched by the Delta 2914 launch\nvehicle which is managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight\nCenter, Greenbelt, Md.\n\nESA will reimburse NASA for cost\n\nof the Delta launch vehicle, launch services and other\nadministrative costs totaling $16.5 million.\n-more-\n\nMailed:\nJune 26, 1978\n\n-2The GEOS-2 launching will mark the 143rd flight mission\nfor Delta.\n\nOf the previous 142 launchings, 131 were suc-\n\ncessful for a better than 90 per cent launch record.\n\nGEOS-2 will study the magnetosphere, that region of\nnear-Earth space where the magnetic field of the Earth still\nplays a dominating role.\n\nThe magnetosphere prevents direct\n\naccess of energetic solar particles to the Earth and thus\nprotects our planet from the harmful effects of space\nradiation.\n\nThe satellite's position above the equator will be\nshifted between 9 degrees and 35 degrees east longitude\nduring the first year of operation to maximize the correlation of satellite data with ground station, balloon and\nsounding rocket measurements made on the same magnetic\nlongitude.\n\nGEOS-2 will carry out the mission originally conceived\nfor GEOS-1 which did not achieve a proper orbit April 20,\n1977, due to a premature separation of the Delta second and\nthird stages caused by a failure of the separation bolts.\nDifferent bolts with greater reliability are now used.\n\n-more-\n\n-3-\n\nThe GEOS-1 spacecraft was placed in a 12-hour elliptical\norbit by the European Space Operations Centre at Darmstadt,\nWest Germany.\n\nThus the GEOS-1 mission was modified to\n\nachieve some scientific return.\n\nThe cylindrically-shaped GEOS-2 satellite will weigh\n573 kilograms (1,263 pounds) at liftoff (including experiments, apogee boost motor and propellant).\n\nIt has been de-\n\nsigned for a two-year lifetime.\n\nExcept for minor modifications to certain spacecraft\nsubsystems, the seven GEOS-2 experiments are the same as for\nGEOS-1.\n\nThey were provided by 11 research institutes in\n\neight ESA member countries (Denmark, France, Germany, Italy,\nNetherlands, Sweden, Switzerland and the United Kingdom).\n\nThe European Space Operations Centre at Darmstadt \xe2\x80\x94\nabout 35 km (22 mi.) northwest of the Odenwald ground\nstation \xe2\x80\x94 will be-responsible for all orbital operations\nof GEOS-2.\n\nThis includes the firing of the satellite's\n\nsolid propellant motor approximately 36 hours after liftoff,\nplacing the satellite into its final stationary orbit 35,750\nkm (22,300 mi.) above the equator and the African continent.\n\n-more-\n\n-4-\n\nThe Delta Program is managed by the Goddard Center\nfor NASA's Office of Space Transportation Systems.\n\nLaunch\n\noperations are the responsibility of NASA's Kennedy Space\nCenter Expendable Vehicles Directorate.\n\nPrime contractor\n\nfor the Delta launch vehicle is McDonnell Douglas Astronautics Co. of Huntington Beach, Calif.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE.\n\nBACKGROUND INFORMATION
