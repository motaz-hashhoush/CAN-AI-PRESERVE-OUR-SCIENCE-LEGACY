This article applies nonstandard analysis to derive jump conditions for one-dimensional, converging shock waves in a\ncompressible, inviscid, perfect gas. It is assumed that the shock thickness occurs on an in\xef\xac\x81nitesimal interval and the jump functions in the\nthermodynamic and \xef\xac\x82uid dynamic parameters occur smoothly across\n\n1\n\nthis interval. The lack\nof such a comprehensive theory means that each type of physical problem,\nand hence, each type of equation, requires a di\xef\xac\x80erent analytical or numerical\ntechnique for its solution. Functional analysis has been applied to develop\npowerful techniques for analyzing and constructing generalized solutions to\nfamilies of linear partial di\xef\xac\x80erential equations. However, the methods of\nfunctional analysis are limited in studying nonlinear partial di\xef\xac\x80erential equations, because such techniques are based on linear mathematical structures.\nSince linear mathematical structures do not describe the simplest nonlinearity (multiplication), it is not surprising that functional analysis has not\nyielded a comprehensive framework for studying generalized solutions of par2\n\ntial di\xef\xac\x80erential equations.\nMany approaches have been used to attempt to de\xef\xac\x81ne a useful multiplication operation on spaces of functionals applied in linear analysis to study differential equations. The motivation for multiplying functionals comes from a\nplethora of applications that occur in modeling physical problems: construction of generalized solutions of certain partial di\xef\xac\x80erential equations requires\nthe multiplication of linear functionals. However, a fundamental theorem of\nSchwartz [1] shows that the standard algebraic product cannot be de\xef\xac\x81ned\nconsistently in spaces of functionals, such as the distributions or generalized\nfunctions; all such methods will produce ambiguous results leading to logical and arithmetical contradictions. Because standard multiplication does\nnot produce meaningful results, any product of generalized functions de\xef\xac\x81ned\nmust be weaker than standard multiplication.\nOver the last thirty years, E.E. Rosinger [2 - 6] and J.F. Colombeau [7,\n8], working independently, have developed a theoretical framework for quotient algebras of generalized functions to analyze nonlinear partial di\xef\xac\x80erential\nequations. Their approach extends classical linear mathematics to nonlinear\nmathematics by de\xef\xac\x81ning multiplication on sets of generalized functions by\nembedding these linear spaces into abstract algebras of nonlinear generalized\n\n3\n\nfunctions with weak operations of multiplication. The potential application of a theory of nonlinear generalized functions enabling the analysis and\napproximation of solutions to nonlinear partial di\xef\xac\x80erential equations is enormous; such a theory could impact almost all areas of science and technology.\nAlthough it is probable that the existing theories of quotient algebras of\ngeneralized functions will yield important insights into the analysis and computation of certain classes of nonlinear \xef\xac\x81eld equations, signi\xef\xac\x81cant limitations\nof these theories are known. Because any logically consistent operation of\nmultiplication of generalized functions must be much weaker than the classical multiplication of numbers and functions, such a product of generalized\nfunctions will always yield large collections of abstract objects representing\nweak solutions of the problem under investigation. Basically problems with\nno classical weak solutions, such as the product, \xce\xb4 \xc2\xb7 H, are converted into\nproblems with uncountably many generalized solutions. The fundamental\nquestion then becomes how to determine a physically observed weak solution from an in\xef\xac\x81nitude of possible weak solutions.\nTo approximate solutions, Colombeau (see References [7, 8]) constructed\nan explicit theory of nonlinear generalized functions. This theory has been\napplied to analyze and compute solutions to nonlinear partial di\xef\xac\x80erential\n\n4\n\nequations. The logic of solving a di\xef\xac\x80erential equation in a space of Colombeau\ngeneralized functions follows the standard approach of functional analysis:\na method is established for generating sequences to approximate solutions\nwithin a collection of nonlinear generalized functions and then a compactnesstype argument is applied to show that the approximating sequences actually\nconverge to limits within the space of approximating objects. A resulting\nlimit then represents a solution to the equation. A key di\xef\xac\x83culty in applying\na space of Colombeau generalized functions is determining a speci\xef\xac\x81c representation for the products of generalized functions occurring in the di\xef\xac\x80erential\nequation. As Colombeau [9] and his coworkers [10, 11] have shown, di\xef\xac\x80erent physical problems require di\xef\xac\x80erent distinct representations for a product\nof generalized functions such as \xce\xb4 \xc2\xb7 H. The representation of the product is\nfound by adding more information about the problem. Roughly speaking, the\nsolution of a concrete problem via nonlinear generalized functions requires:\nthe equations modeling the physics, the initial-boundary values, and the additional problem-speci\xef\xac\x81c physical information required to \xef\xac\x81x the product of\ngeneralized functions. For example, in continuum shock wave problems, the\nadditional information is obtained by requiring the product of generalized\nfunctions to satisfy the standard shock wave jump conditions.\n\n5\n\nA number of authors, including, Oberguggenberger and Todorov [12], and\nHoskins and Sousa Pinto [13], have shown that versions of the Colombeau algebras of nonlinear generalized functions may be constructed using nonstandard analysis. Nonstandard analysis is a relatively new area of mathematics,\nwhich emerged from basic research in mathematical logic. The subject was\ndiscovered in the early 1960s by A. Robinson [14]. The main contribution\nof nonstandard analysis to mathematics is the extension of the real numbers\nR to the hyperreal numbers \xe2\x88\x97 R that contain in\xef\xac\x81nitely small (in\xef\xac\x81nitesimals)\nand in\xef\xac\x81nitely large numbers. The in\xef\xac\x81nitesimals in a hyperreal number system have the algebraic properties of standard numbers, which justi\xef\xac\x81es the\nformal manipulations of in\xef\xac\x81nitesimals that engineers and physicists often\nuse. Moreover, the existence of distinct, in\xef\xac\x81nitely large numbers that can\nbe manipulated and used like \xef\xac\x81nite numbers to solve problems provides a\npowerful new tool for applications. Nonstandard methods may be used to\nconstruct algebras of nonlinear generalized functions, or nonstandard analysis may be used directly to study speci\xef\xac\x81c problems involving multiplication\nof generalized functions. The second approach is considered here.\nIn this article, jump conditions are derived using nonstandard analysis for\none-dimensional converging shock waves in a compressible, inviscid, perfect\n\n6\n\ngas. A shock wave is governed by the\nstandard conservation equations written in primitive form. Predistributions of the Heaviside functions were introduced and used to model\nthe microstructure of the \xef\xac\x82ow \xef\xac\x81eld across a shock wave governed by the classical conservation equations. For \xef\xac\x82ow variables with their jumps de\xef\xac\x81ned on\nthe same in\xef\xac\x81nitesimal interval, it was shown that the equations of motion\nwritten in nonconservative form produced unambiguous relations between\nthe various nonstandard Heaviside functions. Because inviscid \xef\xac\x82ow was assumed, the analysis only produced the relations between the \xef\xac\x81eld variable\nHeaviside functions.\nThe present work was motivated by the authors\xe2\x80\x99 interest in understanding\nhow to add physical information to specify products of generalized functions\ncontained in di\xef\xac\x80erential equations modeling physical problems with well de\xef\xac\x81ned regions of solution discontinuity. The main conclusions of the study are\n\n44\n\nas follows:\n1. Nonstandard analysis may be applied to identify predistributions of\nthe Heaviside function de\xef\xac\x81ned on in\xef\xac\x81nitesimal intervals that reproduce\nthe standard jump function results from the theory of generalized functions. The predistributions considered here are piecewise di\xef\xac\x80erentiable\nelements of the function space \xe2\x88\x97 Lloc (R).\n2. Nonstandard asymptotic arguments were used to simplify the derivation of the shock wave jump functions.\n3. The relationships between the internal Heaviside functions for one dimensional, converging shock waves in an inviscid, perfect gas were derived unambiguously from the equations of motion in nonconservative\nform.
