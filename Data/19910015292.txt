Without any design modification,\nthe momentum\ngenerated by the driver caused the target to bounce back to its\nstarting position.\nInitially, elastomeric\ndampers were used between the driver\nand the target. However, this design could not prevent the bounce, and it\ncompromised\nthe positional accuracy of the calibration\ntarget. A design that\nsuccessfully\nmet all the requirements\nincorporated\na sealed pocket 85 percent\nfull of 0.76-mm diameter stainless steel balls in the back of the target to provide\nthe effect of a "dead-blow"\nhammer.\nThe energy dissipation\nresulting from the\ncollision of balls in the pocket successfully\ndampened\nthe excess momentum\ngenerated during the target deployment.\nThis paper describes the disastrous\neffects of new requirements\non a design with a successful flight history, the\nmodifications\nthat were necessary to make the device work, and the tests\nperformed\nto verify its functionality.\n\nINTRODUCTION\nThe Pressure Modulator Infrared Radiometer\n(PMIRR) is a nine-channel\nlimb and nadir scanning instrument\non the Mars Observer Spacecraft,\ndesigned\nto study the geosciences\nand climatology\nof Mars. It achieves high radiometric\nprecision by means of a two-point calibration\ncycle. The first calibration\nsource\nis an external, fiat aluminum target disk that views cold space. The bounce problem occurs when\nthe momentum\ngenerated by the arm and the target during actuation overcomes\nthe latching force of the magnet.\nIn addition to generating\ngreater momentum,\ndriving a larger mass has the compounded\neffect of lengthening\nthe actuation\ntime. Because the voltage from the capacitor decays quickly after a slow\nactuation, the coil provides little holding force to assist the magnet in\novercoming\nthe arm/target rebound at the end of travel. With a target mass less\nthan 1.5 grams, the bounce merely lengthened\nthe settling time; the Drive Arm\nalways ended up where it was supposed to go. However, with a target mass\ngreater than 1.5 grams, the drive arm would bounce overcenter\nto its starting\nposition.\n\n33\n\nThe torque effects of the magnetic field on the Drive Arm were\ncharacterized\nusing a force transducer\n(Figure 6). While running a constant\nvoltage from a power supply through the coil, the torque on the Drive Arm was\nmeasured at different arm positions.\nThe effects of the cabling were\ncharacterized\nusing the same method without the magnet at the Drive Arm pivot\npoint. It was found that, for coil voltages less than or equal to 5 volts, the\nmagnitude\nof the torque from the cabling is greater than the torque generated by\nthe coil.\n\nDESIGN\nMore Powerful\n\nMODIFICATIONS\n\nDrive Electronics\n\nThe initial drive electronics\ninitially provided a 33-millisecond\ndischarge\nfrom a 660-microfarad\ncapacitor charged to 50 volts.
