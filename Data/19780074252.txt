VEHICLE\nThe Atlas Centaur is NASA\'s standard launch vehicle for\nintermediate weight payloads. It is used for the launch of\nEarth orbital, Earth synchronous and interplanetary missions.\nCentaur was the nation\'s first high-energy, liquidhydrogen/liquid-oxyqen propelled rocket. Developed and\nlaunched under the direction of NASA\'s Lewis Research Center,\nit became operational in 1966 with the launch of Surveyor 1,\n.the first U.S. spacecraft to, soft-land on the Moon\'s surface.\nSince that time, both the Atlas booster and Centaur\nsecond stage have undergone many improvements. At present,\nthe vehicle combination can place, 4,536 kg,(10,000 Ib.) in\nlow Earth orbit, 1,882 kg (4,150 Ib.) in a synchronous transfer orbit and 907 kg (2,000 Ib.) on an interplanetary\ntrajectory.\nThe Atlas Centaur, standing approximately 40.8 m ( 1 4\n.3\nft.) high, consists of an Atlas SLV-3D booster and Centaur\n\' D-1AR second stage. The Atlas booster develops 1,920 kilo,, newtons (431,300 Ib.) of thrust at liftoff using two 822,920newton (185,000-lb.) thrust booster engines, one 266,890-N\n(60,000-lb.) thrust sustainer engine and two vernier engines\ndeveloping 2,890 N (650 Ib.) thrust each. The two RL-10\nengines on Centaur produce a total of 133,450 N (30,000 Ib.)\nthrust. Both the Atlas and the Centaur are 3.048 m (10 ft.)\ni n diameter.\n, , , . , .\nUntil early 1974, Centaur was used exclusively in combination with the Atlas booster/ It was subsequently used\nwith a Titan III booster to^launch heavier payloads, into\nEarth orbit and interplanetary trajectories.\nThe Atlas and the Centaur vehicles have been updated\nover the years. Thrust of the Atlas engines has been increased about 222,400 N (50,000 Ib.) since its debut in the\nspace program in the early 1960s.\nThe Centaur D1AR has an integrated electronic system\nthat performs a major role in checking itself and other\nvehicle systems before launch and also maintains control of\nmajor events after liftoff. The new Centaur system handles\nnavigation and guidance tasks, controls pressurization and\nventing, propellant management, telemetry formats and transmission and initiates vehicle events. Most operational needs\ncan be met by changing, the computer software.\n-more-\n\n-5-\n\nTYPICAL LAUNCH VEHICLE CHARACTERISTICS\nLiftoff weight including spacecrafts\nLiftoff height:\n\n40.8 m (134 ft.)\n\nLaunch Complex:\n\n148,060 kg\nC326,419 Ib.)\n\n36B\n\nAtlas Booster\n\nCentaur Stage\n\nWeight (with\npropellants)\n\n13Q,317 kg\n(287,300 Ib.)\n\n17,781 kg\n(39,200 Ib.)\n\nHeight\n\n21.3 m (70 ft.)\n\n19.5 m (64 ft.)\nwith payload - 4\nfairing\n\nThrust\n\n1,919 kn\n(431,300 Ib.)\nat sea level\n\n133,447 N\n(30,000 Ib.)\nin vacuum\n\nPropellants\n\nLiquid oxygen\nand RP-1\n\nLiquid oxygen\nLiquid hydrogen\n\nPropulsion\n\nMA-5 system two\n822,921-N (185,000\nIb.) thrust booster\nengines, one\n266,893-N (60,000 Ib.)\nthrust sustainer\nengine, two 2,891-N\n(650-lb.) thrust\nvernier engines.\n\nTwo 66,723-N\n(15,000-lb.)\nthrust RL-10\nengines, 12\nhydrogen peroxide\nthrusters.\n\nVelocity\n\n9,205 km/hr (5,720\nmph) at booster\nengine cutoff (BECO)\n13,061 km/hr (8,116\nmph) at sustainer\nengine cutoff (SECO),~\n\n33,345 km/hr\n(20,720 mph) at\nspacecraft separation,\n\nGuidance\n\nPreprogrammed profile\nthrough BECO, switch\nto inertial guidance\nfor sustainer phase.\n\nInertial guidance\n\n-more-\n\n-6IiAUNCH OPERATIONS\n\nThe Atlas Centaur stages of the Atlas Centaur 48\nlaunch vehicle arrived at Cape Canaveral Air Force Station\nJan. 11, 1978, and were erected on Pad Bf Complex 36,\nJan. 25-26.
