Packard\xe2\x80\xa1\nNASA Glenn Research Center, Cleveland, OH, 44135\n\nA crewed mission to Mars poses a signi\xef\xac\x81cant challenge in dealing with the physiological issues that arise with the crew being exposed to a near zero-gravity environment as\nwell as signi\xef\xac\x81cant solar and galactic radiation for such a long duration. While long surface stay missions exceeding 500 days are the ultimate goal for human Mars exploration,\nshort round trip, short surface stay missions could be an important intermediate step that\nwould allow NASA to demonstrate technology as well as study the physiological e\xef\xac\x80ects\non the crew. However, for a 1-year round trip mission, the outbound and inbound hyperbolic velocity at Earth and Mars can be very large resulting in a signi\xef\xac\x81cant propellant\nrequirement for a high thrust system like Nuclear Thermal Propulsion (NTP). Similarly,\na low thrust Nuclear Electric Propulsion (NEP) system requires high electrical power levels (10 megawatts electric (M We ) or more), plus advanced power conversion technology\nto achieve the lower speci\xef\xac\x81c mass values needed for such a mission. A Bimodal Nuclear\nThermal Electric Propulsion (BNTEP) system is examined here that uses three high thrust\nBimodal Nuclear Thermal Rocket (BNTR) engines allowing short departure and capture\nmaneuvers. The engines also generate electrical power that drives a low thrust Electric\nPropulsion (EP) system used for e\xef\xac\x83cient interplanetary transit. This combined system\ncan help reduce the total launch mass, system and operational requirements that would\notherwise be required for equivalent NEP or Solar Electric Propulsion (SEP) mission. The\nBNTEP system is a hybrid propulsion concept where the BNTR reactors operate in two\nseparate modes. During high-thrust mode operation, each BNTR provides 10\xe2\x80\x99s of kiloNewtons of thrust at reasonably high speci\xef\xac\x81c impulse (Isp ) of 900 seconds for impulsive\ntrans-planetary injection and orbital insertion maneuvers. When in power generation /\nEP mode, the BNTR reactors are coupled to a Brayton power conversion system allowing\neach reactor to generate 100\xe2\x80\x99s of kWe of electrical power to a very high Isp (3000 s) EP\nthruster system for sustained vehicle acceleration and deceleration in heliocentric space.\n\nNomenclature\nIsp\ng\nV\xe2\x88\x9e\nC3\n\xe2\x88\x86V\nT\n/W\nBN T EP\nBN T proton events and galactic cosmic radiation\nduring interplanetary transit and solar proton event e\xef\xac\x80ects during close perihelion passage are well outside\ncurrent permissible exposure limits.1 Limiting total mission time to 1-year can reduce interplanetary time\nby 75-300 days, signi\xef\xac\x81cantly reducing exposure time.\nThe analysis presented in this paper covers a detailed trajectory design and mission architecture for a\n2033 one-year round trip, 1 M We -class BNTEP mission with an outbound transit time of 126 days, a 30\nday stay in a 24 hour elliptical Mars orbit and an inbound transfer time of 209 days with direct re-entry at\nEarth by the crewed MPCV. The dual-mode BNTEP vehicle enables a short round trip mission by reducing\nthe use of the high thrust BNTR engines. Having an initial mass in LEO of 389.9 mt, this mission requires\na total of 5 SLS Block-IB launches to deliver all the vehicle components for assembly in LEO. This is on-par\nwith the stay time and IMLEO for N T R opposition-class mission and accomplishes the mission in 365 days\ninstead of 400-600 days. Key Components of BNTEP Mars Transfer Vehicle\n\n2 of 16\nAmerican Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics\n\nII.\nII.A.\n\n2033 1-Year Earth-Mars Roundtrip BNTEP Mission\n\nMission De\xef\xac\x81nition\n\nA 1-year mission during the 2033 opportunity requires a lower combined BNTR and low-thrust \xe2\x88\x86V budget\nthan other opportunities in the 2031-2041 time frame (Appendix A). Although 2033 is not the preferred\nmission opportunity for ballistic transfer NTR missions due to the high launch asymptote declination, the\ncompressed mission timeline causes the launch date to move later than the ballistic optimal and out of the\nhigher declination region. Plane change to adjust for declinations beyond 28.5\xe2\x97\xa6 is performed during the\nlow-thrust burns during the outbound transit.\nThis mission uses the BNTR engines in high-thrust mode to depart Earth on May 6, 2033. Following\ninjection into the hyperbolic Earth escape trajectory, the BNTR engines are then powered down to powergeneration mode to complete low-thrust EP burns during transit. The BNTR engines are powered up again\njust prior to Mars arrival for a high-thrust burn to injection into a 24 hr Mars orbit on September 8, 2033.\nThe MTV stays in orbit around Mars for 30 days at which point the BNTR engines perform the last highthrust burn to escape Mars. During the return leg of the trajectory the BNTR engines are switched to\npower-generation mode again to complete the return low-thrust EP burns. Upon arrival at Earth on May\n6, 2034, the crew boards the MPCV, separates from the MTV and performs a direct re-entry into Earth\xe2\x80\x99s\natmosphere. Over the course of the mission, the total time the BNTR engines are operating in high thrust\nmode is 1.75 hours.\nII.B.\n\nMission Design Analytical Methods\n\nThe trajectory optimization program COPERNICUS2 was used to model and perform analysis for the 1-year\nBNTEP missions. Impulsive burns were used to model the BNTR propulsive maneuvers and \xef\xac\x81nite burns\nwere used to model the longer EP burns. Trajectories were optimized for the 2031-2041 opportunities. Earth\ndeparture and arrival dates were optimized parameters but the total time of \xef\xac\x82ight was constrained to be\nno longer than 365 days. The MTV begins in an 407 km circular LEO orbit inclined at 28.5\xe2\x97\xa6 . The BNTR\nengines perform TMI to put the MTV into a hyperbolic Earth-Mars transfer orbit. A minimum half-day\ncoast is constrained to occur before and after each BNTR burn to allow for targeting and checkout operations. Following TMI and a half-day checkout period, the optimization is allowed to initiate the \xef\xac\x81rst EP\nburn. This burn usually occurs as early as possible in the transfer trajectory in order to reduce the burden\non the BNTR system to provide all the energy required for Earth departure. A coast period separates this\n\xef\xac\x81rst EP burn from a second EP burn that occurs prior to Mars arrival. The second EP burn is terminated\nno later than a half-day prior to MOI. The second EP burn is used to slow down the MTV to reduce the\nMOI \xe2\x88\x86V . MOI is performed using the BNTR engines to insert the MTV into a 300 km x 24 hr Mars orbit.\nThe stay time at Mars is constrained to be a minimum of 30 days. Due to the geometry of this mission,\nstay time never extends past the 30 day lower bound. To depart Mars, the BNTR engines perform the\nTEI burn to put the MTV into a hyperbolic Mars-Earth transfer orbit. After a half-day checkout coast\nfollowing TEI the optimization is allowed to initiate the third EP burn. A coast period separates this third\nEP burn from a fourth EP burn. Typically the fourth EP burn is removed, as it is deemed unnecessary by\nthe optimizer. Unlike the other EP burns which occur as close to departure/arrival as possible, the fourth\nEP burn, if performed, occurs near the mid-point of the inbound trajectory. Since the MTV employs a direct\nentry at Earth rather than EOI to capture into an Earth orbit, the optimization uses the fourth EP burn to\nshape/correct the trajectory to return to Earth within 365 days.\nSimilar to ballistic Earth-Mars trajectories, optimal BNTEP Earth-Mars trajectories can have outbound\nlegs that are either Type-I or Type-II trajectories. Type-I trajectories are characterized as having shorter\ntrip times while Type-II trajectories are characterized as having longer trip times. Type-I and Type-II\ntrajectories are identi\xef\xac\x81ed as having heliocentric travel angles less than or greater than 180\xe2\x97\xa6 respectively.\nWhile 2031, 2033 and 2035 are more optimal with Type-I outbound transfers, 2037, 2039 and 2041 are more\noptimal having a Type-II outbound trajectory leg. For this analysis, only the optimal trajectory type was\nanalyzed for each opportunity.\n\n3 of 16\nAmerican Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics\n\nFigure 2. COPERNICUS is a 3-Degrees of Freedom spacecraft trajectory design and optimization program\nthat was originally developed at the University of Texas with recent developments and maintenance being\ndone by Johnson Space Center.2 Trajectory models are built as a series of segments propagated in either\na point-mass or higher order gravitational \xef\xac\x81eld. The segments can be connected and constrained with the\naddition of linear or non-linear constraints. COPERNICUS has the capability to model both low thrust\nburns as well as high thrust burns as either impulsively or with \xef\xac\x81nite maneuvers for propulsion systems\nwhich can be based on BNTR, solar electric, or nuclear powered engines. TMI is separated into two\nburns in order to reduce gravity losses as well as to take advantage of the drop tank design of the MTV. In\norder to reduce the inert mass that needs to be accelerated throughout the mission, the MTV jettisons the\n6 of 16\nAmerican Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics\n\ndepleted LH2 drop tanks following each of the TMI burns. Likewise, the second\npair of LH2 drop tanks are jettisoned following TMI-2 in order to further reduce the inert mass carried\nthroughout the remainder of the mission. Jettison of Drop Tanks from Star Truss following TMI Burn\n\nAt the start of the in-transit phase to Mars, the BNTR engines are powered down from 545 M Wt (for\nthe NTR/thermal propulsion mode) to 1.76 M Wt (for the EP/power generation mode). Energy generated\nin the reactor fuel assemblies is removed using a secondary closed gas loop that carries a helium-xenon\n(He-Xe) gas mixture. In a BNTR design derived from the Rover/NERVA technology program,3 the existing regenerative-cooled core support tie tubes would carry recirculated He/Xe coolant gas. Power from\nthe NERVA fuel elements surrounding the tie tubes enters the tubes via conduction. In the fast reactor\nBNTR design called ESCORT,4 a closed loop, coaxial energy transport duct, integrated into each U O2 -W\nfuel element, carries the He-Xe coolant gas. The heated gas is then routed to a high power (500 kWe ) BRU\nconsisting of a Turbine-Alternator-Compressor (TAC) assembly that generates electricity at approximately\n20% energy conversion e\xef\xac\x83ciency to power the EP system and spacecraft systems. Waste heat is rejected to\nspace using a pumped-loop radiator.5 A 36.7 day low thrust EP burn follows the half-day forced coast after\nTMI. This burn is used to add additional energy to the MTV such that the high thrust BNTR engines do\nnot need to provide the full departure energy that would be necessary for a true ballistic transfer. A 44 day\ncoast period separates this \xef\xac\x81rst EP burn from a second 43.8 day low thrust EP burn then ends a half-day\nprior to MOI.\nFollowing completion of the last EP burn just prior to Mars arrival, the power level and fuel temperature\nof the BNTR engines are increased to full capability for the high thrust/high Isp 2-perigee burns required\nto propulsively capture into a 24 hr elliptical Mars parking orbit on September 9, 2033. At this point the\ncrew will be captured in a 300 km x 24 hr Mars orbit for 30 days.\nThe BNTR engines are used again to perform the 2-perigee burn TEI maneuver which injects the MTV\ninto a Mars-Earth hyperbolic transfer trajectory on October 9, 2033. After a successful TEI, the BNTR\nengines are again powered down to 1.76 MW for a 80.8 day EP burn occurring a half-day after TEI completion. A 127.7 day coast returns the MTV to Earth and aligns for a direct re-entry. V\xe2\x88\x9e at Earth was\nconstrained to be less than 11.5 km/s. The optimization eliminated the need for a \xef\xac\x81nal EP burn near Earth\non the return leg of the trajectory. One may be added if a case arises where Earth arrival V\xe2\x88\x9e exceeds 11.5\nkm/s or when the trajectory needs to be corrected part way through the return coast to align properly to\nintercept Earth. Once the MTV has reached the Earth entry interface point, the crew boards the MPCV\nand separates from the MTV. EP \xe2\x88\x86V s vs Total Mission Time of Flight for 2033 Mission Opportunity\n\nIII.\n\nConclusion\n\nBallistic opposition-class (short stay) Mars round trip missions are characterized by long in-space durations, shorter surface stays, and high \xe2\x88\x86V requirements. Because of this, architectures for crewed Mars\nmissions, including Mars DRA 5.0, often select a conjunction-class (long stay) trajectory. However, the\noverall shorter duration of an opposition-class mission is desirable for reducing risk associated with crew\nbehavioral health and performance as well as medical capabilities.1 Utilizing BNTEP technology for short\nduration Mars missions can reduce the in-space duration as well as the IMLEO for comparable NTR missions.6 BNTEP technology enables a feasible 1-year Mars round trip mission by using the EP system during\ntransits to maintain relatively low escape and arrival energy requirements for the high thrust BNTR engines.\nReducing propulsive requirements for the BNTR engines allows the IMLEO of the MTV to remain relatively\nlow. The analysis presented in this paper shows that the MTV of a 2033 BNTEP 1-year mission would\nhave an IMLEO of 389.9 mt, requiring 5 SLS Block-IB launches to deliver all of the components. Future\ninvestigation into the use of BNTEP technology for conjunction-class and split mission architectures could\npotentially further reduce in-space time and potentially IMLEO for these missions.\nAdditional work in the future could also be done to assess vehicle reusability by capturing the MTV into\na highly elliptic Earth orbit or halo orbit around the Earth-Moon L2 libration point following MPCV and\ncrew separation.
