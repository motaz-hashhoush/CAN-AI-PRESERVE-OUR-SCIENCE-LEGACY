This code was\ndeveloped and exercised extensively during the 60\'s and early 7 0 \' s when much\nwork was being performed in the area of ion propulsion mission analysis. NBODY\nis a multi-purpose trajectory optimization program that includes either a highthrust escape or tangential-thrust spiral escape, an interplanetary trajectory\nwith possible coast times, and either a high-thrust or spiral capture.\nDetalled ephemeris data provide for a possible ten-body perturbation problem.\nOptimization methods incorporated into the code provide for optimum thrust\nangle (angle between the thrust and velocity vectors), central travel angle\n(angle describing the arc traveled from initial to final locations), and on-off\nthrust times for a given trip time to insure maximum payload m a s s .\nThe initial mass was held constant for each power level. For the 300 kW\npower level the initial mass was restricted to one shuttle-equivalent payload\nto low Earth orbit (LEO). With the shuttle lift capability taken to be\n27 270 kg (60 000 lbm), and allowing for 15 percent airborne support equipment\n(ASE) mass, the Initial mass in LEO for the 300 kW power level was assumed to\nbe 23 180 kg. The existence of an orbit transfer vehicle (OTV) to place the\nspacecraft in its initial starting altitude and velocity was assumed based on\nrecent OTV mission models (ref. 15). For the 3 HW power level an initial mass\nwas obtained by adding the power source mass (30 000 kg) to an estimated maximum propulsion system wet mass plus contingency plus an estimate for a sizable\npayload. This initial mass in orbit for the 3 HW power level was assumed to\nbe 150 000 kg. During this phase the Earth was the center of the system with\nthe sun acting as a perturbing body. Table I V summarizes the six different\ninitial conditions used in the study. Two power levels (300 kW and 3 MW) were combined with two propellants (xenon and argon) at four values of specific impulse per propellant\nto provide a total of 16 possible propulsion systems. These were used as\ninputs to six trajectory options (starting positions of nuclear start orbit,\ngeosynchronous-equivalent orbit, and lunar orbit with either local circular or\nlocal escape velocities) for a total of 96 mission scenarios. This number was\nreduced to 60 trajectory analyses and 20 trajectory and propulsion system analyses due to technology constraints and mission similarities.\n8\n\nIncreasing the Isp increased the payload mass delivered to Mars\' orbit.\nThis increase in payload mass was most significant (10 to 25 percent) for the\nmore energetic (higher delta V ) missions such as the spiral from nuclear start\norbit. For the less energetic missions such as the trajectory starting In\nlunar orbit with escape velocity, the payload mass increased less than\n4 percent over the range of specific impulse studied.\nBecause fixed power was delivered to the propulsion module, the total\nthrust generated by the ion propulsion system decreased with increasing specific impulse. Due to this decrease in thrust, the propulsion module needed to\noperate for longer periods of time. These increased thrusting times partly\nnegated the reduction in propellant mass requirement achieved by higher values\nof specific impulse. The lower thrust also resulted in longer total mission\ntimes. Consequently, a trade-off exists between Isp, total trip time, and\npayload mass delivered to Mars\' orbit.\n\nA mass breakdown showed that the power source i s a significant percentage\nof the initial mass, and reductions in this would directly increase payload\nmass. Also, the dry mass of the propulsion system i s not a significant portion\nof the initial mass.\nTotal trip times for the 300 kW power level ranged from 344 days for a\nxenon propulsion system beginning from lunar orbit with escape velocity, to\n770 days for an argon propulsion system beginning from nuclear start orbit with\ncircular velocity. Total trip times for the 3 HW power level ranged from\n356 to 413 days. For the missions starting at local circular velocity, the\ntime of the geocentric escape spiral was as much as 40 percent of the total\nmission time (starting from nuclear start orbit). For a given trajectory\noption (starting altitude and velocity), the total mission time was always\nlonger when using argon propellant.\nPayload masses ranged from 5700 to 12 300 kg for the 300 kW power level,\nand 72 200 to 8 500 kg for the 3 MW power level. For a given trajectory\n1\noption, payload mass was always larger when using argon propellant.\nThe most significant result o f the study was the determination of payload\nmass delivered to Mars\' orbit. Any of the low power missions can deliver payloads that will be required of a manned Mars buildup. Examples of these payloads would be a sample return mission or robotic survey mission. The high\npower cases studied have the capability of delivering a habitat module, power\nunit, or heavy machinery, or a combination of this equipment. A nuclearpowered ion propelled vehicle can play a vital role in mankind\'s first step\ninto the solar system by accomplishing all unmanned missions presently envisioned in support of manned Mars exploration.\n\n9\n\nAppendix A\nThe assumptions used i n calculating projected 50-cm ion thruster performance include:\n(1) The beam current was derived from an empirical equation which predicts\nthe performance of 30-cm ion optics (ref. 19). Abstract\n\nNuclear-powered ion propulsion technology was combined with detailed trajectory\nanalysis to determine propulslon system and trajectory options for an unmanned\ncargo mission to Mars i n support of manned Mars missions. A total of 96 mission\nscenarlos were identified by combining two power levels, two propellants, four\nvalues of specific impulse per propellant, three starting altitudes, and two\nstartlng velocities. Sixty of these scenarios were selected for a detailed trajectory analysis; a complete propulsion system study was then conducted for 20\nof these trajectories. Trip times ranged from 344 days for a xenon propulslon\nsystem operating at 300 kW total power and starting from lunar orbit wlth escape\nvelocity, to 770 days for an argon propulsion system operating at 300 kW total\npower and starting from nuclear start orbit with circular velocity. Trip times\nfor the 3 MW cases studied ranged from 356 to 413 days. Payload masses ranged\nfrom 5700 to 12 300 kg for the 300 kW power level, and from 72 200 to 81 500 kg\nfor the 3 MW power level.\n\n18. Distribution Statement\n\n7.
