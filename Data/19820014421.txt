LAUNCH\nIntelsat V-D, the fourth of a new series of nine international telecommunications satellites owned and operated by the\n105-nation International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat), is scheduled to be launched by the NASA Kennedy\nSpace Center on board an Atlas Centaur launch vehicle no earlier\nthan March 4, 1982,\n\nfrom Cape Canaveral, Fla.\n\nThe three Intelsat\n\nVs were successfully launched by NASA in December 1980, May 1981\nand December\n\n1981.\n\nIntelsat V-D weighs 1,928\n\nkilograms (4,251 pounds) at launch\n\nand has almost double the communications capability of early\nsatellites in the Intelsat series \xe2\x80\x94\ntwo color television channels.\n\n12,000 voice circuits and\n\nIt will be positioned in geosyn-\n\nchronous orbit over the Indian Ocean as the prime Intelsat satellite to provide communications services between Europe, the\nMiddle East and the Far East.\n\nFebruary 26, 1982\n-more-\n\n-2-\n\nIntelsat V satellites are built by the Ford Aerospace and\nCommunications Corp., Palo Alto, Calif., using system\n\ncomponents\n\ndeveloped by firms in the United Kingdom, France, the Federal\nRepublic of Germany, Italy and Japan.\nThe International Telecommunications Satellite Organization\nis headquartered in Washington, D.C.\n\nNASA is reimbursed for all\n\ncosts of the Atlas Centaur and launch services under the provisions of a launch services agreement signed in May last year.\nThe Atlas Centaur (AC-58) will place the Intelsat V-D into\na highly elliptical transfer orbit ranging from 144.8\nkilometers (90 to 22,254 miles) perigee to apogee.\n\nto 35,807\n\nIt is from\n\nthis orbit at apogee that a solid propellant rocket motor\nattached to the satellite will be fired to circularize the orbit\nat geosynchronous altitude over the equator.\n\nAt that altitude,\n\nbecause the speed of the satellite in orbit matches the rotational speed of the Earth, the satellite remains in position over\none spot.\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center, Cleveland, Ohio, has management responsibility for Atlas Centaur development and operation.\nNASA\'s Kennedy Space Center, Fla.,\n\nis assigned vehicle checkout\n\nand launch responsibility once the Atlas Centaur reaches Cape\nCanaveral.\nOverall direction of the NASA expendable launch vehicle program is vested in the Office of Space Transportation Operations\nin Washington,\n\nD.C.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE; BACKGROUND\n-more-\n\nINFORMATION FOLLOWS.)\n\n-3-\n\nATLAS CENTAUR LAUNCH VEHICLE STATISTICS\n\nIntelsat V-D will be launched by the The Atlas stage was erected on the\npad on Oct. 27, the interstage adapter on Oct. 28, and the\nCentaur stage was mated to the vehicle on Oct. 29. The flight\nevents demonstration test, a comprehensive electrical test of the\nlaunch vehicle, was undertaken on Feb. 23.\nIntelsat arrived at Hangar AO at the Cape on Jan. 18. The\nspacecraft\'s electrical systems were tested on Feb. 2, and the\nvehicle\'s three Earth sensors and two solar wings were installed\non Feb. 9. The satellite was moved to Satellite Assembly and Encapsulation Building No. 2 in the KSC Industrial Area on Feb. 14.\nIntelsat underwent additional checkout in SAEF-2 and the apogee\nkick motor was installed on Feb. 16. The satellite was enclosed\nin its protective fairing on Feb. 23, and the spacecraft was\nmated with the launch vehicle on Feb. 24 at the
