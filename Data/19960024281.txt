INTRODUCTION\n\nA. Background\nThe space shuttle was only the first step in achieving\nroutine\naccess to space. Recently,\nthe\nNASA\nMarshall\nSpace\nFlight Center\n(MSFC)\nhas been studying\na whole new spectrum\nof launch\nvehicles\n(L/V\'s) for space transportation.\nSome of these could transport components\nof the space station\nto orbit, and some could take us to Mars and beyond to boldly expand our frontiers of knowledge.\nIn all our future L/V designs,\ndecreasing\nthe structural\nweight will always be of great concern.\nThis is tantamount\nto increased\npayload capability,\nwhich in turn means reduced cost-per-pound\nto orbit.\nOne very significant\nincrease\nin payload\ncapability\nhas been defined.\nIn an L/V recently\nstudied\nat\nMSFC,\nit has been shown that a sizable weight\nsavings\ncan be realized\nby a rearrangement\nof the\ninternal\npropellant\ntanks.\nStudies\nhave been conducted\nboth at MSFC\nand at Martin\nMarietta\nCorporation,\nmaker of the space shuttle external\ntank (ET), which show that approximately\n10,000 lb\ncan be saved by inverting\nthe relative\npositions\nof the liquid hydrogen\n(LH2) and the liquid oxygen\n(LOX) propellant\ntanks in a particular\nL/V studied.\nAs the vehicle sits on the launch pad, in the conventional\nconfiguration\nthe heavier LOX tank is\nlocated\non top of the lighter LH2. This requires\na heavy structural\nmember\nbetween\nthe two tanks to\nprevent the lighter LH2 tank from being crushed. This configuration\nalso requires\nlarge, long, and even\ndrag producing\nLOX feed lines running the length of the vehicle on the exterior fuselage.\nIf the relative\nposition\nof the propellant\ntanks is inverted,\nboth the heavy structural\nseparation\nmember\nand the long\nLOX feed lines could be deleted, and the fueling time would be reduced.\nHowever,\nthe LOX tank aft configuration\ngave the vehicle an aft center-of-gravity\n(cg) location\nthat caused controllability\nconcerns\nto surface. In the conventional\nconfiguration,\nthe L/V is controlled\nin\nthe ascent trajectory\nby the gimbaling\nof its rocket engines. Studies have been conducted\nat MSFC that\nshowed\nthat the resulting\naft-cg configured\nL/V would not be adequately\ncontrollable\nwith the engine\ngimbaling\nalone.\n\nB. Problem\n\nStatement\n\nIt is known that the controllability\nof an aft cg L/V is decreased.\nIn addition\nto an aft cg being\ncaused by an internal rearrangement\nof propellant\ntanks, aft cg L/V\'s may appear for other reasons such\nas the addition\nof solid and/or\nliquid\nstrap-ons.\nTherefore,\nin the new spectrum\nof L/V\'s\nbeing\nconsidered,\nthe controllability\nof the aft-cg configured\nvehicle must be assessed.\nWhen the available\ncontrol authority\nhas been determined\nto be inadequate\nor marginal using engine gimbaling\nalone, some\nmeans of flight control augmentation\nis required.\nIn this research\neffort, the author has proposed\na novel solution\nto provide\nthe required\nflight\ncontrol\naugmentation\nfor an aft-cg configured\nL/V when needed\nmost in the ascent trajectory,\nduring\nmaximum\ndynamic\npressure.1\n2 The L/V used in this research\nis one that has recently\nbeen studied at\nMSFC. The LH2 and LOX propellant\ntanks in the ET have been interchanged,\ngiving the vehicle an aft\n\ncg. It has been determined\nthat engine gimbaling\nalone does not provide adequate control. The required\nflight control\naugmentation\nis provided\nby aerodynamic\nflight control\naugmentors.\nThis solution\nproposes\nto not only solve the original problem of augmenting\nthe control of the aft cg vehicle, but also\nmay be used in the marginal\ncontrol\nconfiguration\nto enhance\ncontrollability,\nas load alleviators,\nto\nreduce engine gimbaling\nrequirements,\nto provide\nengine actuator\nfailure protection,\nand to enhance\ncrew safety and vehicle reliability\nby providing\nmore control in engine-out\nevents.\nThese devices\ncan reduce wind restrictions.\nConventionally,\nthe L/V loads during\nascent are\nalleviated\nby turning the vehicle into the wind, thereby reducing\nthe flight angle-of-attack.\nThus, load\nrelief is accomplished\nat the expense of trajectory\ndeviation.\nLoad relief control is most necessary\nwhen\nthe L/V experiences\nmaximum\ndynamic pressure\nand the aerodynamic\nloads are greatest. This would be\nwhen the flight control augmentors\nwould provide the most significant\nassistance.\nThe proposed\nadded\ncontrol\ncapability\nthrough the use of these surfaces\nallows greater tolerance\nof wind magnitudes\nand a\nminimization\nof bending\nmoments\non the vehicle both during ascent and during launch. For prelaunch,\nthe unfueled\nvehicle on the pad is assumed to withstand\npeak loads of 75 kn, and fueled at lift-off peak\nwinds of 50 kn. The environmental\ndisturbances\nare multiplied\nby 1.5 to account for von Karman vortex\nshedding\neffects.\nWind profiles\nshow that the greatest\nsteady wind speeds occur between\n20,000 and\n60,000 ft, with a gust overshoot\nof up to 50 percent. 3 The more the engines are required\nto gimbal, i.e.,\nthe greater\nthe required\nengine\ngimbal\ndeflection\nangles,\nthe more engineering\ndesign\nand cost is\ninvolved\nto have the propellant\nducts move with the gimbal action while maintaining\na full flow of fuel.\nGimbaled\nengines\nare difficult to seal and have high actuation torques. The extension,\ncompression,\nand\ntorsion of propellant\nducts become limiting factors of engine gimbaling.\nThus, the designed\naerodynamic\nsurfaces of this research\nprovide not only the required control augmentation,\nbut a plethora\nof additional\nsignificant\nbenefits.\n\nC. Approach\nAs a starting\npoint for the novel design of aerodynamic\nflight control augmentors\nfor a Saturn\nclass, aft cg L/V, which is the mission of Center Director\'s\nDiscretionary\nFund (CDDF)\nproject 93-05,\nthis report undertakes\na review of our national heritage of L/V\'s using aerodynamic\nsurfaces, along with\na survey\nof current\nuse of aerodynamic\nsurfaces\non large L/V\'s of other nations.\nNASA has a rich\nnational\nheritage\nof L/V\'s that have used aerodynamic\nsurfaces,\nboth to provide\nflight stability\nand to\nprovide flight control. The Saturn V L/V has flown 13 successful\nmissions. The Saturn V missions to the\nMoon utilized 300 ft 2 of aerodynamic\nsurfaces. Today, there is a myriad of L/V\'s actively launched\nfrom\nalmost two dozen geographic\nsites, many of which are using aerodynamic\nsurfaces.\nThis CDDF research effort draws on advancements\nin many other fields. Since NASA journeyed\nto the Moon with the Saturn V and its 300 ft 2 of aerodynamic\nsurfaces, the wealth of smart materials\nand\nadvanced\ncomposites\nthat have been developed\nallow for the design of very lightweight,\nstrong, and\ninnovative\nL/V flight control augmentors.\nA previous companion\npublication\n4 conducted\na state-of-theart assessment\nof smart materials\nand advanced\ncomposites\nwhich are directly applicable\nto aerodynamic\nflight control augmentors\nof future L/V\'s. Thus, any added weight concerns\nare addressed.\nIn a subsequent\nCDDF\nProject\n93-05\npublication,\nthe actual control\nrequirements\nof the\nexperimental\naft-cg configured\nL/V of this research\nare determined.\nThese determined\nflight control\nrequirements,\ntogether\nwith the review of this report, the L/V mass properties,\nand the ascent trajectory\ndata are used to design the candidate\nflight control surface augmentors.\nA wind tunnel test program\nhas\nbeen designed\nfor these candidate\nflight control augmentors\nand the host experimental\naft cg L/V. The\nwind tunnel test data are then utilized to conduct the vehicle static and dynamic stability analyses\nand to\ndemonstrate\nthe augmented\ncontrol\nauthority\nachievable\nwith the use of the flight control\naugmentor\ndesigns.\nFigure\n1 shows the flow chart of this conducted\nresearch effort (CDDF Project 93-05) and the\nrelationship\nof previous\nand subsequent\npublications\nto this one.\n\n2\n\nI\n\nBackground\n\nI Problem Statement\n\nI Appr\xc2\xb0ach\nReview of National\nHeritage of L/V\'s\n\nCurrent\nUse of\nAero\nForeign\nSurfaces\n\n_\n\n$\n\nSmart Materials;\nAdvance Composites\n\nControl Requirements\nDetermination of\n\n1\n\nl\n\nSaturn V;\nX-15\n\nI\n\nt\n\nI==I Design of Candidate Surfaces\n\nI _\n\n/\n\nStatic Wind Tunnel\n\n|\n\nI\n\n[gYnamieCr\nwindTunne.\n\nStatic Test\n\nI\n\nI\n\nstat\'c Stabi\'ity\nAnalyses\n\nI\n\nI\n\ngYnam\'c Stabi\'itY\nAnalyses\n\nElastic\n\nL\n\nVehicle\n\nDynamicTest\n\nProgram\n\nJ\n\nMode Shapes\n\nFrequencies\n\nI\n\nI\n\nI\n\nI\n\nDemonstration of Flight Control Augmentation\nProvided by Selected Designs\n\nI\n\nI\n\nFuture Work\n\nFigure\n\nII.\n\nREVIEW\n\n1. The X-15 flew to an\naltitude of 354,200 ft, reached Mach 6.7, and made almost 200 flights over a 9-year period. In the words\nof Neil Armstrong:\n"The X-15 rocket research vehicle is a large ring of keys for unlocking\nthe mysteries\nof future flight. \'\'15 The X-15\nwas part L/V, with an RCS and a thermal\nprotection\nsystem,\nand was\ndesigned\nto explore the hypersonic\nspeed regime and near-space\nenvironment.\nIt was actually the second\nstage of a two-stage\nsystem. Saturn\n\nI-B\n\nThe Saturn I-B aerodynamic\nsurfaces underwent extensive\nredesign and produced\nnearly 2.5\ntimes the lift as the eight stubs of the previous designs,\nand weighed\n1,800 lb less. Because of the long\nchord and short span, the eight stub fins on the Saturn I were very inefficient\naerodynamic\nsurfaces.
