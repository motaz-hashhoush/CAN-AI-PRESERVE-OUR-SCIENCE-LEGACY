It is used for the launch of\nEarth orbital, Earth synchronous and interplanetary missions.\nCentaur was the nation\'s first high-energy, liquidhydrogen/liquid-oxygen propelled rocket. -Developed and\nlaunched under the direction of NASA\'s Lewis Research Center,\nit became operational in 1966 with the launch of Surveyor 1,\nthe first U.S. spacecraft to soft-land on the Moon\'s surface.\nSince that time, both the Atlas booster and Centaur\nsecond stage have undergone many improvements. At present,the vehicle combination can place 4,536 kg (10,000 Ib.) in\nlow Earth orbit, 1,882 kg (4,150 Ib.) in a synchronous transfer orbit and 907 kg (2,000..1b.) on -an interplanetary trajectory.\n.;: The Atlas Centaur, standing approximately 40.8 m (134 ft.)\nhigh,_consists of an Atlas SLV-3D booster and Centaur D-1AR\nsecond stage. The Atlas booster develops 1,920 kilonewtons \xe2\x80\xa2\n(431,300 Ib.) of thrust at liftoff using two 822,920-newton\n(185,000-lb.) thrust booster engines, one 266,890-N (60,000-lb.)\nthrust-sustainer engine and two vernier engines developing . \xe2\x80\xa2".\n2,890 N (650 Ib.) thrust each. The two RL-10 engines on\n,"\nCentaur produce a total of 133,450 N (30,000 Ib.) thrust.-: :;\nBoth the Atlas and the Centaur are-3 m (10 ft.) in diameter.\nUntil early 1974, Centaur was used exclusively in com- =: .\nbination with the Atlas booster. It was subsequently used \'\nwith a Titan III booster to, .launch heavier payloads into Earth\norbit and interplanetary trajectories. .\n.\n\xe2\x80\xa2 .,\nThe Atlas and the Centaur-vehicles have been updated .\nover the years.
