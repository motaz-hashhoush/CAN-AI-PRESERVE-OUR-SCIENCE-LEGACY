(COMSAT), the United States\nmember, is also the management services contractor for the\nsatellite system.\n\nNASA is reimbursed for all costs of the\n\nAtlas Centaur and launch services by COMSAT on behalf of\nIntelsat, under provisions of a launch services agreement.\n\nThe Atlas Centaur AC-46 launch vehicle is expected to\nplace the Intelsat IV-A in a highly elliptical orbit of 548\nby 35,940 kilometers (341 by 22,332 miles).\n\nAfter reorien-\n\ntation of the satellite, a solid propellant rocket motor\naboard the spacecraft will be fired to circularize the orbit\nat synchronous altitude 35,940 km (22,332 mi.) over the\nequator.\n\nAt that altitude, because the speed of the space-\n\ncraft in orbit matches the rotational speed of the Earth,\nthe satellite remains in position over one spot.\n-more-\n\nThe launch of Intelsat spacecraft aboard Atlas Centaur\nrockets requires the coordinated efforts of a large government and industry team.\n\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center,\n\nCleveland, Ohio, has management responsibility for the\nAtlas Centaur development and operation.\n\nNASA\'s Kennedy\n\nSpace Center, Fla., is assigned vehicle checkout and launch\nresponsibility once the vehicle reaches Cape Canaveral.\n\nThe Intelsat IV-A satellites, built by Hughes Aircraft\nCo., El Segundo, Calif., are 6.98 meters tall (about 23 feet)\nand weigh 1,515 kg (about 3,340 Ib.) at liftoff and 825.5 kg\n(1,820 Ib.) after apogee motor firing.\n\nThe Intelsat IV-A program represents an investment by\n101 nations of approximately $295 million (U.S.).\nlaunch costs approximately $47 million \xe2\x80\x94\n\nThis\n\n$18 million for\n\nthe satellite and $29 million for the Atlas Centaur launch\nvehicle and related services.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE.\n\nBACKGROUND INFORMATION FOLLOWS.)\n\n-more-\n\n-4-\n\nSPACECRAFT DESCRIPTION\nThe Intelsat IV-A spacecraft has an overall height of\n7 m (23 ft.) and a diameter of 2.4 m (8 ft.). The height\nof the solar panel is 2.8 m (9 ft.). Liftoff weight is\napproximately 1,511 kg (3,332 lb.), and in-orbit weight\nafter apogee motor firing is 825.5 kg (1,820 lb.).\nAlthough it has the same basic structural design as\nits predecessor, Intelsat IV, the Intelsat IV-A spacecraft\nincorporates new antenna technology to yield about 6,250\ntwo-way voice circuits plus two television channels in the\nsystem configuration in which it will be used. This is\ntwo-thirds greater than the communications capacity of the\nof the Intelsat IV series satellite. The increased capacity\nis made possible by a new antenna design which provides\ncoverage of land manses on both sides of the Atlantic basin,\nusing shaped beams. The eastern and western beams are sufficiently isolated to allow the frequency spectrum to be\nused twice \xe2\x80\x94 once in the east and once in the west direction\n\xe2\x80\x94 thus doubling the use of the frequency spectrum and increasing the communications capacity of the satellite.\nATLAS CENTAUR LAUNCH VEHICLE\nThe Atlas Centaur is NASA\'s standard launch vehicle for\nintermediate weight payloads.
