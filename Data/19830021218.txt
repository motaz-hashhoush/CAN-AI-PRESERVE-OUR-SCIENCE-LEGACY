KOBRICK\nJet Propulsion Laboratory, Pasadena, California 91103, USA\n\nABSTRACT\nThe application of side-looking radar images in geoscience fields can be enhanced when using overlapping image strips that are viewed in stereo. A\ncurrent question concerns the quality of stereoradar. This paper is an attempt to describe this\nquality, evaluating stereo viewability and using the\nconcept of vertical exaggeration with sets of actual\nradar images. A conclusion is that currently available stereo radar data are not optimized, that\ntherefore a better quality can be achieved if data\nacquisition is appropriately arranged , and that the\nactual limitations of stereo radar are still unexplored.\nKeywords: Radargrammetry, Stereo, Side-looking\nradar, Cartographic mapping.\nI. INTRODUCTION\nStereo viewing of overlapping images is a valuable\ntool in photo-interpretation. It is also an indispensable technique to identify homologue image\npoints for measurement of image coordinates and reconstruction of the three dimensional terrain. This\nmay serve to create a model of terrain topography\nsuch as in the form of contours, or to selectively\nmeasure slopes and relative height differences.\nS t e r e o refers to a visual preception of an\noverlapping image pair by an observer. A three-dimensional model is formed in the observer\'s brain.\nAlso the computational process of generating 3dimensional object space coordinates from sets of\nmonocular image measurements is sometimes denoted\nby stereo.\n\nFor the observer radar stereo is not different from\nits photographic equivalent. However, there is an\nentirely different projection geometry and mathematical model. The human operator perceives relief\ndisplaceme\xe2\x80\x9et in the form of so-called parall.:x\ndifferences, ju,-t as is the case in natural binocular vision ad by presentation of two images to\nthe eyes that is called stereoscopic viewing.\nThere is a considerable body of literature on stereo\npercent : on. A recent review is by LaPrade et al.\n(1980). On radar stereo, work has been done since\n1963, starting with LaPrade (1963). Most recently\nsome nutlerical results were presented by Leberl\n(1979 a). Stereo viewability of radar images was\ndiscussed by LaPrade (1970), Graham (1975), Leberl\n(1975, 1978, 1979). Computational stereo was analysed by Innes (1964), Rosenfield (1968), Gracie et\nal. (1970), Konecny (1972), DBA-Systems (1964),\nGoodyear (1974), Derenyi (1975), Leberl (1972, 1975,\n\n1978).\n\nCommonly discussed stereo imaging arrangements have\nbeen either with both flights to the same side or\neach flight at opposite sides of the object. Other\narrangements have been described but did not materialize, such as cross-wise flights, different\nflight altitudes or single flight convergent schemes\nsuch as with tilted real antennas ( Leberl, 1972;\nCarlson, 1973; Bair and Carlson, 1974, 1975). A\nsingle f l ight line attempt to generate stereo SAR\nwould fail. This has been explained in detail in\na previous report (Leberl, 1979 ).\nThe current paper is an attempt at expanding previous theoretical error analyses using some real\nimages and to evaluate them with the concept of\nexaggeration factors. These images are from X-band\nSAR (Goodyear), L-band SAR (JPL), Apollo 17 -VHF\nlunar imagery, SEASAT-SAR and real aperture Motorola radar. Some examples of overlapping radar\nimages are presented; not all can be viewed sucessfuliy .n stereo. However, a clear conclusion can\nnot be obtained on the limiting cases where stereo\nis still feasible. More data are needed for that\npurpose.\n2.
