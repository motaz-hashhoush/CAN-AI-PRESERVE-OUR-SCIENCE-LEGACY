THE THEORY\n\nUnfortunately, since household outputs are not sold, it\nis difficult to place a monetary value on them, so as to\ncompare their value with those of outputs in the market\neconomy. There is, however, one household output which is\nsold in the market economy--labor. CONCLUSIONS\nThe household economy far exceeds the market economy in\nsize. If one were to redefine personal consumption expenditures (PCE) to include the value of time expended in the\nhousehold, the 1973 Gross National Product (GNP) would have\nbeen around $4700 billion, rather than $1307 billions Many\nof the "industries" of the household economy are gigantic\ncompared to most conventional industries. The value of time\nand money which are devoted to watching television, for example, far exceeds the market expenditures on either housing\nor food.\n\nIn virtually every activity, the value of time which people devote to the activity exceeds the value of market expenditures on the activity. This suggests a substantial\nwillingness of people to pay for innovations which would reduce the time spent on activities which are displeasureable\nor neutral. Although this statement may not sound very\noriginal, this willingness to pay for time savings may be\n\nS This number was obtained by adding my total expense for all\nfamilies (value of time and market expenditures) of $4197\nbillion to the 1973 total of $497 billion for gross privnie\ndomestic investment, net exports, and government purchases.\nThe number is approximate, since my definition of market\nexpenditures does not exactly match the standard definition\nof PCE.\nSource:U.S, Bureau of the Censu,;t, Statistical Abstract of\n\nthe United States _ 1978, p. 440.\n11\n\nf\n\nmuch greater than is commonly perceived. For example, most\nof us are accustomed to thinking of eating out as an "expensive" activity. Yet, the data in Appendix C indicate that\nthe value of a restaurant customer\'s time is an average of\n1.9 times more valuable than the cost of the meal purchased.\nThe success of the fast-food restaurant industry in recent\nyears may be attributable more to the time savings these establishments offer than to their low prices. \t\n\nIf this hy-\n\npothesis is correct, there is every reason to expect that\nmany people would prefer higher quality food than most\nfood\nfast-food restaurants offer, and be willing to pay for it,\nif only they could get it quickly. \t\n\nHence, there is probab\'\n\na substantial untapped market for high-quality fast-food\nrestaurants.\n\nIn general, one might assume that the higher the ratio of\ntime value to market expenditures, the more willing people\nwould be to make a given percentage increase in their expenditures so as to obtain a given percentage decrease in\ntime spent on an activity.\t\n\nHouse cleaning, with its 19.3\n\nratio, and personal care at home with a 25.7 ratio would\nseem ripe for innovation.\t\n\nSo would education (2.3 ratio),\n\nreading (28.3), and hobbies and crafts (18.8). Although it\nwould require a substantial technological breakthrough, any\ninnovation which could safely and comfortably enable people\nto reduce the time they spend sleeping would have an enor-\n\n12\n\nmous impact. People currently spend time with a value\nroughly equal to the GNP sleeping.\n\nFor a few activities, expecially medical care and housing, the very low ratios of time value to market expenditure\nsuggest that innovations which allow people to reduce market\nexpenditures by devoting a bit more of their own time to the\nactivity would have an impact.\t\n\nDo-it-yourself solar water\n\nheating systems might be one example of such an innovation,\nVarious types of medical monitoring equipment for use in the\nhome might be another.\n\nInterestingly, the ratio of time value to market expenditures are remarkably stable across income classes. There\nare distinctly higher ratios for food and clothing in lower\nincome classes, indicating a "do-it-yourself" tendency among\nlower income households. However, the opposite would appear\nto be true of housing. Perhaps this is because lower income\nhouseholds generally have more modest housing, requiring\nless care and maintenance than higher income households. As\nmight be expected, market expenditures per hour devoted to\nan activity rise with income for all activities. \t\n\nIn gen-\n\neral, one might infer than all income classes would be receptive to innovations which improve household productivity.\n\n13\n\nThere is a need for regular monitoring of the houshold\neconomy through some indicators similar to those presently\nused to monitor the market economy. These indicators would\nnot necessarily require the type of detailed time allocation\ndata used in this study, although they could be improved if\nthis type of data were available on a regular basis.\t\n\nUseful\n\nindicators of total household production could , be developed\nfrom existing data on wage rates, employment, and the size\nof various socio-economic groups. These indicators would\nremind everyone of what has been demonstrated in this paper--that what most economic planners think of as \'the economy\' is merely the tip of the economic iceberg.
