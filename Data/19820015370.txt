Equilibrium constraints normal to the\nrunway surface and about the aircraft roll and pitch axes were imposed. Transient\ntire forces from steady-sta-te data were introduced by inserting a time lag between\nthe computed tire yaw angle and the resulting tire force. planar equations\nThe\nderived were used to describe the position and heading of an aircraft relative to a\nrunway coordinate-axis system. Computed trajectories were compared with those found\nexperimentally for a small-scale landing-gear model traversing a laterally sloping\nrunway with and without nose-gear steering. Correlation with experiment was good.\nNumerical studies indicated that trajectories were sensitive to the initial translatory velocities of the test, tire drag, and angular landing-gear misalinement. The\neffect of the time lag on the computation of the tire forces, however, was found to\nbe negligible for the trajectories of this paper.\nINTRODUCTION\nThe landing of an aircraEt in a strong crosswind is a difficult and possibly\nhazardous operation. Crosswind effects have not been as significant in the past\nbecause they have been lessened by airport design considerations such as alining\nrunways in the direction of prevailing winds or providing several runways with different headings. However, such consideratiahs are not practical \xe2\x82\xacor all airports;\nand, in particular, airports envisioned for short take-off and landing (STOL) aircraft operations are expected to offer few choices of runway headings. Such runway\nalinement restrictions potentially expose the aircraft to higher. crosswinds than\nthose currently encountered. Furthermore, the slow landing speeds of STOL-type\naircraft make them especially sensitive to crosswinds. Methods are needed to reduce\nthis sensitivity and increase landing flexibility.\n\nLanding-gear systems have been conceived to increase control of the aircraft\non\nthe ground in the presence of a crosswind, and tests of some crosswind gear concepts\nusing a small model were reported in reference In these tests, a laterally sloped\n1.\nrunway provided a side component of the gravity force to simulate a crosswind.\nAlthough the model tests provided some evaluation and comparisons of the concepts,\nanalytical techniques are needed to supplement landing-gear-system studies and provide insight into gear behavior. A set of equations of motion is required for aircraft rollout which has the capability to represent the behavior of unusual, pneumat\nically tired, landing-gear systems when there are sidewise external loads upon the\naircraft.\nEquations that describe the motion pneumatically tired automobiles have been\nof\ndeveloped (see refs.2 and 3), but corresponding equations that describe the aircraft\nrollout and taxi motion are not generally available in the literature. Reference\n4\ndiscusses the ideal track of a steered aircraft that rolls without slipping, but\ninfluences of external loads and tire behavior are missing.\n\nThis paper presents planar equations that describe the rolling motion of an\naircraft in a touchdown attitude subjected to applied forces and moments and having\n\nfreely castoring or steerable landing gears equipped with pneumatic tires. Supple\nmental expressions are developed from tire test data relating the instantaneous\nforces and moments\non each wheel to the wheel angle.
