It is from this orbit at apogee\nthat a solid propellant rocket motor attached to the satellite\nwill be fired to circularize the orbit at geosynchronous altitudes over the equator.\n\nAt that altitude, because the speed of\n\nthe satellite in orbit matches the rotational speed of the Earth,\nthe satellite remains in position over one spot.\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center in Cleveland has management\nresponsibility for Atlas Centaur development and operation.\nNASA\'s Kennedy Space Center, Fla., is assigned vehicle checkout\nand launch responsibility once the Atlas Centaur reaches Cape\nCanaveral.\n\nOverall direction of the program is vested in the\n\nOffice of Space Transpoztation Operations at NASA Headquarters.\nThis Intelsat V launch costs approximately $76.6 million -including $34 million for the satellite and $42 million for the\nAtlas Centaur and related launch services.\n\n(END OF GENERAL RELEASE; BACKGROUND INFORMATION FOLLOWS.)\n\n-more-\n\n-3-\n\nATLAS CENTAUR LAUNCH VEHICLE\nThe Atlas Centaur is NASA\'s standard\'launch vehicle for\nintermediate weight payloads. It is used for the launch of Earth\norbital, Earth synchronous and interplanetary missions.\nCentaur was the nation\'s first high energy, liquid hydrogen/\nliquid oxygen propelled rocket. Developed and launched under the\ndirection of NASA\'s Lewis Research Center, it became operational\nin 1966 with the launch of Surveyor 1, the first U.S. spacecraft\nto soft-land on the Moon\'s surface.\nSince that time, both the Atlas booster and Centaur second\nstage have undergone many improvements. At present, the vehicle\ncombination can place 4,536 kilograms (10,000 pounds) in low\nEarth orbit, 1,928 kg (4,250 Ib.) in a geosynchronous transfer\norbit and 907 kg (2,000 Ib.) on an interplanetary trajectory.\nThe Atlas Centaur, standing approximately 39.9 meters (131\nfeet) high, consists of an Atlas SLV-3D booster and Centaur D-1AR\nsecond stage.\nThe Atlas booster develops 1,920 kilonewtons\n(431,300 Ib.) of thrust at liftoff using two 822,920-newton\n(185,000-lb.) thrust booster engines, one 266,890-N (60,000-lb.)\nthrust sustainer engine and two vernier engines developing 2,890\nN (650 Ib.) thrust each. The two RL-10 engines on Centaur produce a total of 133,450 N (30,000 Ib.) thrust. Both the Atlas and\nthe Centaur are 3 m (10 ft.) in diameter.\nUntil early 1974, Centaur was used exclusive in combination\nwith the Atlas booster. It was subsequently used with a Titan III\nbooster to launch heavier payloads into Earth orbit and interplanetary trajectories.\nThe Atlas and the Centaur vehicles have been updated over\nthe years. Thrust of the Atlas engines has been increased about\n222,400 N(50,000 Ib.) since their first use in the space program\nin the early 1960s.\nThe Centaur has an integrated electronic system that performs a major role in checking itself and other vehicle systems\nbefore launch and also maintains control of major events after\nliftoff. The system handles navigation and guidance tasks, controls, pressurization and venting, propellant management, telemetry formats and transmission and initiates vehicle events.\nThe Atlas and Centaur stages of Atlas Centaur 54 arrived at\nCape Canaveral Air Force Station Aug. 6, 1980.\nThe Atlas was\nerected on Pad B of Launch Complex 36 on Aug. 12; the Centaur was\nerected on Aug. 14. A Terminal Countdown Demonstration test was\nconducted Oct. 3 to verify the integrity of\'the vehicle-to-ground\nsystems in an environment duplicating launch conditions.\n\n-more-\n\n-4-\n\nThe Intelsat spacecraft arrived Oct. 21 and received its\ninitial checkout in Hangar AO at Cape Canaveral.\nIt was moved\nNov. 7 to the Explosive Safe Facility for final servicing and\ncapsulation. There the spacecraft\'s attitude control system was\nfueled with hydrazine, the apogee kick motor was installed and\nthe protective payload shroud was put in place.\nThe spacecraft and launch vehicle were to be mated on Pad B\nNov. 24. A Combined Readiness Test was scheduled for Nov. 25.\nThe final countdown begins three days before launch.\nAll launch vehicle and pad operations during the launch\ncountdown are conducted from the blockhouse at Complex 36 by a\nKennedy Space Center contractor team.\n\n-more-\n\n-5-\n\nLAUNCH VEHICLE CHARACTERISTICS\nLiftoff weight including spacecraft:\nLiftoff height:\n\n39.9 m (131\n\nLaunch Complex:\n\n148,173 kg\n(326,665 Ib.)\n\nft.)\n\n36A\n\nAtlas Booster\n\nCentaur Stage\n\nWeight (with\npropellants)\n\n129,397 kg\n(285,272 Ib.)\n\n18,826 kg\n(41,504 Ib.)\n\nHeight\n\n21.3 m (70 ft.)\n\n18.6 m (61 ft.)\nwith payload\nfairing\n\nThrust\n\n1,920 kN\n(431,300 Ib.)\nat sea level\n\n133,447 N\n(30,000 Ib.)\nin vacuum\n\nPropellants\n\nLiquid oxygen and\nRP-1\n\nLiquid oxygen\nand liquid\nhydrogen\n\nPropulsion\n\nMA-5 system, two\n822,920-N (185,000-lb.)\nthrust booster engines,\none 266,890-N (60,000Ib.) thrust sustainer\nengine, two 2,890-N\n(650-lb.) thrust vernier\nengines.\n\nTwo 66,723-N\n(15,000-lb.)\nthrust RL-10\nengines, 12\nsmall hydrogen\nperoxide\nthrusters.\n\nVelocity\n\n8,642 km/hr (5,370 mph)\nat booster engine cutoff\n(BECO), 12,971 km/hr\n(8,060 mph) at sustainer\nengine cutoff (SECO).\n\n35,021 km/hr\n(21,761 mph)\nat spacecraft\nseparation.\n\nGuidance\n\nPreprogrammed profile\nthrough BECO.
