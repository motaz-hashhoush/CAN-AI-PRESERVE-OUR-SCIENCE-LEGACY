The speeds involved\nranged from a Mach number of 0.7 to 1.05. The motions were characterized by extreme variations in angles of attack and sideslip which\nresulted in load factors as large as 6 . 7g (negative) and 7g (positive)\nnormal acceleration and 2g transverse acceleration.\nINTRODUCTION\nDuring flight testing of the X-3 straight-wing research airplane\nand a swept- wing fighter - type airplane, a number of aileron maneuvers\nwere performed which resulted in extremely violent inadvertent lateral\nand longitudinal motions. In the case of the X-3 the inadvertent\nmotions resulted from level- flight aileron rolls at M = 0 . 92 and 1 . 05 .\nThe fighter airplane encountered the violent motions as a result of an\naileron roll at M = 0.70. In all cases the motions were characterized\nby the attainment of large angles of sideslip and attack with resulting\nhigh load factors.\nThe present paper presents the first data with little attempt at\nanalysis, since it is felt that data showing this behavior will be of\ngeneral interest in that other current airplanes might be expected to\nencounter similar behavior.\n\n2\n\nNAeA RM\n\nH55Al3\n\nSYMBOIS\nAy\n\ntransverse acceleration at center of gravity, g units\n\nAZ\n\nnormal acceleration, g units\n\nEM\n\nstructural bending-moment load, exposed panel, in.-lb\nairplane normal-force coefficient,\n\nAzW/qS\n\naileron stick force, lb\nrudder pedal force, lb\nelevator stick force, lb\npressure altitude, ft\nincidence angle of all movable tail, leading edge up is\npositive, deg\nL\n\nM\'\n\nMach number , corrected for level-flight position error\n\nMc/4\n\nstructural torque load, exposed panel about the quarterchord of the panel mean aerodynamic chord, in.-lb\n\np\n\nstatic pressure, lb/sq ft\n\np\n\nrolling angular velocity, radians/sec\n\np\n\nrolling angular acceleration, radians/sec 2\n\nq\n\npitching angular velocity, radians/sec\n\nq\n\ndynamic pressure,\n\nq\n\npitching an~lar acceleration, radians/sec 2\n\nr\n\nyawing angular velocity, radians/sec\n\nr\n\nyawing angular acceleration, radians/sec 2\n\ns\n\nL\n\nstructural shear load, exposed panel, lb\n\nwing area, sq ft\n\no. 7\n\n~p\n\nNACA RM H55A13\n\n3\n\nt\n\ntime, sec\n\nW\n\nairplane weight, lb\n\na\n\nindicated angle of attack, deg\n\nP\n\nindicated angle of sideslip, deg\n\n0a\n\naileron angle, deg\n\nOr\n\nrudder angle, deg\n\nSubscripts :\nW\n\nwing\n\nlIT\n\nhorizontal tail\n\nVT\n\nvertical tail\nAIRPIANES AND INSTRUMENTATION\n\nI\n\nI .\n\nThe mass and geometric characteristics of airplane A, the\nX- 3 research airplane, are presented in table I, a three - While the ailerons are deflected for the aileron\nroll, a favorable sideslip angle is generated, together with a rather\nlarge increase in angle of attack. (The initial decrease in angle of\nattack is probably attributable to pilot stabilizer control input.) At\ntime 3.S seconds, even though the pilot is now applying 100 right aileron\ncontrol, left rolling velocity increased and exceeded 5 radians/sec\naccompanied by violent pitching and sideslipping motions. During this\nuncontrollable phase of the maneuver, an angle of attack of 20\xc2\xb0 and left\nsideslip angle of 16\xc2\xb0 were encountered. It might be of interest to note\nthat the onset of the violent maneuver coincided with the attainment of\nthe angle of attack (a ~ SO) at which unpublished flight data indicates\nthe occurrence of reduction of longitudinal stability. Also, the angle\nof attack of SO corresponds to the angle of attack at which a reduction\nin the measured wing lift slope occurs; therefore, large wing loads were\nnot experienced at the maximum angles of attack (fig. 5(c)). After the\nprimary rolling motion has subsided at t = 5 seconds, the large lateral\nand longitudinal motions damp fairly well.\nAnother left roll time history for airplane A is presented in\nfigure 6 for a Mach number of about 1.05. During this maneuver the\nfavorable sideslip builds up rapidly with roll velocity and peaks at\n21\xc2\xb0 at the time the airplane ceases rolling left. This large sideslip\nangle results in about 2g transverse acceleration. Near the time at\nwhich maximum sideslip occurs (t ~ 4.0 sec) a large divergence in\npitch develops in the negative direction which attains about -6.7g.\nThe pilot applied control to stop this pitch-down and immediately\nreduced the control deflection, but was unable to avoid obtaining 7g\n.Then the airplane pitched up. Maximum wing loads measured during the\nmaneuver did not approach or exceed the design limit load (fig. 6(c));\nhowever, the fuselage load obtained from airplane weight and acceleration, and horizontal tail loads, and wing load showed maximum values of\n63,000 pounds . These maximum values approximated the limit design total\nload of the fuselage. The measured horizontal tail loads were near\nlimit design load (fig. 6(d)) . The maximum measured vertical tail loads\nreached approximately 50 percent of the limit load (fig. 6(e)) at sideslip angles of 21\xc2\xb0 . It may be noted that a reduction in vertical tail\nload with sideslip was experienced at sideslip angles about So. In this\nmaneuver, as in figure 5, when the rolling stopped, the airplane motions\nquickly damped.\nThe measured quantities for an abrupt left aileron roll for airplane B\nat an altitude of 30,000 feet and a Mach number of 0.70 are shown as\n\nNACA RM H55Al")\n\n5\n\nfigure 7. Unfortunately, the roll record was lost as a result of\ninstrument malfunctioning. As peak aileron deflection is attained,\nthere is a steady development of left (adverse) sideslip and a progressive decrease in angle of attack. Between t = ") seconds and\nt = 4 seconds, the divergence rates are accelerated considerably and\nnegative angles of attack greater than 160 (-4.4g) and left sideslip\nangles as large as 26 0 were reached in the more violent stages of this\nmaneuver. Maximum vertical tail loads of 5,500 pounds were measured at\na sideslip angle of 26 0 (fig. 7(c)). It may be noted that appreciable\nreduction in the rate of increase of vertical tail loads with increase\nin sideslip angle occurred at a sideslip angle of 7\xc2\xb0. It also should\nbe noted that a rather large stabilizer input to oppose the motion and\na small rudder input to aggravate the motion were fed in by the pilot.\nRecovery was affected by bringing the stabilizer and rudder controls\nclose to their initial settings and permitting the airplane to recover.\nThe slat motions shown result from aerodynamic and inertia loads, the\nslats being free floating but with the segments loosely interconnected.\nThe behavior of the two airplanes in the aileron rolls is similar\nin that large cross-coupling effects are evidenced. The airplanes are\nloaded primarily along the fuselage, particularly in the case of\nairplane A, so that considerable inertial coupling is expected (see\nref. 1).\nCONCLUDING REMARKS\nIt is evident from the time histories presented herein that violent\ncoupled pitching and yawing motions can be encountered during abrupt\naileron maneuvers. This motion is sufficiently violent to cause the\npilot to lose control of the airplane and could easily result in structural failure.\nHigh-Speed Fli ght Station,\nNational Advisory Committee for Aeronautics,\nEdwards, Calif., December 27, 1954.\n\n6\n\nNACA RM E55Al3\nREFERENCE\n\n1.
