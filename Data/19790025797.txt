accuracy of the prediction in the forward quadrant seems to depend\non the propagation distance. It can be noted that half of the flight test\ncases have minimum propagation distances of approximately 90 m (295 ft)\nwhile the remainder have 335 m (1100 ft). Those comparisons of measured\nand predicted noise that have larger propagation distances show larger differences in noise levels.\nIt is important to notice that frequency for a predicted and a corresponding measured tone differ sometimes by a third-octave. This can be\nattributed to doppler frequency shift change caused either by a tone occurring\nat the frequency limits of a third-octave filter band or a basic error in\ncorrelating measured and predicted data at the same radiation angle. Further,\na difference in tone amplitudes can be explained in terms of an aircraft\nvelocity effect that seems to be most significant in the forward quadrant as\nseen in earlier cited data. This flight effect can be traced to a direct\nresult of empirical fan source noise prediction or acoustic liner insertion\nlosses based on static engine data.\nAnother factor that would particularly increase predicted forward inlet\nradiated noise relative to measured is structural shielding. The L-1011\ncenter engine inlet is most likely shielded by the fuselage during overhead\nflyover measurements. Possibly, a better method to compute noise would\ninclude using two inlets and three exhaust flows rather than assuming three\nof each.\nAccuracy of the prediction of the ground reflection phenomenon is\nvery good. Upon examining the spectral plots comparing measured and predicted data, the cancellation and reinforcement effects from an above ground\nmicrophone match very closely. In fact, a good procedure for determining the\ndifference in radiation angles of measured and predicted data could lie in\nthe location of the first null from a ground reflection.\nParticular significance is placed on the capability of predicting peak\naircraft flyover noise in the aft quadrant. This is demonstrated reasonably\nwell by ANOPP.\n\xe2\x80\xa2\nANOPP improvements that would be helpful to a user would include:\n\xe2\x80\xa2\n\nmore detailed documentation on technical content of prediction\nmethods\n\n\xe2\x80\xa2\n\ngeneral procedure or guidelines for constructing a run deck applied\nto a particular type airplane or specified testing requirements\n\n\xe2\x80\xa2\n\n124\n\ncapability of generating aircraft community noise characteristics\ni - noise versus distance curves\n- noise contours.\n\nEven with the brevity of the existing ANOPP documentation it is possible\nfor someone with rudimentary knowledge of computer programming to successfully\nuse ANOPP within a few weeks. Once it is realized there is nothing mysteriously\nor technically prohibitive about ANOPP, then it is merely a task of becoming\nfamiliar with the program structure and format.\nANOPP would be useful in performing noise trade-off studies. By adjusting aircraft/engine operating parameters flyover noise could be minimized at\nparticular location(s) on the ground. Criteria of performance and economics\nwould be used to determine optimum flyover noise level. Another utility of\nANOPP would be in preliminary design studies where aircraft/engine design\nparameters are varied to produce a minimum noise airplane. In most cases the\nabsolute magnitude of noise levels is not a critical factor. ABSTRACT\nThis report describes the work completed by Lockheed to verify the\nAircraft Noise Prediction Program (ANOPP) developed by NASA Langley Research\nCenter. In this study, ANOPP was used to predict the far-field noise characteristics of an L-1011 aircraft equipped with RB.211-524B engines.\nA comparison was made with actual flight noise with noise predicted\nfor the same operating and weather conditions. Correlation of predicted\nand measured results was very good in the aft quadrant of radiated noise.\nPredicted noise levels were significantly different from measured values\nin the forward quadrant because fan noise was predicted high.\nSince the major noise sources were fan and core, corrections are\nsupplied to adjust ANOPP fan and core noise components so that the total\npredicted noise matches the measured L-1011 noise levels.\n\n17.
