The logic mechanization is\naccomplished by germanium transistor flip-flops and silicon diode gates\ntogether with germanium transistor logic drivers and inverters.\nIn\nthis phase of the project, the CALCOMP designers drew on their previous experience in the Minuteman project.\nThis mechanization is typical of much of the circuitry in both the command clock and the ground\nstation.\nThe input and output interfaces of the command clock are handled primarily with germanium and silicon transistor switching amplifiers.\nWherever possible, the -24.5 vdc spacecraft regulated-bus voltage is\nused directly; however, to conserve power, lower dc voltages are provided by the chopper power supply.\nThe command clock has two major functions: timing and command.\nThe timing section provides the precision frequencies and the spacecraft clock. The precision frequencies are mutually coherent squarewave outputs which are free-running,\nand in no way connected with the\nground control.\nIn the timing section, shown in Figure 3, signal frequencies above i00\ncps are generated by flip-flop counting chains which count down from\nthe precision oscillator.\nThe precision oscillator is a conventional\nquartz-crystal\ntype with proportional oven control.\nLong-term oscillator stability of better than one part per million is expected.\nSignal frequencies below i00 cps are generated by using the T-loop\n(Time Loop) delay line to count pulses and report overflows.\nThe Tloop with its associated logic circuitry also provides the command-input\nbuffer register, plus time storage and time-code generation.\nThe time\nused to initiate commands is stored in a portion of the T-loop, and is\nconstantly updated with an accuracy determined by the precision of the\noscillator.\nThe command section reciprocates by supplying the timing section with\nthe capability to reset the time to within 0.5 second of ground time upon\norders from the ground station. the inter-\n\nBecause of the expected high count of components in the command\nclock, a special in-house component-reliability\nprogram was initiated\nby CALCOMP at the beginning of the development to select component\ntypes; specify production lots; age components; measure parameters,\nand process parameter data. Silicon components were aged at 100\xc2\xb0C\nand germanium components at 55\xc2\xb0C for periods up to 2000 }_ours. Parameters were measured by electronic test equipment built specifically\nfor this purpose, shown in Figure 5. The test equipment automatically\nsequences from component to component, measures up to eight parameters, and simultaneously prints out the measurements on IBM punched\ncards. The resulting data is then processed by digital computers which\nsupply data on parameter shifts as well as parameter distributions.\nThe command clock incorporates an unusual and very effective thermal\ndesign, shown in Figure 6. Semisolid copper planes are permitted to\nremain on the etched circuit boards after the etching operation; these\nplanes, in close proximity to component leads, serve as effective heat\nsinks to all components. The copper planes are terminated at the top\nof each etched board to aluminum straps which, in turn, are terminated\ndirectly to the casting. Using this design, the temperature difference\nbetween the hottest transistor junction and the coldest mounting bracket\nof the casting is less than 13\xc2\xb0C.\n\'fhe command-clock flip-flop,\nFigure 7, is a good example of the circuitry developed specifically for this job. The flip-flop uses six microenergy germanium transistors and dissipates 15 milliwatts.\nThe input\nbuffers save additional gate power, and the output buffers eliminate\nfalse triggering due to noise.
