The field was selected to be in the ecliptic plane, near the\nupper limit of the ISO solar elongation constraint (i.e., near 106\xc2\xb0), and located west of\nthe Sun (to facilitate ground-based follow-up). In addition, the field was chosen to lie far\nfrom the Galactic plane and to contain no known IRAS sources or bright stars. The\nsensor used was ISO\'s AOT CAM01 with a 6-arcsecond PFOV and using the ISOCAM\nLWl0 filter (IRAS Band 1, i.e., 12 I_m).\nAsteroids move and their flux may vary appreciably on time scales as short as\nminutes. Consequently, the exposure time was chosen to freeze asteroid motion on\neach sub-map, where a sub-map is a three-by-three arcminute area (the size of the\nISOCAM array) in which each point was observed three times. Each sub-map consisted\nof a 30 second exposure sequence2 at a fixed position followed by a step of one\narcminute in ecliptic longitude or latitude where the exposure sequence was repeated\nand ending with another one arcminute step in ecliptic longitude or latitude where the\nexposure sequence was again repeated. The total time spent doing each point in a submap was 90 seconds. Because the apparent rate of motion for main belt asteroids\n(MBAs), under the observing geometry described above, is between 0 and 60\narcseconds per hour, the maximum angular distance moved during the time required to\nobtain a sub-map is less than 1.5-arcsecond. However, each sub-map was sampled\nthree times to create the complete map, and the times between successive sub-maps\nvaried from 30 sec to 870 sec. Thus, the maximum distance a main belt asteroid would\nmove between samples of a given point in the map is 14.5-arcseconds.\nFigure 1 is a schematic diagram of the map coverage. Each box is one arcminute on\na side with North up and East to the right. The raster began with the 3\'x3\' array located\nin the NE corner of the map, as indicated by the heavy lines around the nine cells in the\nupper right of the figure. One exposure sequence was made at this position and then\nthe array was moved one arcminute (cell) west. Seventeen exposure sequences were\nmade along a line of constant ecliptic latitude. This brought the array to the end of the\nfirst row. At this point, it stepped South one arcminute, made an exposure sequence at\nthis position, and then made 16 one arcminute steps to the East to complete the\nsecond row. This process was then repeated until the center of the array had scanned\n17 rows.\nSee the movie for an animated version of this figure speeded up by a factor of about\n60. As can be seen from the movie, or the numbers in the figure, each cell around the\nouter one arcminute of the map received no more than three exposure sequences and\nthose in the one-arcminute border interior to this region no more than 6.
