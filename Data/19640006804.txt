at nearly ten miles range from ground zero. The\ndifference in the spectra may be explained by (i) distance - the usual\ninverse square law attenuation plus the excess attenuation due to absorption and other atmospheric effects_ (2) atmospheric fluctuation - the\nresult of local inhomogeneities in assumedwind layers in the atmosphere_\n(3) Doppler shift - the shift in the apparent frequency of the received\nsound due to the relative motion of the source with respect to the receiverj\n(4) source directivity\n- the result of lobes of stronger 3 more intense\nsound which emanate from the engine between 50 and 70 degrees from the\nexhaust stream. Since this lobe would pass over the receiving points at\ndifferent timesj depending upon the range of the points from the launch3\nthe directivity\nwould have the effect of changing the shape of the timehistory plot.\nThe octave band time-histories\n(Figs. 4 through i0) generally show\nthe broad peak of energy to exist below I00 cycles per second as was\nfound to occur during the Saturn launch. The maximum\nenergy occurred in\nthe 50 cycle per second octave bands both at one and ten miles range and\nappeared to result during the lobes passage over the monitoring point.\nSince this energy peak is lower in frequency than would be expected from a\ntheoretical consideration of the vehicle size and thrust and since the 50\ncycle peak did not show up during the on-pad portion of the firing it may\nbe presumedthat it was a characteristic\nof the lobe.\nThe peak overall sound pressure levels shown in Table I occurred at\ndifferent times and are largely the results of the major acoustical lobe\npassing over the measuring point.
