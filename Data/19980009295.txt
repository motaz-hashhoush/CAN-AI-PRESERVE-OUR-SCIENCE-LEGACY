Physiol. 73(4):\n1504-1509, 1992.--The\nrat whole body suspension technique\nmimics responses\nseen during exposure to microgravity\nand\nwas evaluated as a model for cardiovascular\nresponses with two\nseries of experiments.\nIn one series, changes were monitored in\nchronically\ncatheterized\nrats during 7 days of head-down tilt\n(HDT) or non-head-down\ntilt (N-HDT) and after several hours\nof recovery. Elevations\nof mean arterial (MAP), systolic, and\ndiastolic pressures of _20% (P < 0.05) in HDT rats began as\nearly as day I and were maintained\nfor the duration of suspension. Pulse pressures were relatively unaffected, but heart rates\nwere elevated _ 10%. During postsuspension\n(2-7 h), most cardiovascular\nparameters\nreturned\nto presuspension\nlevels. NHDT rats exhibited elevations chiefly on days 3 and 7. In the\nsecond series, blood pressure was monitored\nin 1- and 3-day\nHDT and N-HDT rats to evaluate responses to rapid head-up\ntilt. MAP, systolic and diastolic pressures,\nand HR were elevated (P < 0.05) in HDT and N-HDT rats during head-up tilt\nafter 1 day of suspension, while pulse pressures remained unchanged. HDT rats exhibited elevated pretilt MAP and failed\nto respond to rapid head-up tilt with further increase of MAP\non day 3, indicating some degree of deconditioning.\nThe whole\nbody suspended rat may be useful as a model to better understand responses of rats exposed to microgravity.\nmean arterial blood pressure; systolic blood pressure; diastolic\nblood pressure; pulse pressure; heart rate; weightlessness;\nmicrogravity\n\nEXPOSURE of humans\nto microgravity\nis known to result\nin cardiovascular\nchanges.\nThese include a transient\ninitial increase\nin cardiac volume\nand cardiac output\nthat\nrecovers\nwithin several\ndays (1).
