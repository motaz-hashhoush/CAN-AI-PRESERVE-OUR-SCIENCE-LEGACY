Specifically, criteria are developed for sizing the output and\ninput capacitors, the charging inductance, and the starting and drive,\ncircuit components. Also included are specific design examples and a\ncomparison of predicted to measured performance.\n\nPao i\n\nII\nINTRODUCTION\n\nA. BACKGROUND\nThe particular Boost Regulator (BR) design studied under this\ncontract, was conceived as operational within the general constraints\nof a "Mariner-type" power subsystem. The constraints of a Mariner\nsystem are:\n(1) A wide input voltage rang, from 25VDC to 50VDC.\n(2) High efficiency over a wide load range, e.g. low standby losses.\n(3) Non-Synchronous operation.\n(4) Low parts-count, less than the present (Mariner \'69) design\nwhich uses 80 parts.\n(5) Low weight.\nAn improved version of a Boost. Regulator (IBR) design was\nachieved within the general constraints cited above. The basic\nIBR design was developed for the Capsule System Advanced Development\n(CSAD) program, by Wilorco, under sub-contract to the Jet Propulsion\nLaboratory (1). INtl RATION\nIt is also of some interest to determine if a configuration\nin which the IBR drives an Inverter can be regulated from the\nInverter\'s output terminals. This question has to do with the\nstability of the resultant closed-loop. If this configuration\nproves feasible it will provide the means to maintain the 50VAC,\n2.4KRZ, Inverter output to much tighter regulation limits than are\npresently achievable.\nTwo kinds of protective schemes are required by the Power\nSubsystem:\n1. Protection of the raw (solar array\nand battery) bus from short-circuits.\n2. Protection of the inverter output\nfrom a short-circuit of one particular\nload.\nProtection of the raw DC bus may be accomplished by insertion\nof a transistor switch between the raw bus and the INR input. This\nswitch would remain saturated for all operating currents, but would\n\nPage 73\n\nenter a current-limiting mode in the event of an overload or shortcircuit at its output terminals. We envision a current limiting\nscheme in which a DC current transformer is biased to a preset\nmaximum value. This saturable transistor, in conjunction with\nothers, can also be used to accomplish those switching functions\npresently necessitating the Kinetics switch. This latter fact\nmay be particularly useful in missions having extremely long life,\nsuch as the "Grand Tour".\nMagnetic protection of individual spacecraft loads is also\npractical at the 2.4KHZ operating frequency of the inverter. A\nreactor in series with a given load is biased to a preset maximum\n(gate) current level; as the load tries to draw more current, the\nreactor starts to absorb (input) voltage thus creating a currentlimited input. The obvious advantage of this scheme is that reset\nis automatic.\nIt seems to us mandatory that these areas be investigated\nin a thorough manner. One result of the investigation may be the\nincrease of the spacecraft\'s power subsystem frequency from 1.4KHZ\nto 4.8KHZ.\nIf this frequency increase is adopted, a second result may be\nthe replacement of the inverter\'s switching relays by all solidstate switching. This is because it becomes practical to use more\nmagnetic components at the higher frequencies.\nC. THEORY\nA direct analog of the IBR is a \'bucking" regulator configuration.\nIt is possible to build a Buck Regulator using the same feedback.\n\nPage 74\n\nmechanism used on the IBR.\nA program to investigate the efficiency, and to write the\noperating equations for the Buck Regulator is a natural outgrowth\nof the IBR investigation. Buck Rsgulators may be particularly\nuseful where solar-cell panels reach very low (steady-state)\ntemperatures and where maximum power is required at the low\ntemperature (i.e. at high bus voltages).\nA program should also be instituted to investigate the buckboost configuration. This is the configuration in which the output\nis taken from an isolated secondary winding and thus allows the\noutput voltage to be, in effect, unrelated to the input voltage\nrange.\n\nD.
