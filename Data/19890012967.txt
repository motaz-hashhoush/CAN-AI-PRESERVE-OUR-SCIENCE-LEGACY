b'NASA GROUND COMMUNICATIONS\n\nJohn Roeder\nNASA Headquarters\nABSTRACT\n\nAs part of the Communications Requirements and Constraints, NASA\'s two\nmajor Ground Data Networks were briefly described.\nThe NASA Communication Network, called NASCOM, is the worldwide\noperational telecommunications system which interconnects as the\ntracking and telemetry acquisition sites, launch areas, mission and\nproject control centers, data capture facilities, and network control\ncenters in support of space flight. Currently, the NASCOM network\ncontains over 2 1/2 million circuit miles using satellite,\nterrestrial, and submarine cable leased links; more than 630 circuits\nconnect 139 domestic and foreign sites. The network is engineered\nand controlled at the Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC) with major\nswitching centers in Australia, Spain, and at the Jet Propulsion\nLaboratory (JPL) in California.\nAll kinds of communications traffic is supported, from low rate\ndigital data and voice to narrow and wide band analog and digital at\nrated up to tens of megabits. NASCOM is transitioning to an all\ndigital network with wideband links which utilizes improved technology\nin the competitive market place.\nFor the Space Station era, NASCOM plans are set for higher data rate\nservice (up to 300 Mbps) utilizing data packet switched technology\n(CCSDS standards). Increased use of fiber optics is expected in a\nmuch more diverse network topology.\nThe second major ground network, the Program Support Communications\nNetwork (PSCN), interconnects all NASA Centers and NASA contractor\n\nlocations for intercenter non-operation communications. The primary\nfunctions are to transport voice, video, data and facsimile\ninformation for intercenter coordination, and to provide user access\nto space science and applications data bases. Currently, the PSCN\ncontains almost 400 thousand circuit miles using satellite and\nterrestrial links, supporting many teleconference rooms and high and\nlow speed FAX stations and a packet switched at 35 locations. This\nnetwork is engineered and managed at Marshall Space Flight Center\n(MSFC).\nIndividual data rates covered are from 110 bps to 56 Kbps, 388\ndifferent types of computers and terminals are accommodated, and more\nthan 3000 mail boxes are provided for NASA\'s TELEMAIL network. A\ncomputer networking subsystem allows resource sharing among the four\naeronautics and space technology centers scientific mainframe\ncomputational centers.\nFor the Space Station era, PSCN plans address the significant increase\nin forecast requirements for science data distribution and access to\nthe Numerical Aerodynamics Simulator, and increased use of the Video\nTeleconference System.\nFor communications in general, a recent NASA life cycle cost analysis\npredicts total data volume for NASA science missions to increase as\nmuch as two orders of magnitude, by the year 2000.
