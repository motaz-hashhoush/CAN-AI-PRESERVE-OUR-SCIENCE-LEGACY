Harper\n\nSUMMARY\nA study and evaluation of interception attacks made by an experienced\npilot flying a Grumman F9F-3 airplane on a nonmaneuvering target have been\nmade. The interception runs were made under visual conditions at subsonic\nspeeds and at an altitude of 30,000 feet. The attacks were of the leadpursuit type and the interceptor pilot utilized a computing type of gunsight. The method used provides a good means of studying interceptor\ncontrol characteristics and their relationship to tactical situations.\nThe general control procedure employed by the interceptor pilot\nduring the runs has been determined as a sequence of five control phases.\nThese phases were: (1) positioning of interceptor, (2) initial turn into\ntarget, (3) transition into lead-pursuit tracking, (4) lead-pursuit\ntracking) and (5) breakaway. This sequence of maneuvers is apparently\na logical one that could be adapted to efficient automatic interceptor\ncontrol by a system capable of programing maneuvers.\nSeveral other factors which may be important in automatic control\nof an interceptor were in evidence during the tests. In cases where\nlead-pursuit navigation is desired, it may be necessary to incorporate\nin the autopilot tie-in a means for anticipating the turning rate (bank\nangle) required for smooth transition into tracking. Avoidance of buffet\nregions is important to the success of interception runs. This avoidance\nof buffeting is more than a problem of limiting the acceleration in that\nthe control system should be designed so as to limit the type of attacks\nto those for which continuous tracking is possible without the necessity\nfor undesirably high normal accelerations. Another point which may have\nautomatic-control implications is that the interceptor pilot in the\ntracking phase of the runs generally used coordinated maneuvers and\nlimited sideslip to low values. Many of the foregoing limitations could\nbe avoided in cases where lead-collision navigation is possible. This\ntype of attack is feasible only with armament which can be fired in\nsalvo.\n\n2\n\nNACA RM L53EOl\n\nA tactical evaluation of the interception runs has indicated that\nthe starting position of the attac~ part of the run is a very important\nfactor in determining the effectiveness of an attack . The simplest type\nof interceptor attack appeared to be that initiated from an overtaking\nencounter in which the flight paths of the two airplanes were parallel\nbut laterally separated. Successful attacks were made from frontal and\nperpendicular encounters but only on runs in which the starting position\nwas sufficiently separated from the target\'s flight path to allow the\ninterceptor pilot to complete his sequence of maneuvers without needing\nto exceed the turning and rolling limitations of the interceptor.\nThe perpendicular encounters were in general the most demanding with\nregard to control rates, rates of roll, rates of change of interceptor\nline of sight to the target, and speed losses . I n general, the maximum\naileron control rates occurred in the initial turn phase of the attack,\nthe maximum elevator control rates occurred in the transition into the\ntracking phase, and the maximum rudder control rates occurred in the\ntracking phase.\nTracking-error characteristics are discussed and values of computed\nstandard deviation of tracking error are presented for various combinations of atmospheric turbulence and interceptor maneuvering acceleration.\nThese standard deviations indicate a magnitude of the yaw and pitch components of about 2 mils in smooth air and slight acceleration . Either\nmoderate turbulence or moderate maneuvering normal acceleration increased\nthe standard-deviation values by a factor of about 3, and maneuvering\nnormal accelerations near the maximum attainable increased them by a\nfactor of 4.\nINTRODUCTION\nThe development of techniques for all-weather radar-guided bombing\ntogether with tremendously effective bombs has made apparent the need for\na commensurate defense. One form of defense is the aircraft interceptor,\nand much research is being directed toward the development of such a\nweapon . In order to be effective, the interceptor must be capable of\nall -weather operation, of intercepting an aircraft target in a minimum\nof time, and of efficient use of airborne ordnance with high kill probability. For such highly demanding performance specifications, it appears\ndesirable to make the controlling element in the interceptor completely\nautomati c.\nApparatus necessary for accomplishing automatically controlled\ninterception is being developed, and the point has been reached where\nit is necessary to know what characteristics should be incorportated to\nprovide successful operation. One approach to the problem of obtaining\n\n-\n\n3\n\nNACA RM L53EOl\n\nthis information is based upon the belief that a study and evaluation of\ninterception runs made by experienced pilots under visual conditions at\nsubsonic speeds may provide a basis for determination of the characteristics of efficient interception control. Although the controlling\noperations of a human pil~t executing an interception run under visual\nflight conditions are not wholly comparable to an automatically controlled\ninterception, it is believed that the characteristics that make one system\nefficient may have similarity with those that make the other system efficient. For this reason, it was decided to conduct interception runs in\nwhich a series of relative orientations of an interceptor and a target\nairplane would be covered. The interceptor was provided with suitable\ninstrumentation so that the controlling operations used could be studied,\nand ground radar was also provided to track the two airplanes so that\nthe effect of the tactical situation could be assessed.\nSince the data obtained in these tests were considered to be of\nvalue in their basic form, the presentation is in the form of time histories of the instrument recordings in the interceptor airplane together\nwith time - correlated plots of the ground paths of the two airplanes.\nAPPARATUS\n\nInterceptor airplane.- The airplane used as the interceptor during\nthe flight test program was a Grumman F9F-3, Bureau No. 122560, a Navy\njet fighter. A flight limitation that should be taken into account is the\nroll rate and roll acceleration capabilities of the interceptor. It is advantageous from this standpoint\nto be able to utilize the maximum turning capability of the interceptor;\nhowever, doing this may result in severe airplane buffeting .
