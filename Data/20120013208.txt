Dish Stirling solar systems and nuclear reactor Stirling systems are two promising applications of Stirling engine technology. Sources of thermal energy used to drive the Stirling engine typically\nhave non-uniform temperatures and heat fluxes. Liquid metal heat pipe receivers are often used as heat\ntransformers to uniformly deliver thermal energy at high temperatures to the heater heads of these Stirling\nengines. The use of heat pipe receivers can greatly enhance system efficiency and potential life span.\nOne issue raised during the design phase of heat pipe receivers is the potential solubility corrosion of\nthe heater head section by the liquid metal working fluid. Stainless steels and nickel-based super-alloys\nare standard materials of construction for high temperature heat pipes and heater heads operating in the\ntemperature range 823 to 1073 K. At these operating temperatures, some components of these materials\nare appreciably soluble in working fluids such as sodium, potassium, and NaK. Over a typical life span of\n7 to 10 yr, essentially pure working fluid condensate will condense on the heater head surfaces. The condensate will leach the soluble components of the heater head material and transport them to the evaporator\nsection of the heat pipe. When the working fluid is evaporated again, the soluble materials are precipitated\nand essentially pure working fluid is returned to the condenser section to leach more material. The condensation heat flux for a Stirling heater head is typically 20 to 25 W/cm2. This corresponds to approximately\n760,000 L of sodium per year, condensing on a heater head for a 33% efficient 25 kWe Stirling engine.\nThermacore was awarded Phase I and Phase II SBIR programs to investigate solubility corrosion and\nto develop coatings that would essentially eliminate the solubility corrosion potential. A complete\ndescription of the work performed and the conclusions reached can be found in the Final Report for\nContract Number NAS3\xe2\x80\x9326925. The final task of the program was to fabricate a 1/10th segment of the\ncurrent Stirling Space Power Converter (SSPC) utilizing the coatings and coating processes developed\nduring the program. This heat pipe would then be life tested for up to 10 yr by Thermacore as Phase III.\nUnfortunately, the heat pipe intended for life testing had several weld failures after charging and\nprocessing. Because this pipe was no longer available, Thermacore and NASA acquired the original\n1/10th segment heat pipe from Mechanical Technology Incorporated (MTI) for the Phase III effort. The\n1/10th segment heat pipe, fabricated by Thermacore for the SSPC project under MTI Subcontract No.\n003\xe2\x80\x9305034, was returned to Thermacore for testing on this program.\n\n2.0\n\nHeat Pipe Description\n\nThe SSPC starfish heater consists of fifty radial fins with 38 one-millimeter diameter gas passages in\neach fin. The helium working fluid in the converter flows through the gas passages while the sodium in\nthe heat pipe condenses on the outside of each fin. The annular heat pipe is attached to the outer radius of\nthe starfish heater. The 1/10th segment is a 36\xc2\xb0 slice of the overall heater and heat pipe. The 38 small\ndiameter helium gas passages are replaced with five larger diameter passages to allow for installation of a\ncalorimeter to remove and measure the heat flow. Several photographs of the 1/10th segment are shown\nin Figure 1. The heat pipe is heated primarily by\nradiation from 12 silicon carbide heating elements. In order to minimize heat loss, the heating elements\nare surrounded by a 9-in.-thick graded insulation package. The insulation package is formed to fit and\nsupport the heat pipe/calorimeter package. The power is controlled with a phase angle power controller in\nconjunction with a PID temperature controller. The Silicon Carbide heating elements operate in series at a\nrelatively low voltage, approximately 30 V. A 10 kVA step down transformer is used to reduce the\nprimary 208 to 35V.\nThe desired operating temperature is set on the PID temperature controller. The input signal to the\ntemperature controller is the heat pipe vapor space temperature (Type K thermocouple). The temperature\ncontroller sends a control signal to the phase angle power controller to increase or decrease power to\nmaintain the heat pipe at the set point. The temperature controller also has a latching, over temperature\nalarm feature. In addition, the temperature controller has a second control feature that is being used to\nenergize the hour meter when the temperature is within 5\xc2\xb0 of the set point.\n\nFigure 2.\xe2\x80\x94Photograph of 1/10th segment life test setup.\n\nNASA/CR\xe2\x80\x942012-217430\n\n3\n\nThe power that the heat pipe transfers is extracted and measured using a gas gap calorimeter. The gas\ngap calorimeter consists of small diameter water tubes that are inserted into the heater head gas passage\nholes. The gap between the heater head holes and the water tubes outer diameter is filled with a mixture\nof helium and nitrogen gas. This arrangement results in the calorimeter water tube, a gas gap, and the heat\npipe wall.\nOperation of the gas gap calorimeter is best understood by tracing the flow of thermal energy from\nthe heat pipe wall to the water in the water tubes. The sodium vapor generated in the evaporator section of\nthe heat pipe condenses on the Starfish heater head webs releasing its latent heat of vaporization. The\nthermal energy is transferred through the thickness of the heater head web by conduction. Next, the\nenergy is transferred across the gas-gap by radiation and by conduction through the gas. By controlling\nthe fraction of nitrogen, a low thermal conductivity gas, and the fraction of helium, a high thermal\nconductivity gas, in the gas gap, the resistance to conduction across the gap can be modulated. The heat is\nthen transferred through the water tube wall by conduction and transferred by convection to the water\nflowing through the water tubes. The temperature rise of the water flowing through the tubes and the flow\nrate are used to calculate the heat pipe power throughput.\nIn this setup, the coolant is now distilled water. The coolant is pumped from a tank under the test\nsetup, through a 50 \xce\xbcm filter, into the calorimeter. The coolant exiting the calorimeter then flows through\ntwo liquid-to-air heat exchangers, which are also mounted under the test setup. Therefore, the throughput\npower is ultimately rejected to the room air.\n\n4.0\n\nWork Status\n\nDuring the final period of performance (March 31, 2009 through June 1, 2010), life testing of the\n1/10th segment heat pipe was completed. There were several interruptions in the testing during this\nperiod, which were due to maintenance requirements on the support loop equipment or due to facility\npower failures. The heat pipe has held up well through these situations. A summary of the interruptions is\nprovided below.\nThe first shutdown during the reporting period occurred on March 27, 2009, due to a clogged\ncalorimeter. Scale deposits were found inside the tubes. The calorimeter was rebuilt, the system was\nflushed, the meters were calibrated, and the unit was restarted on June 4, 2009. On August 24, 2009, the\nunit was again shut down to clean a clogged calorimeter. The calorimeter was cleaned, and the unit was\nrestarted on September 15, 2009. The calorimeter again showed signs of scale clogging on October 29,\n2009 and the unit was shut down for maintenance. The tubes were replaced, and the unit was returned to\nservice on November 16, 2009.\nThe unit operated without interruption until March 12, 2010, when a brief potage was required to\nreplace the silicon carbide heaters. The unit was restarted on March 15, 2010. The unit was then operated\nwith no interruptions until June 1, 2010, when it was decided to terminate the life test. The unit had been\noperated at 700 \xc2\xb0C for over 10 yr at that date.\nThe heat pipe appeared to be operating normally at the test termination date. The life test unit has\nsuccessfully operated for 87,783 hr. Work was then completed to remove sodium samples for post-test\nanalysis at to complete metallographic examination of the envelope and wick material. Figure 3 shows the heat pipe at the\nend of life testing.\n\n6.1\n\nPost-Test Analysis of the Working Fluid Chemistry\n\nAt the conclusion of testing, a sodium sample was removed from the heat pipe for analysis. After\nremoval from the support test stand, the two heat pipe fill tubes were torch heated to drive liquid sodium\nout of them. The heat pipe was then placed in an argon-filled glove box, and the fill tubes were cut off. A\nclean stainless steel vessel with a 1/4 in. connecting tube was welded onto one of the fill tubes on the heat\npipe. A tube section with a vacuum valve was welded onto the other fill tube. The heat pipe with these\nvessels attached to the fill ports is shown in Figure 4. The heat pipe was then heated as shown in Figure 5,\nand about 10 g of sodium were pushed into the collection vessel under argon pressure. The collection\nvessel was then placed in an argon glove box, and a solution was prepared by dissolving 1 g in deionized\nwater in a glass Pyrex (Corning, Incorporated, Corning, NY) beaker. The sodium hydroxide solution was\nthen diluted to 500 ml. The resulting solution was acidified to a pH 2.0 using nitric acid, as determined by\npH paper.\nA sample of the sodium was also collected from the source vessel, to compare the initial composition\nwith the composition after ten years of operation. This sample was similarly prepared. Lastly, a sample of\nthe de-ionized water was collected to confirm that no contamination occurred due to impurities in the water.\nThe aqueous sample was placed into a Nalgene bottle and sent to Lehigh Testing Laboratories for\nchemical analysis. The sodium solution was scanned from ~160 to ~ 800 nm with a Spectro CIROS, sideon plasma, inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometer (ICP\xe2\x80\x93AES) equipped with a CCD\ndetector. A background blank of 2,000 mg/L sodium solution from a traceable stock solution was also\nscanned; this sample was also acidified with nitric acid. Element peaks above the background scan were\nnoted and a method was generated for these elements, which were then quantified using the same ICP\xe2\x80\x93\nAES. Calibration curves for each of the elements determined were constructed using traceable standards,\nwhich included a matrix of 2,000 mg/L sodium based upon the sample preparation specifications. A\ncomparison of the sodium peaks (standard, blank and sample) suggests that the value has good integrity.\nThus, a sample concentration of 1g/500 ml or 2,000 mg/L was used to quantify the elemental\nconcentration data. Most of the envelope thickness was not\nsubstantially affected by the test.\nBecause nickel was apparently removed from the inner envelope surface in the evaporator region, an\neffort was made to determine if nickel was transported by the sodium to other regions of the heat pipe. No\nevidence of nickel deposition was observed in any sample; in fact, nickel removal to a 30 \xc2\xb5m depth was\ntypically observed near the inner envelope surface in all of the analyzed samples. This result suggests that\nthe nickel migrated deeper into the envelope, and that diffusion from the inner surface toward the outer\nsurface was the predominant method of transport. The outer surface of the heat pipe envelope was\ncovered with a dark oxide that typically forms from long exposure to air at high temperature.\n\nFigure 17.\xe2\x80\x94Sample Observations of the electron beam welds indicated no evidence of corrosion in the weld\nmetal or heat affected zone. Figures 29 to 32 are images of the electron beam welds observed. Electron\nbeam weld penetrations were measured to be between 3.5 and 5.4 mm. Electron microprobe images of the\nscreen wick material are shown in Figures 33 to 36. Microprobe analysis indicated deposition of sodium,\noxygen, and carbon on the surface of the screen wires (Figs. 34 and 36). These elements were most likely\nresidues of sodium hydroxide and sodium methoxide that remained from the sodium removal and\ndisposal process. Some evidence was seen of species migration in the screen wires; this effect did not\nseem to affect the integrity of the wires. A representative one-tenth segment Stirling Space Power\nConverter heat pipe with an Inconel 718 envelope and a stainless steel screen wick has operated for over\n87,000 hr at nearly 700 \xc2\xb0C. Post-test analysis revealed no significant degradation of the envelope or\nscreen wick material as a result of the life test. The life test provides strong evidence for long-term\nchemical compatibility of sodium heat pipes at high operating temperatures.\nChemical composition was determined for the source sodium and for the fluid charge removed from\nthe tenth segment unit after 87,000 hr of operation. The analysis of the sodium working fluid from the\ntenth segment unit showed small quantities of more than a dozen other elements, some of which were\noriginally present in the source sodium.\nMigration of some alloying elements was observed, though the end effect on the integrity of the\nenvelope was minimal. A 30 \xc2\xb5m region of the inner surface was significantly depleted of nickel; it\nappeared to migrate outward through the envelope, since no region of the envelope inner surface or wick\ncontained re-deposited nickel.\n\nReferences\n1. ABSTRACT\n\nHigh-temperature heat pipes are being evaluated for use in energy conversion applications such as fuel cells, gas turbine re-combustors,\nStirling cycle heat sources; and with the resurgence of space nuclear power both as reactor heat removal elements and as radiator elements.\nLong operating life and reliable performance are critical requirements for these applications. Accordingly, long-term materials compatibility\nis being evaluated through the use of high-temperature life test heat pipes. Thermacore, Inc., has carried out a sodium heat pipe 10-year life\ntest to establish long-term operating reliability. Sodium heat pipes have demonstrated favorable materials compatibility and heat transport\ncharacteristics at high operating temperatures in air over long time periods. A representative one-tenth segment Stirling Space Power\nConverter heat pipe with an Inconel 718 envelope and a stainless steel screen wick has operated for over 87,000 hr (10 yr) at nearly 700 \xc2\xb0C.\nThese life test results have demonstrated the potential for high-temperature heat pipes to serve as reliable energy conversion system\ncomponents for power applications that require long operating lifetime with high reliability. Detailed design specifications, operating\nhistory, and post-test analysis of the heat pipe and sodium working fluid are described.\n15. SUBJECT TERMS\n\nHeat pipes; Sodium; Stirling cycle\n16.
