Liou\nNASA Orbital Debris program Office, Johnson Space Center, Houston, TX 77058, USA\n7 r-chvi. hou-1 knasa.gov\n^e\nKeywords: orbital debris, modeling, population growth, mitigation\nThe anti-satellite test on the Fengun-1 C weather satellite in early 2007 and the collision between Iridium 33\nand Cosmos 2251 in 2009 dramatically altered the landscape of the human-made orbital debris environment in\nthe low Earth orbit (LEO). The two events generated approximately 5500 fragments large enough to be tracked\nby the U.S. Space Surveillance Network. Those fragments account for more than 60% increase to the debris\npopulation in LEO. However, even before the ASAT test, model analyses already indicated that the debris\npopulation (for those larger than 10 cm) in LEO had reached a point where the population would continue to\nincrease, due to collisions among existing objects, even without any future launches. The conclusion implies\nthat as satellites continue to be launched and unexpected breakup events continue to occur, coimnonly-adopted\nmitigation measures will not be able to stop the collision-driven population growth. To remediate the debris\nenvironment in LEO, active debris removal must be considered.\nThis presentation will provide an updated assessment of the debris environment after the Iridium 33 / Cosmos\n2251 collision, an analysis of several future environment projections based on different scenarios, and a\nprojection of collision activities in LEO in the near future. The need to use active debris removal to stabilize\nfuture debris environment will be demonstrated and the effectiveness of various active debris removal\nstrategies will be quantified.\n\nNational Aeronautics and Space Administration\n\nAn Assessment of the Current\nLEO Debris Environment and\nthe Need for Active Debris Removal\nJ.-C.
