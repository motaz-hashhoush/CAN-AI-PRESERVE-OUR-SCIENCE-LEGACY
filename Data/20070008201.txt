Sullivan\xe2\x80\xa0\n\nResearch Engineer\n\nResearch Engineer\n\nClosed cell foams are often used for thermal insulation. In the case of the Space Shuttle,\nthe External Tank uses several thermal protection systems to maintain the temperature of the\ncryogenic fuels. A few of these systems are polyurethane, closed cell foams. In an attempt\nto better understand the foam behavior on the tank, we are in the process of developing\nand improving thermal-mechanical models for the foams. These models will start at the\nmicrostructural level and progress to the overall structural behavior of the foams on the\ntank.\nOne of the key properties for model characterization and veri\xef\xac\x81cation is thermal expansion.\nSince the foam is not a material, but a structure, the modeling of the expansion is complex.\nIt is also exacerbated by the anisoptropy of the material. During the spraying and foaming\nprocess, the cells become elongated in the rise direction and this imparts di\xef\xac\x80erent properties\nin the rise direction than in the transverse directions. Our approach is to treat the foam as\na two part structure consisting of the polymeric cell structure and the gas inside the cells.\nThe polymeric skeleton has a thermal expansion of its own which is derived from the basic\npolymer chemistry. However, a major contributor to the thermal expansion is the volume\nchange associated with the gas inside of the closed cells. As this gas expands it exerts\npressure on the cell walls and changes the shape and size of the cells. The amount that this\noccurs depends on the elastic and viscoplastic properties of the polymer skeleton. The more\ncompliant the polymeric skelton, the more in\xef\xac\x82uence the gas pressure has on the expansion.\nAn additional in\xef\xac\x82uence on the expansion process is that the polymeric skeleton begins to\nbreakdown at elevated temperatures and releases additional gas species into the cell interiors,\nadding to the gas pressure. The fact that this is such a complex process makes thermal\nexpansion ideal for testing the models.\nThis report focuses on the thermal expansion tests and the response of the microstructure.\nA novel optical method is described which is appropriate for measuring thermal expansion\nat high temperatures without in\xef\xac\x82uencing the thermal expansion measurement. Detailed\nmicrostructural investigations will also be described which show cell expansion as a function\nof temperature.
