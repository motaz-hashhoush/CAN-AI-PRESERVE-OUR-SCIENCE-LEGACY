Ward\nLouisiana State University\nBaton Rouge, Louisiana\nABSTRACT\nAircraft gas turbine engine components are subjected to severe stress,\ntemperature, and environmental conditions.\n\nIn these components, local\n\nstress raisers (e.g., notches, boltholes, welds, fillet radii) are very often\nlife limiting areas in that low cycle fatigue failures generally initiate\nin these critical regions.\n\nEconomic and reliability demands have prompted\n\ninordinate effort in development of analytic methods to first predict stresses\nand strains in these complex geometry regions and, ultimately, predict the low cycle\nfatigue life for components containing these necessary design features.\n\nThese\n\nanalytical developments have apparently been successful since numerous techniques\n(most notably in the form of finite-element computer models) have evolved.\n\nThere\n\nremains, however, the need to check or verify these analytical methodologies\nagainst actual experimental data measurements \xe2\x80\xa2. This is not a simple task.\n\nMost\n\nstress concentration regions in gas turbine engines are geometrically very\nsmall, eliminating many conventional extensometry methods for strain measurement.\nFurther, conditions of interest include long times at elevated temperatures (near\n649 0 C), eliminating conventional strain gage measurement methods.\n\nThe laser\n\nInterferometric Strain Displacement Gage was recognized as having the potential\nto accomplish this demanding task and was employed in this program.\nThe overall objective of this program was the generation, measurement,\nand documentation of the actual strains incurred at the root of a discontinuity in cyclically loaded test samples subjected to inelastic deformation at\nhigh temperature where creep deformations readily occur.\n\nA secondary objective\n\nwas to perform an analysis of the steady-state cyclic stress-strain response\nat the root of the discontinuity in the tested samples for comparison to the\nmeasured results.\nA comprehensive set of local notch root strain measurements for a variety\nof load patterns in an Inconel 718 notch specimen at 649 0 C (1200\xc2\xb0 F) was\nobtained and documented using the laser Interferometric Strain Displacement\n\n25\n\nGage (ISDG).\n\nThe ISDG was successfully adapted to the high-temperature measure-\n\nments in this typical Ni-base superalloy and was shown to have a relative\nuncertainty of\n\n\xc2\xb1 3%\n\nof the measured strain with an additional uncertainty of\n\n+ 150 microstrain.\nMeasurements were made for six load patterns including continuous cyclic,\ncreep and cyclic with tensile and comprehensive hold periods on flat, doublenotch bars with an elastic stress concentration factor of 1.9.\nPedigree tensile and cyclic stress-strain data were also generated at\n649\n\n0\n\nC and employed in a simple Neuber analysis to obtain analytic predictions\n\nfor comparisons to test results.\n\nA modified Neuber approach and a limited\n\nfinite-element study were also compared to the data.\nA smooth bar specimen subjected to the notch root strain history recorded\nfrom a continuously cycled notch bar was also used to obtain stress behavior\ndata for comparison to the predictions.\nThe Neuber analysis predicted the first cycle notch root behavior very\nwell on the basis of hysteresis loop comparison when the notch root\nstress-strain response curve was used.\n\nstrai~\n\nrate\n\nThe stabilized cyclic loops were not\n\nwell predicted even when cyclic softening had stabilized.\n\nThe modified Neuber\n\nequation (corrected for stress redistribution due to plasticity) and the\nfinite-element analysis improved the cyclic correlation but did not totally\nresolve the problem.\nThe smooth bar test using the notch root strain history control shed\nadditional light on this in that the maximum monotonic and stable cyclic stresses\nwere \'well predicted by Neuber (as was the minimum stable cyclic stress) while\nthe minimum monotonic\' (first cycle unload) was not.\n\nThis suggests that\n\nkinematic-hardening assumptions may be incorrect for the early cyclic transition period.\nThe utility of the computerized data acquisition and storage system\nassociated with the ISDG was demonstrated by examining cyclic history dependent parameters (e.g., loop area} on a cycle-by-cycle basis.\n\nThese parameters\n\nwere used to assess data quality as well as behavioral trends, and have\npotential for extension to life prediction application.\n\nThe hysteresis loop\n\nelastic loading slope was used to predict notch root crack initiation which was\nconfirmed by posttest microscopy.\nThe program objectives of\n\ngene~ation\n\nof benchmark notch data in a turbine\n\ndisk alloy at elevated temperature and comparison to a Neuber analysis were\nmet.\n\nSignificant .implications for future model development were
