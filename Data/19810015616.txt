V-B, the second of a new series of nine international telecommunications satellites owned and operated by the\n105-nation International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat), is scheduled to be launched by the NASA Kennedy\nSpace Center on board an Atlas Centaur launch vehicle no earlier\nthan May 21, 1981, from Cape Canaveral, Fla.\n\nThe first Intelsat\n\nV was successfully launched by NASA last December.\nLike its predecessor, Intelsat V-B weighs 1,928 kilograms\n(4,251 pounds) at launch and has almost double the communications\ncapability of early satellites in the Intelsat series \xe2\x80\x94\nvoice circuits and two color television channels.\n\n12,000\n\nIt will be\n\npositioned in geosynchronous orbit over the Atlantic Ocean as the\nprime Intelsat satellite to provide communications services between the Americas, Europe, the Middle East and Africa.\n\nMay 15, 1981\n\n-more-\n\n-2-\n\nIntelsat V satellites are built by the Ford Aerospace and\nCommunications Corp., Palo Alto, Calif., using system components\ndeveloped by firms in the United Kingdom, France, the Federal\nRepublic of Germany, Italy and Japan.\nThe International Telecommunications Satellite Organization\nis headquartered in Washington, B.C.\n\nNASA is reimbursed for all\n\ncosts of the Atlas Centaur and launch services under the provisions of a launch services agreement signed in May last year.\nThe Atlas Centaur (AC-56) will place the Intelsat V-B into\na highly elliptical transfer orbit ranging from 166.6\nkilometers (103 to 22,347 miles).\n\nto 35,964\n\nIt is from this orbit at apo-\n\ngee that a solid propellant rocket motor attached to the satellite will be fired to circularize the orbit at geosynchronous\naltitude over the equator.\n\nAt that altitude, because the speed\n\nof the satellite in orbit matches the rotational speed of the\nEarth, the satellite remains in position over one spot.\nNASA\'s Lewis Research Center, Cleveland,
