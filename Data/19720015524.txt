Armature winding insulation life of over 80, 000 hours\nfor an average winding temperature of 248\xc2\xb0C was predicted using the developed procedure.\n\n17. and field winding electrical insulations of two 60 KW, 120/208\nVolts, 400-Hz, 1 2 , 0 0 0 RPM homopolar inductor alternators. The alternators\ncompleted endurance tests in SNAP-8 turbo-alternators accumulating 12,440\narid 23, 130 hours at rated load.\nElectrical measurements were made on the alternators to establish quality\nas received and under simulated SNAP-8 environmental p r e s s u r e and altitude.\nInitial insulation studies were also conducted under high humidity conditions\nfollowing thermal stabilization. The insulation condition of these alternators\nas received was considered excellent and suggested a capability for substantially longer life than established by the turbo-alternator endurance tests.\nOne of the two endurance tested alternator stators was subjected to an\nincreasing step-temperature aging test, starting at 200\xc2\xb0C with increments\nof 25\xc2\xb0C for 48-hour periods to 400\xc2\xb0C. The armature coils of this stator\nwere isolated to provide approximately 13 groups of 5 armature coils each\nfor insulation measurements. A dielectric breakdown test was applied to\neach group in turn through the step-temperatures with a criterion of end-life\nestablished.at 600 Volts dc. The predicted life for each 48 hour period and\nrespective aging temperature was projected from an aging rate slope determined from industry tests on polyimide insulated wire. Using this procedure,\ntotal projected armature insulation life for the tested stator was determined\nto be over 80, 000 hours for an average winding temperature of 248\xc2\xb0C.\nThe field insulation life was also projected to be equal to the 80, 000 hour\narmature life, using the same end-life criterion, although the field winding\nto frame insulations were susceptible to leakage at high temperature and low\npressure indicating less margin than the armature winding to frame insulations.\nThe armature winding insulations appear to be of excellent quality with\nmaterial and design capabilities substantially in excess of dielectric requirements for a 200\xc2\xb0C SNAP-8 operating condition. The field winding to frame\ninsulations do not appear to be dielectrically as strong, and design and material improvements are therefore recommended for future applications.\n\nSECTION I\nINTRODUCTION\nA.\n\nBACKGROUND\n\nA 60 KW inductor alternator was developed by General Electric Company\nduring the mid 1960\'s for the SNAP-8 Space Electrical Power System\nunder NASA Contract NAS5-417 ( r e f . 1, 2). The alternator is a threephase, 120/208 volts 400 Hz, 1 2000 RPM brushless, solid rotor homopolar\ntype, designed for 10, 000 hours minimum life and high reliability. The\nrating and performance characteristics of this alternator, identified as\nGeneral Electric Model 2CM391B1, are summarized in Table I.\nSeveral 2CM391B1 alternators constructed under Contract NAS5-417 were\nassembled into turbo-alternator electrical systems at Aerojet-General\nCorp. and subjected to endurance tests under NASA Contract NAS3-13458\n( r e f . 3). Thermal\naging test procedures for insulation life prediction have been developed and\nused by industry for extrapolating life of materials and material systems.\nIEEE No. 57 and No. 117 are typical procedures ( r e f . 4, 5). Such procedures are not known to have been applied to the study of the material combinations used in the endurance tested alternator or to determine remaining\ninsulation life of equipment with a previous operational history. The wound components and insulations described throughout this report are depicted and\nreferenced in the cross-sectional view of the alternator. (Figure 1).\nA detailed description of the insulating materials and application to this\nalternator is shown in Tables II and III.\nA. FIELD WINDING\nThe field winding (Figure 1, Item 3) is an epoxy bonded double annular\ncoil wound into a double cavity copper box to provide maximum heat\ntransfer and coil stability. Each coil starts at the bottom of the box with\na well insulated joint at the bottom center of the box so as to minimize\nconductor cross-over and eliminate bulky joints at the coil periphery.\nMaterials used for insulating the field included fabricated sheet insulations\nof two thicknesses (Table III, Item 1) cemented to the box sides and\nbottom with epoxy adhesive. The corners of the box are insulated with\nthree turns of . 026" diameter untreated glass cord saturated with epoxy\ncompound. The field coils (Table III, Item 4) were "wet wound" in place\nusing epoxy cement as the "wet winding" compound.\nThe coil periphery is insulated by placement of two layers of treated glass\ncloth (Table III, Item 7) around the coil. This operation was followed by\nwinding tin-plate copper wire into the insulation and soldered in place\nwith pure tin solder to provide a heat path (Figure 1, Item 9) from the\ncoil to the alternator frame. The width of the coil periphery insulation\nexceeded the width of the field such that sides could be folded to contain\ntin-plated copper wire and solder and maximize field coil insulation\nreliability. The soldered structure is s ized by machining, positioned\nbetween the two armature cores and, after armature coil placement, the\nassembly inserted into the frame (Figure 1, Item 1) providing a line-toline fit between the oil cooled frame and field coil outer diameter.\n\nB.\n\nARMATURE WINDING\n\nThe armature stator is a double core construction (Figure 1, Item 2) with\na square bottom semi-closed slot. The armature winding consists of one\nturn per slot per phase with conductors (Table III, Item 2) inserted into\nthe semi-closed slot from the core end.\nThe armature slots are insulated with a slot liner (Table III, Item 1) that\nis continuous and extends through both armature cores with a butt-fold over\nthe top conductor to close the slot. The slot phase separation is achieved\nby cementing a strip of .016" thick silicone glass laminate to one end of the\nbottom conductor prior to conductor insertion into the slot.\nEnd turn phase insulation is provided by a wrap-around of impregnated\nglass cloth (Table III, Item 7) positioned snug between the involute of the\narmature coil and the slot liner extensions. Coil side phase insulation is\nprovided by sleeving (Table III, Item 6) positioned on formed coils prior\nto insertion. The sleeving provides sufficient separation between coil\nsides and is later saturated with epoxy resin to effect dielectric and\nstructural strength for coil support.\n,\nThe armature slot is closed with a roof-shaped top-stick machined from\npolyimide sheet. The top-sticks were inserted from each end of the cores\nand were positioned between the folded slot liner and overhang of the semiclosed slot core such that the top of the stick is essentially flush with the\nstator bore.\n,\nThe completed armature winding was impregnated with a pre-heated\nsolventless epoxy compound by vacuum-pressure processing. These tech,niques were applied to assure optimum slot fill and saturation of glass\ntapes and sleevings in order to achieve best bond and dielectric strength.\nC.\n\nBETWEEN CORE STRUCTURE\n\nFollowing placement of the armature c,oils, the open area around armature\nconductors between the two stator areas (Figure 1, Item 8) and field winding was filled with a trowelable-inorganic filled epoxy compound. The.\nspace between the folded slot liners in this area and the base was filled\nwith a glass laminate fabricated in place by .wet- lay up of three layers\n. 006" thick leno glass weave cloth and the trowelable epoxy to effect a\nsmooth, strong reinforced hoop at the bore.\n\nD.\n\nCONNECTIONS\n\n-\n\n.?\n\n\':\n\n\'\n\n:\n\n"\n\nSolid connections (joints) were achieved with Tungsten Inert Gas Weld methods\nand oxygen-free high conductivity copper (OFHC). Measurements were specified for field and armature windings\nunder ambient conditions and at operating pressure and temperature. The\ntest objective was to determine if these stators would encounter corona in\n.service operation and to use corona data as a dielectric tool for evaluating\nthe quality of the insulation system. In keeping with the 1000 V dc voltage\nlimit used in the leakage current measurement, a limit of 700 V ac was\nused for the corona work, since the peak value of the 700 V ac sine wave\ncorresponds ( X 1.4) to the 1000 V dc value. It was\nsuspected that outgassing was the result of an oil film in the stator frame\ncooling passages.
